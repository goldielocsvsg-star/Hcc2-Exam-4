Concept Definition & Scope of Anxiety

Anxiety is defined in Giddens as "a subjectively distressful experience activated by the perception of threat, which has both a potential psychological and physiological etiology and expression." This is the exact definition used in your course.

📖 Textbook Elaboration — Why the Definition Matters

The definition acknowledges two critical dimensions: psychological (thoughts, perceptions, emotional responses) and physiological (autonomic nervous system activation, biochemical changes). This is why anxiety manifests with both mental symptoms (worry, dread, fear) and physical symptoms (tachycardia, diaphoresis, tremors). Both must be assessed.

⭐ High-Yield: Why Anxiety Disorders Are Different from Normal Anxiety
  • Anxiety disorders are distinct from normal anxiety — they interfere with the ability to function in daily life
  • Normal anxiety (mild-moderate) is actually beneficial — enhances learning, motivation, and performance
  • Prof's focus for this module: Panic Disorder and PTSD as the two exemplar disorders
  • Medical causes must always be ruled out first — heart disease, hyperthyroidism, pheochromocytoma, substance withdrawal, tumors producing catecholamines all produce anxiety symptoms
Physiologic Underpinning — The Fight-or-Flight Response
📖 Textbook: Cannon's Fight-or-Flight + General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye)

When the sympathetic nervous system is activated in response to a perceived threat, it interacts with the adrenal cortex to release epinephrine. The resulting cascade includes: heart rate increase, pulmonary dilation (↑O₂ capacity), hepatic glucose release, pupil dilation, and GI peristalsis inhibition. Observable signs include tachycardia, tremors, diaphoresis, and xerostomia.

Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (3 Stages):

  • Stage 1 — Alarm: Stressor perceived → SNS activates → mobilizes body resources
  • Stage 2 — Resistance: PNS attenuates reaction → returns many functions toward homeostasis → body remains alert and vigilant
  • Stage 3 — Exhaustion: If stressor is sufficiently intense or prolonged → exceeds compensatory capacity → susceptibility to disease and death

The amygdala-centered neural circuit is directly linked to fear symptoms (panic, phobia). The cortico-striatal-thalamo-cortical circuit is linked to worry symptoms (GAD). This explains why different drugs target different aspects of anxiety.

Slide 3 + Textbook Box 31.2 — Nursing Assessment of Anxiety

Assessment of the Anxious Patient

Your professor emphasizes a structured assessment approach. The textbook (Box 31.2) identifies three core components: history, physical examination, and mental status examination (MSE). Assessment requires establishing therapeutic rapport before asking sensitive questions — rote questioning without rapport is described as "ill-advised and at worst unprofessional."

Slide 3: What to Include in Assessment of Panic History
  • Event or situation causing stress or crisis — identify the precipitating trigger
  • Subjective experience — ask open-ended: "What does this loss mean to you?" Avoid clinical jargon; book advises asking "Do you find yourself worrying a lot and feel unable to control it?" instead of "Do you experience anxiety?"
  • Level of anxiety — identify which of Peplau's four levels the patient is currently experiencing; use scaled questions (1–10)
  • Suicidal ideation — is it present? — bolded by Prof as the PRIORITY item
  • Coping style and strengths — is the patient using adaptive or maladaptive coping mechanisms?
  • Support systems available
⚠️ Priority — Prof Emphasizes This Repeatedly

Suicidal ideation is the PRIORITY assessment item. Patients with anxiety disorders, especially PTSD, are at elevated risk. The textbook case study (Nicole) illustrates a patient who developed a plan to kill herself — her disclosure to the department counselor is what led to intervention. Always assess directly — asking about suicide does not increase risk, it opens the door to intervention.

📖 Textbook — Critical Point: Rule Out Medical Causes First

Autonomic activation in anxiety can manifest as hypertension, tachycardia, tachypnea, diaphoresis, ataxia, tremulousness, GI distress, and muscular tension. These symptoms are not specific to anxiety. The textbook states: "Making a determination of anxiety should never be done until other high-risk medical explanations (e.g., myocardial infarction, embolism, and stroke) have been ruled out."

Slide 4 — Therapeutic Communication with Anxious/Distressed Clients
Prof asks: "What is the nurse's best response when clients are crying, stressed, visibly upset? What responses are therapeutic when clients are in crisis or experiencing anxiety?" ▼
✓ Correct Approach

Focus on feelings, not content. The therapeutic response acknowledges the patient's emotional experience and communicates presence and safety. Examples:

  • "It seems like you're feeling really overwhelmed right now. I'm here with you."
  • "You are safe. I will not leave you."
  • Use a low-pitched voice, speak slowly and calmly — this activates the parasympathetic nervous system and models calm regulation
  • Maintain a calm, non-threatening presence — your demeanor directly influences the patient's nervous system response

What NOT to do (common distractors on exams):

  • ✗ "Don't worry, everything will be fine" — false reassurance; dismisses the patient's experience
  • ✗ Redirecting immediately to education or discharge information
  • ✗ Asking multiple assessment questions at once (overwhelming at panic level)
  • ✗ Leaving the patient alone to "give them space" — staying is therapeutic

Lecture note key: Prof highlights that the nurse's calm presence is a direct intervention. Mindfulness, relaxation, and breathing techniques activate the parasympathetic nervous system, counteracting the SNS fight-or-flight cascade.

Slides 6–10 + Textbook Chapter 31: Panic Disorder

Panic Disorder

A panic disorder occurs when recurrent, unexpected panic attacks cause persistent concern about future attacks or significant behavioral changes. The textbook notes it affects approximately 6 million Americans, occurs twice as often in females, and most often develops in adolescents or adults.

Characteristics of Panic Disorder
  • Episodes of intense anxiety — characterized as intense terror, apprehension, and fear
  • Characterized by recurrent panic attacks
  • Abrupt onset with symptoms peaking within 10 minutes — this is a key diagnostic feature
  • Why do patients visit the ED? Because symptoms mimic acute cardiac events — palpitations, chest pain, SOB, diaphoresis → patient believes they are having a heart attack. Cardiac causes must be ruled out before diagnosing panic disorder.
Diagnostic Criteria: 4 or More Symptoms = Panic Attack
⭐ Know All of These — May Be Tested as SATA
  • Palpitations, pounding heart, or accelerated heart rate
  • Sweating (diaphoresis)
  • Trembling or shaking
  • Sensation of shortness of breath or smothering
  • Feelings of choking
  • Chest pain or discomfort
  • Nausea or abdominal distress
  • Dizziness, unsteadiness, lightheadedness, or faintness
  • Chills or hot flushes
  • Paresthesias (numbness or tingling sensations)
  • Derealization — feelings that the world is unreal
  • Depersonalization — feeling detached from oneself
  • Fear of losing control or "going crazy"
  • Fear of dying
Acute Nursing Care During an Active Panic Attack
⚠️ Acute Care Priority Actions — Slide 9
  • Stay with the client — reassure them that you will not leave; abandonment increases terror
  • Minimize stimuli — move to a quiet private room; reduce lighting; ask unnecessary staff/visitors to leave; lower noise levels
  • Remain calm and quiet — your regulated nervous system helps regulate theirs (co-regulation)
  • Instruct slow, deep breathing — activates parasympathetic nervous system; counters SNS hyperactivation
  • Client is unable to concentrate on learning new information during a panic attack — do NOT attempt health teaching during active panic
  • Use low-pitched, slow voice: "You are safe. I am right here with you. This feeling will pass."
Prof asks: "How do we minimize stimuli?" and "What effect do mindfulness/relaxation/breathing techniques have on the nervous system?" ▼
✓ Full Answer

Minimizing stimuli:

  • Move patient to a private, quiet room
  • Dim or reduce lighting
  • Ask non-essential staff, visitors, other patients to leave the space
  • Speak in a low, calm, slow voice — avoid loud or abrupt sounds
  • Reduce environmental triggers (television, phone alerts, overhead paging if possible)
  • Limit physical contact to what is necessary and always announce touch first

Effect of relaxation/breathing on the nervous system:

These techniques activate the parasympathetic nervous system (PNS) — the "rest and digest" branch. Slow diaphragmatic breathing stimulates the vagus nerve, which signals the heart to slow down, reduces cortisol release, and gradually downregulates the amygdala's fear response. This physiologically counteracts the SNS-driven fight-or-flight cascade that produces all panic symptoms.

Long-Term Nursing Care Goals — Panic Disorder (Slide 10)
  • Help client recognize early symptoms of onset of anxiety and intervene before reaching the panic stage — this is about building self-awareness
  • Teach client adaptive coping skills (breathing techniques, progressive muscle relaxation, grounding exercises)
  • Develop a balanced life and nourish the spirit — holistic approach to wellness
  • Promote adherence to long-term pharmacologic and psychotherapeutic treatment plans
  • Teach client to identify and avoid or manage triggers
  • Refer for cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) — largest evidence base for anxiety disorders

Slides 11–17 + Textbook Chapter 31 (PTSD) + Lecture Notes

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

PTSD follows exposure to an extreme traumatic stressor — either a direct personal physical threat or trauma, or witnessing such an event in another person. The textbook case of Nicole (police officer) illustrates classic PTSD: traumatic occupational exposures leading to intrusive memories, sleep disturbances, substance use for coping, social withdrawal, and ultimately suicidal ideation with a specific plan.

  • Follows exposure to extreme traumatic stressor — personal physical threat/trauma OR witnessing trauma in another
  • Characterized by re-experiencing significant, life-threatening events
  • Increased incidence with previous history of abuse or trauma — prior trauma is a major risk factor; heritability estimated at 30–50% (shared with panic disorder, phobias, OCD)
  • Highly comorbid with substance use disorders — substances used to self-medicate intrusive symptoms
Symptom Clusters of PTSD
ClusterSymptoms
Re-experiencing Hallucinations, flashbacks, illusions; recurrent intrusive recollections of the event; recurring nightmares associated with the traumatic event
Avoidance Avoidance of stimuli associated with the trauma — avoiding people, places, or conversations; inability to show feelings; not wanting to talk about the event(s)
Hyperarousal / Increased Arousal Sleep disturbances (insomnia); irritability and anger; trouble concentrating; exaggerated startle response; hypervigilance
Negative Cognition / Mood Detachment from relationships; interpersonal problems; guilt (Nicole: "I killed them, didn't I?"); hopelessness; shame; emotional numbness
Acute Nursing Care — PTSD (Slide 13)
⚠️ Acute Phase Priorities
  • Help client focus on the present — use a structured interview; grounding techniques (5-4-3-2-1 sensory, deep breathing, physical anchoring)
  • Provide safety and comfort during the crisis period — quietly remain with the client; provide safety for other clients and staff as well
  • Assess for suicide risk — a client may not always say "I'm feeling like killing myself." Listen for indirect expressions
  • Assess for comorbid substance abuse — trauma and substance use are highly comorbid; substance intoxication or withdrawal may be occurring simultaneously
Prof asks: "What might a client say that indicates suicidal ideation — without directly stating 'I'm feeling like killing myself'? What might they say?" ▼
✓ Textbook + Clinical Answer

Clients at risk for suicide often communicate intent indirectly. Statements that require immediate safety assessment include:

  • "I just want to go to sleep and not wake up."
  • "Everyone would be better off without me."
  • "There's no point in going on anymore."
  • "I've given away most of my things recently." (behavioral cue)
  • "I just want the pain to stop."
  • "I don't care what happens to me."
  • "I won't be a problem for much longer."
  • "I killed them, didn't I?" (as in the Nicole textbook case — guilt-driven suicidality)

The nurse must:

  • Ask directly: "Are you thinking about hurting yourself or ending your life?" — research confirms this does NOT increase risk
  • Assess for a specific plan, method, and timeline — specificity increases imminent risk
  • Assess access to means (Nicole had a firearm at home — this is a critical risk factor)
  • Assess presence of protective factors (children, religious beliefs, strong support system)
Long-Term Goals — PTSD (Slides 13–16)
  • Use therapeutic communication to help the patient express feelings of anxiety — validate and acknowledge feelings without judgment
  • Promote client participation in decision-making related to care — restores sense of control lost during trauma
  • Help client identify relaxation techniques to manage pain, anxiety, and tension
  • Instill hope for the future — but do NOT give false reassurance ("Everything will be fine" is non-therapeutic)
  • Assist client in identifying maladaptive defense mechanisms that interfere with recovery (denial, avoidance, substance use)
  • Postpone health teaching until acute anxiety subsides — patient cannot learn during panic-level anxiety
  • Integration of the trauma into the patient's persona — acknowledging and processing rather than avoiding
  • Renewing significant relationships and social connections
  • Establishing meaningful goals for the future
  • Progressing through the grief process
  • Developing a sense of optimism and hope for the future

Nutrition and Lifestyle (Slide 5)
  • No caffeine — stimulant; increases heart rate, nervousness, and can precipitate panic attacks
  • No food coloring — associated with behavioral reactivity in some individuals
  • No monosodium glutamate (MSG) — excitatory neurotransmitter precursor; may exacerbate anxiety symptoms
  • Exercise — releases endorphins; reduces cortisol and adrenaline; improves mood, sleep, and resilience; evidence-based for anxiety reduction
  • Alcohol and substance avoidance — alcohol is a CNS depressant but causes rebound anxiety; substance use maintains and worsens anxiety disorders long-term

Slide 19 + Textbook Chapter 31 Pharmacotherapy + Lecture Notes

Pharmacology — Anxiety & PTSD

🎓 Prof's Framework for Pharmacology

Prof distinguishes between acute/crisis management and long-term/maintenance treatment. Benzodiazepines are quick-acting but for short-term (crisis) use only; long-term use leads to dependence. SSRIs are considered first-line for long-term treatment of both anxiety disorders and PTSD. Buspirone is safe for long-term use with no dependence potential.

Prof's NCLEX-Style Questions on Pharmacology
NCLEX Q: "A patient in acute anxiety needs a PRN anxiolytic. Options: Buspirone, Lorazepam, Amitriptyline, Fluoxetine. Which is most appropriate?" ▼
✓ Answer: Lorazepam (Ativan)

Reasoning:

  • Lorazepam (Ativan) = benzodiazepine = fast-acting anxiolytic = appropriate for acute/PRN use
  • Buspirone — takes 1–4 weeks to work; NOT for acute or PRN use
  • Amitriptyline — tricyclic antidepressant (TCA); no PRN anxiolytic indication; not used for panic disorder first-line
  • Fluoxetine — SSRI; takes 4–6 weeks; NOT for PRN or acute use

Key principle: Benzos = fast/acute/crisis. SSRIs and Buspirone = slow/maintenance/long-term.

NCLEX Q (SATA): "Teaching a patient newly prescribed Lorazepam (Ativan) for panic disorder — which information should the nurse include?" ▼
✓ Select All That Apply — Correct Answers
  • ✅ Caution with driving and use of machinery — CNS depression impairs coordination and reaction time
  • ✅ Avoid alcoholic beverages — both cause CNS depression; combination is dangerous (additive effect)
  • ✅ Restrict caffeine intake — caffeine is a stimulant that can precipitate or worsen panic attacks
  • ✅ For short-term use only — long-term use leads to physical dependence and withdrawal risk

Incorrect options (common distractors):

  • ❌ "Foods allowed on a tyramine-free diet" — tyramine-free diet is for MAOIs, NOT benzos
  • ❌ "Take on an empty stomach" — not a benzo-specific requirement; food does not significantly affect benzo absorption
  • ❌ "Takes 1–4 weeks to take effect" — that is Buspirone; benzos work within 30–60 minutes
  • ❌ "Take at the same time every day" — benzos are often PRN, not scheduled
Prof asks: "What is the difference between 'first-line' and 'first-generation' medications?" ▼
✓ Important Distinction
  • First-line = the treatment that should be tried FIRST based on evidence — best efficacy, best safety profile, most supported by research. Example: SSRIs are first-line for anxiety disorders.
  • First-generation = the earliest developed medications in a class, which may have MORE side effects than newer versions. Example: Typical antipsychotics (haloperidol, chlorpromazine) are first-generation antipsychotics — but they are NOT first-line anymore due to EPS risks.
  • These terms can point in opposite directions — something can be first-generation but NOT first-line (e.g., TCAs for anxiety).

Slide 15 + Therapeutic Communication

Crisis Intervention & Therapeutic Communication

Goals of Crisis Intervention (Slide 15)
  • Psychological resolution of the immediate crisis — deal with the "here and now"; do not attempt to process the entire trauma history in crisis
  • Restoration to the level of functioning before the crisis period — stabilize and return to baseline
  • Once past the crisis — improvement in functioning above the pre-crisis level — growth and resilience-building following recovery
📘 Concept — Therapeutic Communication with Anxious Patients

Therapeutic communication for anxiety is fundamentally about acknowledging the person's felt experience, not correcting or educating them. The nurse's goal during a crisis is to create safety, not to problem-solve or teach. All psychoeducation and behavioral intervention is reserved for after the crisis has resolved.

Prof's Recurring Teaching Themes
🎓 These Show Up Repeatedly — Know These Cold
  • Safety first: suicidal ideation assessment is always the top priority with mental health patients
  • Therapeutic communication: focus on feelings, not content; stay present; validate
  • Timing of education: never teach during acute panic — postpone until anxiety subsides
