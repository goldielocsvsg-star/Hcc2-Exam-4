Psychosis: Definition, Scope & Consequences

Psychosis is defined in your textbook as "a syndrome of neurocognitive symptoms that impairs cognitive capacity, leading to deficits of perception, functioning, and social relatedness." The DSM-5-TR defines psychosis as abnormalities in five symptomatic domains: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized thought, disorganized or abnormal motor behavior, and negative symptoms.

📖 Textbook: Psychosis is a Syndrome, Not a Diagnosis

Stahl defines psychosis as "a syndrome — a mixture of symptoms that can be associated with many different psychiatric disorders, but is not a specific disorder itself." Psychosis can be primary (psychiatric etiology — schizophrenia, schizoaffective disorder) or secondary (medical etiology — delirium, substance intoxication, dementia, metabolic disorders).

Critical nursing implication: Always rule out secondary (medical) causes before assuming primary psychosis. A patient on a medical-surgical floor experiencing acute confusion, agitation, and hallucinations may have delirium from a UTI, electrolyte imbalance, or drug toxicity — not schizophrenia.

Slide 2: Compassionate Care — A Foundational Principle

Prof emphasizes this as the opening principle: Even though clients with psychosis may be difficult to care for, it is essential to provide holistic, compassionate care. Do not automatically dismiss complaints or symptoms without first assessing whether there is a physiological basis — patients with schizophrenia can and do have comorbid medical conditions.

⭐ Clinical Application — Why This Is Testable

A patient with known schizophrenia who says "my stomach hurts" or "my chest hurts" should be assessed the same as any other patient. Attributing all symptoms to the psychiatric disorder without physical assessment is called diagnostic overshadowing — it is a clinical error and a major reason schizophrenic patients experience premature mortality. The textbook states this explicitly.

Neurobiology — Why Psychosis Occurs
📖 Textbook — Dopamine and Glutamate Dysregulation

A psychobiological definition of psychosis implicates dopamine and glutamate dysregulation between the limbic system and prefrontal cortex. Normal regulation of dopamine within the mesocortical and mesolimbic pathways, affected by NMDA receptor activity in the prefrontal cortex and serotonin regulation at 5-HT2A receptors in the cortex, reflects the absence of psychosis. This is why antipsychotics target dopamine (D2) receptors and why second-generation antipsychotics also target serotonin (5-HT2A).

Consequences of Psychosis

Acute Psychosis (Slide 3)

  • Outcome depends on etiologic factors
  • Many cases reversible — no residual effects (especially secondary psychosis)
  • Problems with daily activities
  • Cognitive dysfunction
  • Impaired personal, social, occupational function
  • Risk for violence to self and/or others

Chronic Psychosis — Schizophrenia (Slide 4)

  • High rates of recidivism, homelessness, incarceration, joblessness
  • May be danger to self and/or others
  • Premature mortality (20–25 years shorter lifespan)
  • Costly to family and society
  • Long-term disability; approximately half do not receive care

Symptoms of Schizophrenia

📘 Framework for Understanding Symptoms

Schizophrenic symptoms are organized into positive symptoms (things that are added to the patient's experience that normally should not be there) and negative symptoms (things that are absent from the patient's experience that should normally be present). This distinction is clinically important because typical (1st-generation) antipsychotics only treat positive symptoms, while atypical (2nd-generation) antipsychotics treat both.

Positive Symptoms

Command Hallucinations Voices that instruct the patient to perform specific actions ⚠️ SAFETY EMERGENCY — assess immediately: "What is the voice telling you to do?" Commands to harm self/others = psychiatric emergency
Delusions Fixed false beliefs held with conviction despite contradicting evidence Do NOT argue or attempt to convince patient the belief is false — this increases anxiety and strengthens the delusion. Focus on feelings instead.
Paranoia Persistent suspiciousness; belief that others intend harm ⚠️ HIGH SAFETY RISK — patient may harm others out of perceived self-defense. Maintain distance; stay near exit; avoid cornering patient.
Disordered Thought/Behavior Disorganized, incoherent speech patterns; bizarre behavior Frightening and frustrating for the patient. Approach with empathy.

Negative Symptoms — The Five A's
⭐ Memorize: The Five A's of Negative Symptoms

Affect (Flat/Blunted)

  • Reduced range/intensity of emotional expression
  • Blank, expressionless face
  • Monotone voice

Alogia

  • Poverty of speech
  • Unable to sustain conversation
  • Brief, empty responses

Avolition

  • Lack of motivation
  • Inability to initiate goal-directed activities
  • Poor hygiene, unable to self-care

Anhedonia

  • Inability to experience pleasure
  • Loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities

Anergia

  • Lack of energy
  • Profound fatigue and inactivity

Key fact: Typical (1st-gen) antipsychotics do NOT treat negative symptoms. Atypical (2nd-gen) antipsychotics treat both positive AND negative symptoms via 5-HT2A antagonism — this is a major therapeutic advantage.

Loss of Function — Including Catatonia
  • Cannot maintain social norms — unpredictable or inappropriate social behavior
  • Disorderly behavior; inappropriate appearance, dress, and conduct
  • Catatonia — a state of unresponsiveness to external stimuli. Includes psychomotor retardation, mutism, stupor, and waxy flexibility (body can be physically repositioned by another person and held in place like a mannequin). Critical nursing concerns: nutrition, hydration, self-care, aspiration risk.

Slides 9 + Textbook Box 33.2 — Nursing Assessment of Psychosis

Assessment of the Psychotic Patient

The textbook emphasizes that psychosis involves progressively compromised neural circuits, with subclinical symptoms appearing before overt psychosis. Early recognition requires a "critical eye" — the expert nurse may be able to identify symptoms below the clinical threshold, which offers the best opportunity to positively influence the trajectory of care.

📖 Textbook: Do NOT Use Clinical Terminology When Assessing

Patients experiencing psychosis often have anosognosia — a lack of insight into their own illness. Using clinical terms is contraindicated. Instead:

  • Instead of "Are you experiencing hallucinations?" → ask: "Do you ever see or hear things that other people can't see or hear?"
  • Instead of "Are you delusional?" → ask: "Tell me about any strong beliefs you hold that others might disagree with."
  • Instead of "Are you paranoid?" → ask: "Are you influenced strongly by the beliefs or intentions of others?"

Nursing Care — Psychosis

⚠️ Priority: Safety — Slide 10

Watch for themes related to suicide or homicide in patient statements. Examples from Prof's slide: "People are trying to kill me" (paranoid delusion — potential for self-protective violence); "My guardian angel told me to kill my doctor" (command hallucination — direct threat to others).

Nurse safety when interacting with an agitated client: Stay near the exit (never allow the patient to block your path); maintain safe personal distance (at least arm's length plus); use calm, non-threatening body language (open hands, no crossed arms); avoid sudden movements; do not touch without warning; have additional staff available but not crowding the space.

How to Help a Patient with Hallucinations
Prof asks: "How should the nurse help a patient with hallucinations?" What is the correct nursing approach? ▼
✓ Full Clinical Answer
  • Do NOT deny the hallucination: "I don't hear anything" — this invalidates the patient's very real subjective experience and damages trust
  • Do NOT reinforce or play along: "Oh yes, I hear the voices too" — reinforcing delusion/hallucination worsens it and is non-therapeutic
  • Acknowledge the experience and focus on feelings: "I understand you are hearing voices. That sounds very frightening. I want to help you feel safe."
  • Orient gently to reality: "I know the voices feel very real to you. What I can tell you is that you are in the hospital, and you are safe here with me."
  • Provide distraction and activity to interrupt the hallucination cycle — structured activities, music, conversation about neutral topics
  • Teach coping strategies (when patient is calm and stable, not during acute episode) — humming, listening to music, snapping fingers, engaging in physical activity can interrupt auditory hallucinations
  • For command hallucinations specifically: assess immediately what the command is and implement safety measures if needed
Milieu Therapy — Slide 10
  • Structure — a predictable, consistent daily routine reduces anxiety by making the environment comprehensible and safe; important because the psychotic patient has impaired reality testing and cannot create structure independently
  • Safety — therapeutic environment with removal of dangerous objects; clear rules and consistent boundaries; continuous observation for high-risk patients
  • Learning Skills — social skills training, ADL retraining, medication education; the textbook notes that group-format social skills training (SST) improves social performance and interaction
Patient and Family Education — Slide 10
⭐ Early Signs of Relapse — Prof Explicitly Says to Teach This
  • Reduced sleep and concentration
  • Troubling or unusual thoughts returning
  • Hearing voices again or worsening of voices
  • Increasing restlessness or agitation
  • Becoming more uncomfortable around others, increasing social withdrawal
  • Any decline in medication adherence — this is the #1 modifiable cause of relapse

What to do: Teach patient and family that if ANY of these signs appear, they should contact the provider or go to the nearest mental health crisis service before a full relapse occurs. Early intervention dramatically improves outcomes.

  • Residential crisis centers — 24-hour support in a home-like environment; less restrictive than inpatient hospital
  • Halfway houses / Group Homes — supervised community living; structured support while building independence
  • Day treatment programs — outpatient; structured activities, therapy, medication management
  • Textbook also mentions Assertive Community Treatment (ACT) — team of case manager, PCP, mental health provider, and nurse; care is accessible 24 hours/day in community settings; proven clinically and fiscally effective

Antipsychotic Medications

📘 How Antipsychotics Work — Mechanism

All antipsychotics block dopamine D2 receptors. First-generation agents do this primarily in 4 dopaminergic pathways — their blockade in the nigrostriatal pathway causes EPS. Second-generation agents block D2 receptors more loosely AND block serotonin 5-HT2A receptors, which confers benefits for negative symptoms and reduces EPS risk. Third-generation (aripiprazole) works as a partial D2 agonist — more nuanced receptor modulation.

First vs. Second Generation Antipsychotics — Full Comparison
FeatureFirst-Generation (Typical)Second-Generation (Atypical)
Mechanism Block D2 receptors (4 dopaminergic pathways) Block D2 (loosely) + 5-HT2A + some 5-HT1A agonism
Symptoms Treated Positive symptoms ONLY (hallucinations, delusions, paranoia) Positive AND Negative symptoms — major advantage
EPS Risk HIGH — major clinical concern; dystonia, pseudoparkinsonism, akathisia, tardive dyskinesia Lower — but NOT zero; tardive dyskinesia still possible with long-term use
Metabolic Risk Moderate HIGH — weight gain, dyslipidemia, elevated glucose/diabetes — requires serial metabolic monitoring
Current Status No longer considered first-tier; still used in community and forensic settings First-line choice for treatment of psychosis per textbook
Cost Lower Higher
Key Examples Haloperidol (Haldol), Chlorpromazine (Thorazine) Risperidone* (Risperdal), Olanzapine (Zyprexa), Quetiapine (Seroquel), Ziprasidone (Geodon), Aripiprazole** (Abilify), Clozapine† (Clozaril)

*Risperidone = ATI prototype for atypical antipsychotics. **Aripiprazole = third-generation (partial D2 agonist). †Clozapine = last resort only — see below.

Clozapine — Special Considerations
⚠️ Clozapine (Clozaril) — Last Resort Only
  • Used only when other antipsychotics have failed — indicated for treatment-resistant schizophrenia
  • Most serious side effect: Agranulocytosis — life-threatening reduction in white blood cells → severe infection risk
  • Requires regular WBC monitoring — weekly for 6 months, then biweekly, then monthly
  • Patient teaching: Report fever, chills, sore throat, or any sign of infection immediately — this may indicate agranulocytosis and the medication must be stopped
  • Also associated with metabolic syndrome, seizures, myocarditis, excessive salivation (sialorrhea)
Second-Generation Side Effect Teaching — Key NCLEX Points

Metabolic Syndrome

  • Weight gain (most common SE)
  • ↑blood glucose → diabetes risk
  • Dyslipidemia (↑cholesterol/triglycerides)
  • ↑cardiovascular disease risk
  • Teaching: Monitor weight, blood glucose, lipid panel; diet counseling; exercise; report significant weight gain to provider
  • Serial metabolic monitoring: abdominal obesity, triglycerides, HDL, BP, fasting glucose

Anticholinergic Effects

  • Dry mouth (xerostomia)
  • Urinary retention
  • Constipation
  • Blurred vision
  • Tachycardia
  • Teaching: Increase fluid/fiber intake; use sugarless gum for dry mouth; contact provider if unable to urinate

Orthostatic Hypotension

  • BP drops when rising from sitting or lying position
  • Fall risk — especially in elderly patients
  • Teaching: Rise slowly; sit on edge of bed for 1–2 min before standing; hold railing or ask for assistance; report dizziness

Long-Acting Injectables (LAIs)

  • Available for both 1st and 2nd gen antipsychotics
  • Purpose: improves adherence for patients who have difficulty with daily oral medications
  • Examples: Haloperidol decanoate, Risperidone Consta, Aripiprazole Maintena
  • Reserved for patients with history of frequent hospitalizations or confirmed adherence difficulty
NCLEX Q: "A patient with schizophrenia begins a new prescription for risperidone. The patient is 5'6" and weighs 204 lbs. Which topic is MOST important for the nurse to include in teaching?" ▼
✓ Answer: Weight Management Strategies

Reasoning from lecture notes: Risperidone is a 2nd-generation (atypical) antipsychotic. The most common and clinically significant side effects of 2nd-gen antipsychotics are metabolic — weight gain, elevated blood glucose, dyslipidemia. This patient is already at an elevated BMI (overweight). The most important priority teaching addresses the highest-risk side effect for this specific patient.

  • ✅ Weight management strategies — patient is already overweight; risperidone significantly increases metabolic risk
  • Tardive dyskinesia recognition — less immediate concern with 2nd-gen vs. 1st-gen; also patient is newly starting medication (TD appears after 6+ months)
  • Constipation management — anticholinergic concern but not the highest priority for this patient
  • Sleep hygiene — not specific to this medication

Study tip (from lecture notes): To answer this, you must know (1) which generation risperidone is, (2) the most common SE of that generation, and (3) which listed SE is most serious/relevant for this particular patient given their clinical context.

Extrapyramidal Side Effects (EPS)

EPS are medication-induced movement disorders caused by blockade of dopamine D2 receptors in the nigrostriatal pathway (basal ganglia), which creates an imbalance between dopamine and acetylcholine. They are primarily associated with first-generation antipsychotics but can occur with second-generation agents as well.

Prof asks: "Why does Benztropine treat dystonia and pseudoparkinsonism? How does Diphenhydramine work for EPS?" (Lecture notes highlight this mechanism) ▼
✓ Mechanism Answer from Lecture Notes

Antipsychotics block dopamine D2 receptors in the basal ganglia (nigrostriatal pathway). The basal ganglia require a balanced ratio of dopamine and acetylcholine for normal movement. When dopamine receptors are blocked, acetylcholine becomes relatively dominant — this imbalance produces EPS (muscle rigidity, spasms, slowness).

  • Benztropine (anticholinergic) — blocks acetylcholine receptors → reduces acetylcholine activity → restores the dopamine-acetylcholine balance → EPS resolves
  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) — also has significant anticholinergic properties → blocks acetylcholine → equalizes dopamine/ACh ratio → EPS resolves
  • Akathisia exception: does NOT respond well to anticholinergics. Beta-blockers (propranolol) have the best therapeutic success — mechanism involves blocking peripheral adrenergic activity contributing to restlessness
  • Tardive Dyskinesia: Benztropine does NOT treat TD — TD involves a different mechanism (D2 receptor sensitization from chronic blockade). Now treated with VMAT2 inhibitors (valbenazine, deutetrabenazine)
⭐ EPS Treatment Summary — Quick Reference
  • Dystonia → Benztropine or Diphenhydramine (anticholinergics)
  • Pseudoparkinsonism → Benztropine (anticholinergic)
  • Akathisia → Beta-blockers (propranolol) — NOT anticholinergics
  • Tardive Dyskinesia → No cure; VMAT2 inhibitors; AIMS monitoring — Benztropine does NOT work

Slide 17 — Life-Threatening Emergency

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

🚨 MEDICAL EMERGENCY — LIFE-THREATENING REACTION TO ANTIPSYCHOTIC MEDICATIONS

NMS is a rare but potentially fatal adverse reaction to antipsychotic (neuroleptic) medications. It requires immediate recognition, cessation of the causative drug, and transfer to ICU-level care. Mortality rate without treatment can reach 10–20%.

Signs and Symptoms (Slide 17)

Cardinal Signs — FEVER Mnemonic

  • F — Fever (markedly elevated temperature — often >40°C/104°F)
  • E — Encephalopathy (altered LOC, confusion, stupor)
  • V — Vitals unstable (labile hypertension, tachycardia, tachypnea)
  • E — Elevated labs (leukocytosis, elevated liver enzymes, elevated CK/CPK, myoglobinuria)
  • R — Rigidity (severe "lead-pipe" muscle rigidity)

Additional Signs

  • Diaphoresis (profuse sweating)
  • Drooling
  • Oculogyric crisis
  • Pallor
  • Dysphagia
  • Rhabdomyolysis (muscle protein breakdown)
  • Myoglobinuria (dark/brown urine)
Nursing Interventions — NMS (Slide 17)
⚠️ Priority Order of Nursing Actions
  1. Stay with the patient — patient is critically unstable; do not leave
  2. Notify the HCP immediately and prepare to transfer to the ICU
  3. STOP all antipsychotic medications immediately — this is the first pharmacologic intervention; removing the causative agent is essential
  4. Continuously monitor vital signs — labile hypertension and tachycardia require close tracking
  5. Reduce body temperature — apply cooling measures: ice packs to axillae/groin, cooling blankets, antipyretics
  6. IV fluids — maintain hydration; prevent acute kidney injury from myoglobinuria
  7. Cardiac monitoring — continuous ECG monitoring in ICU
  8. Administer Dantrolene (dopamine agonist — reduces muscle rigidity by acting directly on skeletal muscle)
  9. Monitor for complications: DVT (immobility + hyperthermia + rigidity = high clot risk) and rhabdomyolysis (muscle breakdown releases myoglobin → can cause acute renal failure)

Slides 10 + Lecture Notes — NCLEX-Style Application

Therapeutic Communication with Psychotic & Delusional Patients

📘 Core Principle

When communicating with a psychotic patient, the nurse's goal is to acknowledge the patient's emotional experience without reinforcing or arguing against the content of the delusion or hallucination. The content is less important than the feeling it generates. Arguing increases anxiety and actually strengthens the tenacity of delusional beliefs.

NCLEX Scenario Practice — All from Lecture Notes
SCENARIO: Patient shouts "They're all plotting to destroy me. Isn't that true?" What is the most therapeutic response? ▼
✓ Correct: "Feeling that people want to destroy you must be very frightening."

Rationale: This response acknowledges the feeling (fear/terror) behind the delusion without validating the delusional content or arguing against it. It opens space for the patient to feel heard and begins to de-escalate the emotional intensity.

Why the other options are wrong:

  • ✗ "Everyone here is trying to help you. No one wants to harm you." — argues directly with the delusion → increases anxiety, strengthens delusion
  • ✗ "That is not true. People here are trying to help you if you will let them." — same problem; confrontational; provides opportunity for argument
  • ✗ "Staff members are healthcare professionals who are qualified to help you." — focuses on content/reasoning, not feelings; still an implied argument
SCENARIO: Client says "Facebook has a new tracking capacity. If I use the Internet, Homeland Security will detain me as a terrorist." What is the appropriate initial action? ▼
✓ Correct: "You are in a safe place where you will be helped."

Rationale: The client is experiencing paranoid delusional thinking, which generates profound fear. Explaining that the client is in a safe place addresses the underlying emotional need (safety, security) without arguing about the reality of Facebook/Homeland Security.

  • ✗ "Facebook is not a dangerous website, and Homeland Security is not after you." — directly argues with delusion → patient becomes more defensive and anxious
  • ✗ "You don't need to worry about any of that." — dismisses the patient's fear without validation
  • ✗ Administer an antipsychotic prn first — medication does not address the immediate emotional/safety concern; communication is the first step; also, giving medication without first attempting de-escalation is not therapeutic
SCENARIO: Patient with schizophrenia and auditory hallucinations tells the nurse, "The voice is telling me to do things." What is the PRIORITY assessment question? ▼
✓ Correct: "What is the voice telling you to do?"

Rationale: Learning the specific content of a command hallucination is the PRIORITY because the command may direct the patient to harm themselves or others — this constitutes an immediate psychiatric emergency. You cannot assess the level of danger without knowing the command.

  • ✗ "How long has the voice been telling you to do things?" — duration is relevant but not the priority; does not assess safety
  • ✗ "Are you afraid of the voice?" — emotional assessment; secondary to safety
  • ✗ "Do you recognize the voice speaking to you?" — identity of voice is irrelevant to immediate safety

Principle: Safety always precedes all other assessment priorities. Identify the specific command before making any other intervention decision.

Quick-Reference EPS Summary

First-Gen → High EPS Risk

  • Haloperidol (Haldol)
  • Chlorpromazine (Thorazine)
  • Treats POSITIVE symptoms only
  • Dystonia → Benztropine or Benadryl
  • Parkinsonism → Benztropine
  • Akathisia → Beta-blockers
  • TD → AIMS monitoring; VMAT2 inhibitors

Second-Gen → Metabolic Risk

  • Risperidone* (ATI prototype)
  • Olanzapine, Quetiapine, Ziprasidone
  • Aripiprazole (3rd gen)
  • Clozapine† (LAST RESORT — agranulocytosis)
  • Treats + AND − symptoms
  • SE: weight gain, ↑glucose, dyslipidemia
  • Monitor: weight, glucose, lipids, BP
Prof's Recurring Teaching Themes — Know These Cold
🎓 High-Priority Concepts — Appear Across Multiple Questions
  • Safety always first: command hallucinations and paranoid delusions both carry violence risk — assess, then intervene
  • Compassionate care principle: never attribute all symptoms to psychiatric diagnosis without physical assessment first
  • Communication is always before medication: de-escalate verbally before reaching for PRN antipsychotic
  • Positive vs. negative symptoms: 1st-gen treats positive only; 2nd-gen treats both — this drives prescribing decisions
  • NMS is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY: FEVER, stop the drug, ICU, Dantrolene
  • Medication non-adherence = #1 cause of relapse — explore reasons and address barriers; consider LAIs for high-risk patients
  • Do NOT argue with delusions: always focus on the feeling, not the content
  • Least restrictive environment is always the goal for community reintegration
