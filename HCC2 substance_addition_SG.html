Module Goal + Concept Foundation

Big Picture: What Addiction Means in HCC2

Core Concept

Substance misuse and addiction is not just "using drugs." In your notes, addiction is framed as a compulsive dependence on a substance or behavior that creates psychological, physical, economic, social, or legal consequences. The HCC2 priority is not moral judgment. The priority is recognition, assessment, emergency safety, motivation toward recovery, and continuity of care.

What addiction includes

  • Inability to abstain.
  • Craving or strong desire to use.
  • Intoxication effects while the substance is active.
  • Tolerance requiring more substance for the same effect.
  • Withdrawal when the substance is reduced or stopped.
  • Relapse risk even after periods of sobriety.

What makes this an exam safety topic

  • Alcohol withdrawal can progress to seizures and death.
  • Benzodiazepine withdrawal can cause seizures.
  • Opioid overdose can cause respiratory arrest.
  • Stimulant intoxication can cause dysrhythmias, seizures, coma, or suicidal crash.
  • Patients may deny or underreport use, so the nurse must assess patterns.

Priority Nursing Lens

When the patient is chemically impaired, first ask: Is this withdrawal? Is this overdose? Is airway/breathing/circulation threatened? Is the patient suicidal or self-destructive? Are there physical complications? That thinking pattern matches your course objectives and your slides.

Risk Factors + Trauma Connection

Why Substance Use Disorder Develops: Risk, Coping, Trauma, and Brain Reward

Big Picture

Your professor notes repeatedly connect substance use with trauma, mental illness, maladaptive coping, poverty, poor support, and family/genetic risk. The nursing trap is assuming the substance is the whole problem. In this module, addiction is often a coping strategy that became dangerous, compulsive, and physiologically reinforced.

Genetic

  • Slides note that about 40–60% of alcoholism risk comes from inheritance.
  • Family history increases vulnerability but does not remove responsibility for recovery.

Environmental

  • Poverty, poor educational resources, poor parental supervision, impaired support systems.
  • Stress and anxiety can increase, while positive social ties decrease.
  • Maladaptive coping may become alcohol/drug use.

Sociocultural

  • Some cultures normalize alcohol as everyday life.
  • Other cultures strictly forbid substance use.
  • The nurse assesses without assuming values or shaming.
Trauma Connection

Your slides describe trauma as a "bruising" of the central nervous system that can alter neurotransmitter production and neural pathways. The important nursing translation: many clients are not simply "choosing drugs"; they may be attempting to medicate traumatic activation, hyperarousal, anxiety, pain, or emotional distress.

Trauma/SUD LinkNursing MeaningExam Trap
Trauma and addictive behavior are connected.Assessment must include trauma history, coping, mental illness, and safety.Do not isolate substance use from mental health.
Addictive behavior may medicate traumatic activation.The substance may temporarily reduce distress, fear, anxiety, or hypervigilance.Do not lecture first; explore function of use.
Recovery from either requires recovering from both.Long-term plan must address both trauma and substance use patterns.Detox alone is not the whole recovery plan.
Clinical Judgment Note

Therapeutic care means being nonjudgmental but firm. The nurse can hold boundaries, protect safety, and still communicate dignity. Shame increases hiding; trust improves disclosure.

Screening, History, Safety Assessment

Assessment of the Chemically Impaired Patient

Do This First

For a chemically impaired patient, the first priority is determining whether the patient is experiencing life-threatening withdrawal, overdose, suicidal/self-destructive behavior, or physical complications. Long-term teaching comes after immediate stabilization.

History Clues That Suggest Substance Use Disorder
  • Problems in legal, financial, social, marital, or occupational functioning.
  • Leisure activities frequently involve alcohol or other drugs.
  • Frequent references to alcohol/drugs suggest preoccupation.
  • Driving while intoxicated.
  • Injuries from falls, fights, automobile crashes, burns, or other trauma.
  • Hesitation to disclose substance use because of fear, shame, stigma, or consequences.
  • Use of prescription meds, OTC meds, herbal/homeopathic products, caffeine, tobacco, alcohol, and recreational substances should be explored.

CAGE-AID

  • Cut down: felt need to reduce use?
  • Annoyed: criticized by others?
  • Guilty: felt bad/guilty?
  • Eye-opener: drink/use first thing to steady nerves or relieve symptoms?

Major Assessment Priorities

  • Assess severe withdrawal first.
  • Assess overdose needing immediate attention.
  • Assess suicidal thoughts/self-destructive behavior.
  • Evaluate complications from drug use.
  • Explore interest in doing something about the problem.
  • Assess patient/family knowledge of treatment resources.

Therapeutic Communication

Use confidentiality, rapport, active listening, affirmation, and motivational interviewing style. Avoid arguing. Avoid "You need to stop now" as the first move. Better nursing thinking: What does this substance do for the patient? What are they afraid will happen without it? What goal matters enough to them to consider change?

Alcohol / Ethanol • CNS Depressant • Highest Withdrawal Danger

Alcohol: Intoxication, Withdrawal, DTs, CIWA, and Nursing Priorities

High-Yield Alert

Alcohol is identified in the slides as the most dangerous withdrawal substance. A patient with heavy daily alcohol use should not quit "cold turkey." Alcohol withdrawal can progress to tonic-clonic seizures, delirium tremens, dysrhythmias, and death.

Alcohol Intoxication

  • CNS depressant effects.
  • Lower intake: euphoria, reduced social inhibition, flushed skin.
  • Higher intake: drowsiness, impaired balance/coordination/judgment.
  • May show erratic or violent behavior.
  • High levels can cause nausea, vomiting, and potentially lethal CNS depression.

Alcohol Withdrawal

  • Usually starts 4–12 hours after last intake in slides; book notes describe 6–9 hours as possible.
  • Peaks around 24–48 hours.
  • Abdominal cramping, vomiting, tremors.
  • Increased HR, BP, RR, temperature.
  • Transient hallucinations or illusions.
  • Tonic-clonic seizures.

Alcohol withdrawal delirium may occur 2–3 days after cessation and may last 2–3 days. It is a medical emergency. Symptoms from the slides include hallucinations, tachypnea, tremors, insomnia, severe hypertension, severe disorientation, cardiac dysrhythmias, and delirium. Symptoms may progress to death.

Most Concerning Cues

Severe disorientation + autonomic instability + hallucinations + dysrhythmias = unstable patient. Do not treat this as "just anxiety."

Exam Trap

If a hospitalized patient with alcoholism had the last drink two days ago and now has tremors, diaphoresis, and elevated HR, the action is not "just watch hourly" or give opioid pain meds. The notes point toward benzodiazepine treatment per alcohol withdrawal policy.

Opioids • Respiratory Depression • Naloxone

Opioids: Intoxication, Withdrawal, Overdose, and Emergency Care

Priority Nursing Action

Opioid overdose is an oxygenation/ventilation emergency. The immediate threat is respiratory depression or arrest, not the patient's long-term addiction plan.

Opioid Intoxication / Overdose

  • Psychomotor/CNS depression.
  • Decreased respiratory rate.
  • Constricted pupils.
  • Drowsiness or sedation; may be difficult to arouse.
  • Slurred speech, euphoria, decreased LOC.
  • Bradycardia, hypotension, flushed/warm skin.
  • Impaired judgment, coma, death.

Opioid Withdrawal

  • Nausea/vomiting.
  • Fever, chills, sweating.
  • Irritability or panic.
  • Piloerection / goose flesh.
  • Runny nose, watery eyes, yawning.
  • Bone pain, muscle aches.
  • Strong drug craving.
  • Slides: uncomfortable, rarely fatal.
  • Administer naloxone.
  • Give oxygen if hypoxic or respirations are below 10/minute.
  • Have suction equipment available because vomiting/aspiration risk can occur as consciousness returns.
  • Continuously monitor vital signs and level of consciousness for reversal of overdose.
  • Do not leave the patient until fully responsive.
  • Support airway, ventilation, and cardiovascular function.

Safety Risk

After naloxone, the patient may wake abruptly, frightened, or panicked. Do not walk away because the patient can become unsafe, vomit, aspirate, or become sedated again depending on the opioid involved.

Amphetamines • Cocaine • Methamphetamine • Crack • Bath Salts

Stimulants: Overactivation, Cardiovascular Risk, Crash, and Suicide Safety

Intoxication

  • Rush of euphoria/pleasure.
  • Increased mental alertness.
  • Aggressiveness, hypervigilance, sensitivity.
  • Anxious, tense, angry, sociable or "superhuman."
  • Chest pain, dysrhythmias.
  • High or low BP, tachycardia or bradycardia.
  • Dilated pupils, perspiration, chills, nausea/vomiting, weight loss.
  • Confusion, seizures, coma.

Withdrawal / Crash

  • CNS depression ranging from lethargy to coma.
  • Hallucinations.
  • Aches.
  • Depression.
  • Suicidal thoughts.
  • This is opposite-effect rebound: stopping a stimulating drug can produce sedating crash symptoms.
Priority Nursing Action

During stimulant intoxication, think cardiac + neuro safety: chest pain, dysrhythmias, BP instability, seizures, severe agitation, confusion, or coma are unstable cues. During withdrawal, think suicide risk because depression and suicidal thoughts are specifically listed in your notes.

Benzodiazepines • Barbiturates • Sleep Meds • Antianxiety Meds

Sedative, Hypnotic, and Antianxiety Medications

High-Yield Alert

Alcohol and benzodiazepines are the dangerous withdrawal categories emphasized in the slides. Benzodiazepine withdrawal after extended high-dose use can cause seizures, so gradual reduction matters.

Intoxication

  • CNS depressant effects.
  • Slurred speech.
  • Incoordination, unsteady gait, nystagmus.
  • Impaired thinking and judgment.
  • Inappropriate aggression or sexual behavior.
  • Mood fluctuation.
  • Respiratory depression can require ventilation support.

Withdrawal

  • May begin 3–4 days after last dose in your notes.
  • Headache and sweating.
  • Confusion and anxiety.
  • Delirium, paranoia.
  • Seizure risk with benzodiazepine withdrawal.

Withdrawal preventionGradual taper/reduction as ordered.Prevents severe withdrawal and seizures.

Recovery • Relapse • Families • Nurses with SUD

Recovery, Relapse Prevention, Motivation, and Continuity of Care

Big Picture

Recovery is not just absence of use. The notes describe recovery as an ongoing process where the patient gets help needed to maintain sobriety. Relapse is common and should be anticipated through coping plans, support systems, and treatment continuity.

Treatment Continuum

  • Detoxification.
  • Rehabilitation.
  • Halfway house.
  • Partial hospitalization.
  • Intensive outpatient programs.
  • Outpatient follow-up.
  • Mutual support groups such as AA or NA.

Recovery Teaching

  • Goal is self-responsibility.
  • Patient is not responsible for having the disease but is responsible for recovery work.
  • Others cannot be blamed indefinitely; patient must acknowledge own feelings/problems.
  • Nonreligious programs exist if higher-power language is a barrier.
  • Build relationship and trust.
  • Listen more than talk.
  • Do not argue or persuade aggressively because this increases resistance.
  • Explore ambivalence: what does the substance do for them, and what does it cost them?
  • Develop discrepancy between current use and the patient's goals.
  • Use concrete change talk if patient has co-occurring psychosis/confusion.
  • If too psychotic or dangerous, stabilize psychosis/safety first.
Families and Codependence

Addiction affects the family system. Notes identify codependence as enabling the addiction, lying to keep secrets, or becoming overly responsible for the addicted person. Families may benefit from support such as Al-Anon or Adult Children of Alcoholics.

Nurses with SUD

Substance use disorder among nurses is a professional safety issue. Watch for fatigue, mood swings, lateness, medication discrepancies, volunteering to administer controlled substances, and defensiveness. The immediate action is to report concerns to management, followed by objective evaluation/drug screening and support through peer assistance or alternative-to-discipline programs when appropriate.

Final High-Yield Summary

What I Must Know Before the Exam

Most dangerous withdrawal:

Alcohol. Benzodiazepines are also dangerous because of seizure risk.

Opioid overdose cue:

Respiratory depression + constricted pupils + decreased LOC. Give oxygen/support airway and naloxone per protocol.

Alcohol withdrawal cue:

Tremors, diaphoresis, increased HR/BP/temp, hallucinations, seizures. CIWA-Ar may guide benzodiazepine dosing.

DTs:

2–3 days after cessation, severe disorientation, hallucinations, tremors, severe HTN, dysrhythmias, delirium. Emergency.

Stimulant crash:

CNS depression, depression, suicidal thoughts. Assess self-harm risk.

Therapeutic communication:

Nonjudgmental, kind firmness, motivational interviewing, listen more than lecture.
