Foundation

Core Concepts

Get the vocabulary right before anything else — the exam will test whether you know what the patient feels vs. what you observe.

😌Mood — Subjective
💬
DefinitionWhat the patient tells you they feel internally. It is self-reported and subjective.
  • Reported by the patient, not observed by the nurse
  • Documented using the patient's own words in quotes
  • Example: "I feel hopeless and empty"
  • Can be inconsistent with what you observe
👀Affect — Objective
📋
DefinitionWhat you observe about the patient's emotional expression — body language, voice, face.
  • Assessed by the nurse through direct observation
  • Documented using descriptive clinical terms
  • Example: "Patient sits slumped, monotone voice, no eye contact"
  • May mismatch reported mood (important clinical finding)
⚠️
NCLEX TipIf a question says the patient says something → that's mood (subjective). If the question says the nurse observes something → that's affect (objective). This distinction matters for documentation and assessment questions.
Flat Affect
😐
No emotional expression whatsoever — no smiling, no frowning, monotone voice
Blunted Affect
😑
Some expression present, but very minimal and reduced in range
Labile Affect
😂😭
Rapid, unpredictable shifts — laughing then crying then angry
Inappropriate Affect
😆❌
Emotion doesn't match situation — laughing at sad news
Restricted Affect
😶
Narrowed range of expression — some present but noticeably limited
Anhedonia
💔
Loss of pleasure in previously enjoyed activities — ⭐ CORE DSM symptom
⭐
Why This Is High-YieldAnhedonia is one of TWO core symptoms required to diagnose major depressive disorder. Every NCLEX question on depression can reference it. Know it cold.
What Anhedonia Looks Like
  • Stops hobbies they previously loved (reading, sports, music)
  • No interest in socializing or spending time with family
  • Does not respond to things that used to bring joy
  • Food, sex, humor — nothing feels pleasurable
  • Distinct from sadness — it's emptiness, not pain
Clinical Significance
💬
Screening Question"Have you lost interest in activities you used to enjoy?" — This directly assesses for anhedonia.
⚡
In Mania — The OppositePatients pursue excessive pleasurable activities with poor judgment: spending sprees, risky sex, impulsive projects.
👥Social
  • Poor quality relationships
  • Social withdrawal and isolation
  • Family stress and conflict
  • Loss of friendships
💼Functional
  • Decreased work productivity
  • Job loss or academic failure
  • Inability to perform ADLs
  • Financial consequences
🏥Health
  • ↑ Healthcare utilization
  • Chronic illness worsening
  • Substance abuse comorbidity
  • HIGH SUICIDE RISK

🔴
Strongest PredictorsPrior depressive episode · Family history · Female gender · Age <40
All Risk Factors
  • Prior depressive episode — strongest predictor of recurrence
  • Family history of depression or mood disorders
  • Female gender — 2× more likely than males
  • Age <40 — peak onset in young adulthood
  • Postpartum period — hormonal changes + stress + sleep deprivation
  • Chronic illness — cancer, diabetes, heart disease, chronic pain
  • Substance abuse — self-medication, worsens depression
  • Lack of social support — isolation is a major driver
  • Trauma / abuse history — ACEs strongly correlated
  • Medications — steroids, antihypertensives, hormonal contraceptives
  • Medical conditions — thyroid disorders, vitamin deficiencies
Medical Causes — Always Screen
⚠️
Rule Out Medical FirstThyroid disorders, anemia, vitamin D deficiency, and chronic pain can all mimic or cause depression. The nurse assesses for these before attributing symptoms purely to psychiatric cause.
  • Hypothyroidism → fatigue, weight gain, low mood
  • Anemia → fatigue, cognitive slowing
  • Chronic pain syndromes
  • Neurological disorders (Parkinson's, dementia)
  • Corticosteroids — mood changes are common side effect
  • Antihypertensives — beta-blockers linked to depression
🚨
Priority #1 — Always Assess Suicide Risk FirstBefore ANY other nursing action — assess for suicidal ideation. This is non-negotiable in any patient with depression.
Assessment Framework
🚨 First — Suicide Risk
  • Do they have a plan?
  • Do they have access to means?
  • Do they have intent to act?
⚠️ Also Assess
  • Substance use history
  • Medical causes — thyroid, chronic illness
  • Medications causing mood changes
  • Support system and living situation
📋 Screening Questions

  • "Have you lost interest in things you used to enjoy?"

💡
PHQ-2 Questions"Over the last 2 weeks, have you had little interest or pleasure in doing things?" and "Feeling down, depressed, or hopeless?" Score ≥3 → full PHQ-9.
Children
Crying spells, social withdrawal, school refusal, somatic complaints (stomachache, headache). Rarely say "I feel sad" — look for behavioral changes.
Pearl: Look for behavior, not words
Adolescents
Irritability — NOT sadness — is the hallmark. Risk-taking behavior, substance experimentation, academic decline, isolating from peers. Easily missed as "just a phase."
Pearl: Irritability in teens = rule out depression
Elderly
Often presents as physical complaints — pain, fatigue, cognitive slowing. Frequently underdiagnosed because symptoms are attributed to "normal aging." May not report sadness.
Pearl: Never assume it's just aging — screen with PHQ-9
Postpartum
"Baby blues" = mild, resolves within 2 weeks. Postpartum depression = severe, persistent beyond 2 weeks, requires treatment. Can escalate to postpartum psychosis — hallucinations, delusions = EMERGENCY.
Pearl: Screening at every postpartum visit; ask about thoughts of harming self or baby

Mania & Bipolar

Three stages of severity, each requiring a different response. Know when to hospitalize, how to set limits, and how to safely nourish and de-escalate.

Stage 1
🌤 Hypomania
Elevated mood, increased energy, still functional. May feel like they're at their best. Does NOT yet require hospitalization.
MildFunctionalOutpatient
Stage 2
⚡ Acute Mania
Severe impairment. Flight of ideas, grandiosity, pressured speech, poor judgment. Hospitalization required.
SevereHospitalizeImpaired
Stage 3 🚨
🔥 Delirious Mania
Hallucinations + Delusions + Extreme agitation + Panic = Psychiatric Emergency. Full crisis intervention.
EmergencyPsychotic Features
🚨
Recognize Stage 3 on Your ExamThe combination of hallucinations + delusions + extreme agitation + panic together = Delirious Mania. This is a psychiatric emergency. Not just "bad mania" — it requires emergency-level intervention immediately.
Mood & Cognitive
  • Elevated or irritable mood — can swing between euphoric and explosive
  • Grandiosity — inflated self-esteem, believes they have special powers or status
  • Flight of ideas — rapid topic-jumping, thoughts racing faster than speech
  • Pressured speech — talks non-stop, hard or impossible to interrupt
  • Distractibility — pulled to every irrelevant stimuli in the environment
Behavioral
  • Decreased need for sleep — feels fully rested after 2–3 hours
  • Impulsivity — acts without thinking through consequences
  • Poor judgment — spending sprees, risky investments, reckless driving
  • Hypersexuality — inappropriate sexual behavior toward others
  • Psychomotor agitation — constant movement, can't sit still
  • Poor nutrition — too distracted/energized to eat
🚨
Safety — Always FirstMonitor for risky behaviors (financial, sexual, reckless). Remove opportunities for poor judgment. This is priority #1.
🌙 Sleep
  • Encourage rest periods and naps throughout the day
  • Monitor total sleep — deprivation worsens mania
  • Dim lights in evenings, reduce stimulation at night
🔇 Environment — Key Intervention
🔑
LOW STIMULUS = Most Important InterventionPrivate, quiet room away from high-traffic areas. Reduce noise, people, and sensory input. This directly reduces agitation.
🍔 Nutrition Strategy
✅
Finger Foods OnlyManic patients cannot sit still for a full meal. Provide high-calorie, portable snacks: sandwiches, granola bars, cheese, fruit, protein bars.
  • Daily weights to detect nutritional decline
  • Monitor I&O — dehydration risk
  • Hydration throughout the day
🗣️ Communication
  • Never argue — escalates agitation immediately
  • Set clear, firm, consistent limits
  • Stay calm — don't mirror their energy
  • Use short, simple, direct sentences
📢
Inappropriate Behavior ScriptSay: "That behavior is inappropriate." — Direct, calm, non-argumentative. Never ignore it. Never lecture at length.

😞 Depression

  • Low energy, constant fatigue
  • Psychomotor retardation (slowed down)
  • Decreased, slow speech
  • Hypersomnia OR insomnia
  • Poor appetite, weight change
  • Hopeless, worthless, guilty
  • Social withdrawal
  • Flat or blunted affect
  • Loss of pleasure (anhedonia)

⚡ Mania

  • High energy, feels invincible
  • Psychomotor agitation (can't sit still)
  • Pressured, rapid, non-stop speech
  • Decreased need for sleep (feels rested)
  • Neglects eating (too busy)
  • Grandiose, inflated self-esteem
  • Over-involved with everyone
  • Labile or elevated affect
  • Pursues excessive pleasures
🧠
Bipolar DisorderPatients cycle between depression and mania. They may present at either pole, or experience mixed states. Treatment requires mood stabilizers (lithium, valproate) — not just antidepressants, which can precipitate mania.
Highest Priority

Suicide Assessment
& Prevention

The #1 nursing priority in psychiatric care. Safety always beats autonomy. Know how to assess, intervene, and protect.

🚨
Non-Negotiable NCLEX RuleSuicide risk assessment is ALWAYS the FIRST nursing priority with any psychiatric patient — before medication, before education, before any other assessment. Period.
🔴
Biggest Risk FactorPrevious suicide attempt — the single strongest predictor of future attempts. ALWAYS ask about history.
All Risk Factors
  • Previous attempt — #1 predictor
  • Depression or major mood disorder
  • Substance abuse (↑ impulsivity + hopelessness)
  • Lack of social support / isolation
  • Access to lethal means (firearms, stockpiled meds)
  • Family history of suicide
  • Chronic pain or terminal illness
  • Recent significant loss (job, relationship, death)
  • Male gender (higher completion rate)
  • History of trauma or abuse
  • LGBTQ+ youth — significantly elevated risk
Attempt History
#1
Biggest risk factor
Completion Rate
♂
Males complete at higher rates
Protective Factors
  • Strong social support network
  • Religious or spiritual beliefs against suicide
  • Children or dependents at home
  • Engaged in treatment
  • Sense of purpose and future plans
  • Restricted access to lethal means
💬
Ask Directly — It's Safe"Are you thinking about killing yourself?" Direct questioning does NOT increase suicide risk — research proves it. It opens the door for help.
The 4 Assessment Points
PLANIs there a specific method, time, and place?
MEANSDo they have access to that method? (gun, pills, etc.)
INTENTDo they actually intend to carry it out?
TIMELINEIs there a specific time they're planning?
📊
Risk Level Guides ActionThe more specific the plan + means + intent + timeline → the higher the immediate risk → the more intensive the intervention required.
Outpatient Interventions
📋
Safety PLAN — Not ContractOutdated "no-harm contracts" are ineffective. A safety plan includes: warning signs to watch for, coping strategies, contacts to call (988), places to go, means restriction steps.
  • Involve trusted family members in the safety plan
  • Schedule frequent follow-up appointments
  • Means restriction counseling — remove firearms, lock medications
  • Provide 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline number
  • Document everything thoroughly
🚨
1:1 Constant Observation — Never Leave AloneA suicidal inpatient requires continuous, uninterrupted 1:1 observation. Not "checking every 15 minutes" — constant, visual observation at all times.
Environment — Remove Immediately
🔪 Sharp objects
👔 Belts & ties
👟 Shoelaces
💊 All medications
🪢 Cords & strings
🛍️ Plastic bags
🔌 Electronics cords
🧥 Drawstrings
📦
Search Belongings on AdmissionThis is standard of care — not optional, not requiring a separate order. Done immediately on admission for all suicidal patients.
NCLEX Priority Rules
SAFETY
always beats
AUTONOMY
  • Patient refuses search → search anyway — safety overrides
  • Patient refuses medication → address safety first, then rights
  • Patient says they're "fine now" → increased vigilance, not decreased
  • Sudden calm in depressed patient → RED FLAG, not reassurance
Verbal Warning Signs
  • "I wish I were dead"
  • "Everyone would be better off without me"
  • "I can't take it anymore"
  • "I won't be a problem much longer"
  • "What's the point of anything?"
  • Asking about lethal doses of medications
  • "I just want it all to stop"
Behavioral Warning Signs
  • Sudden calm after severe depression — may have decided to act
  • Giving away prized possessions
  • Saying unusual goodbyes
  • Getting affairs in order (will, finances)
  • Stockpiling medications
  • Researching methods
  • Withdrawing from loved ones
⚠️
Sudden Calmness = RED FLAGA severely depressed patient who suddenly seems peaceful and content may have made a decision to act on suicide. This is NOT improvement — assess immediately and increase observation level.
Family Education Points
  • Take ALL threats seriously — "they're just saying it" is dangerous thinking
  • Never keep secrets — if patient asks them not to tell anyone, they MUST tell
  • Remove all weapons from home — especially firearms
  • Lock or remove medications — dispense one dose at a time if possible
  • Know the warning signs and what to do
  • Save 988 in their phone (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
  • Know the closest ER with psychiatric services
  • Don't leave the person alone if in immediate crisis
📞
988 Suicide & Crisis LifelineAvailable 24/7. Call or text 988. Available in English and Spanish. Provide this number to both patient and family members as part of the safety plan.
Pharmacology

Psychiatric
Medications

Antidepressants, mood stabilizers, and lithium. Know your drug classes, critical safety points, and patient teaching.

SSRIs & SNRIs
First-Line Antidepressants
SSRI ExamplesFluoxetine, Sertraline, Escitalopram
SNRI ExamplesVenlafaxine, Duloxetine
Mechanism (SSRI)↑ Serotonin
Mechanism (SNRI)↑ Serotonin + Norepinephrine
Therapeutic Onset2–4 weeks
Common Side EffectsNausea, sexual dysfunction, insomnia
⬛
Black Box WarningIncreased suicidal ideation in patients under 25 years old — especially in the first 1–2 weeks of treatment. Monitor closely at initiation and with dose changes.
⚠️
Serotonin SyndromeSigns: Hyperthermia, agitation, muscle clonus, tachycardia, diaphoresis. Risk ↑ when combined with MAOIs, triptans, or other serotonergic drugs.
MAOIs — Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors
Most Dangerous Antidepressant Class
ExamplesPhenelzine (Nardil), Tranylcypromine
UseTreatment-resistant depression
MechanismBlock MAO → ↑ serotonin, NE, dopamine
🚨
Hypertensive CrisisMAOI + Tyramine-rich foods → blocked tyramine breakdown → massive NE release → BP ↑↑↑ → stroke, death. Avoid tyramine foods strictly.
🚨
MAOI + SSRI = Serotonin SyndromeFatal combination. Requires 2-week washout period between drugs.
Foods to AVOID (Tyramine)

🥩 Cured/smoked meats
🍷 Red wine & beer
🐟 Smoked salmon
🫙 Fermented foods
🫘 Fava beans
🫒 Pickled items
🍺 Tap beer
✅
Safe FoodsFresh meats, fresh fish, fresh fruit, fresh vegetables, fresh dairy products — nothing aged, cured, fermented, or smoked.
TCAs — Tricyclic Antidepressants
Older Class — Used When Others Fail
ExamplesAmitriptyline, Nortriptyline, Imipramine
UsesDepression, neuropathic pain, migraines
💀
Overdose RiskVery narrow therapeutic window. Overdose causes fatal cardiac arrhythmias (QT prolongation). Never prescribe large amounts to suicidal patients.
⚠️
Anticholinergic Side EffectsDry mouth · Constipation · Urinary retention · Blurred vision · Confusion. Use "ABCDE" to remember: Anticholinergic effects, Blurred vision, Constipation, Dry mouth, Elevated HR.
🧍
Orthostatic HypotensionMajor fall risk — especially in elderly. Teach patient to rise slowly, dangle legs before standing.
Lithium Carbonate ⭐
Gold Standard Mood Stabilizer — Know Everything
Primary UseControls mania; prevents suicide
Also UsedBipolar maintenance, depression augmentation
Labs Draw Timing12 hours after last dose (trough)
Also MonitorKidney function, Thyroid function
Sub-therapeutic<0.4
✅ Therapeutic0.4–1.4 mEq/L
⚠️1.4–1.5
🚨 TOXIC≥1.5 mEq/L
⚠️
Early Toxicity (1.5–2.0)Nausea · Vomiting · Diarrhea · Fine hand tremor · Polyuria · Mild drowsiness
🚨

Critical Patient Teaching
  • Consistent sodium — low Na → kidneys retain Li → toxicity
  • Stay hydrated — dehydration concentrates lithium → toxicity
  • Avoid diuretics — especially thiazides → ↑ lithium retention
  • Avoid NSAIDs — ibuprofen ↑ lithium levels dangerously
  • Take with food to reduce GI upset
  • Never skip doses — blood levels must stay stable
  • Report N/V/tremor/confusion immediately
  • Do NOT go on a low-sodium diet without provider guidance

Treatment

ECT & Psychotherapy

Electroconvulsive therapy nursing care plus CBT and other therapeutic modalities. The nursing role for ECT is very testable.

⚡ ECT — Overview
UsesSevere depression, suicidal patients, mania, treatment-resistant
Contraindication↑ Intracranial pressure
MechanismControlled seizure → resets neurotransmitter circuits
SessionsUsually 6–12 treatments, 3×/week
PRE-ECT Nursing Care▼
  • NPO for 8 hours — aspiration risk during procedure
  • Obtain written informed consent
  • Baseline vital signs documented
  • Remove dentures, hairpins, jewelry
  • Empty bladder before procedure
  • Pre-medications as ordered: anesthetic, muscle relaxant, atropine
DURING ECT Nursing Care▼
  • Administer oxygen
  • Muscle relaxant (succinylcholine) to prevent injury during seizure
  • Anesthesia provider present throughout
  • Continuous vital sign monitoring
  • Bite block placed before procedure
POST-ECT Nursing Care ⭐▼
  • Vital signs q15 minutes until stable
  • Side-lying position — prevents aspiration
  • Reorient the patient — confusion is EXPECTED and normal
  • Inform patient that short-term memory loss is normal and usually temporary
  • Headache is common — treat symptomatically
  • Monitor for prolonged confusion or seizure activity (unexpected)
✅
Post-ECT confusion = ExpectedWhen patient asks "Where am I?" after ECT, the nurse reorients them calmly. This is NOT a crisis — it's the expected effect of the induced seizure.
🧠 CBT — Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
🎯
PurposeIdentify and change negative, distorted thinking patterns. The cycle: Thoughts → Feelings → Behaviors. Change the thought, change the feeling.
Best ForDepression, anxiety, PTSD, OCD, eating disorders
Evidence BaseEqual to medications for mild-moderate depression
Nursing Role
  • Teach thought stopping — interrupt automatic negative thoughts
  • Help patient reframe thinking — identify cognitive distortions
  • Reinforce that thoughts are not facts
  • Support behavioral activation — scheduling positive activities

Q1Priority / First Action
A nurse is caring for a 34-year-old client admitted with major depressive disorder. The client states, "Things have just been so hard lately." What is the nurse's PRIORITY action?
  • AAdminister prescribed antidepressant medication
  • BAsk the client directly if they are thinking about suicide
  • CEncourage the client to engage in group therapy activities
  • DNotify the physician of the client's statement
RationaleB is correct. The NCLEX priority is always to assess suicide risk first. When a depressed patient signals distress, ask directly: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" Research confirms direct questioning does NOT increase suicide risk — it opens the door to intervention. Medications and therapy are important but secondary to immediate safety assessment.
Q2Select All That Apply
A nurse is assessing a client with major depressive disorder. Which findings are consistent with this diagnosis? Select all that apply.
  • ✓Client reports sleeping 12–14 hours daily and still feels exhausted
  • ✓Client has lost 8 lbs in 3 weeks without dieting
  • ✓Client states "I don't see the point in anything anymore"
  • ✓Client reports feeling invincible and needs only 3 hours of sleep
  • ✓Client cannot concentrate enough to read a page
  • ✓Client exhibits psychomotor retardation on exam
RationaleA, B, C, E, F are correct. Hypersomnia, significant weight loss, hopelessness/anhedonia ("don't see the point"), poor concentration, and psychomotor retardation are all DSM-5 depressive criteria.

D is WRONG — feeling invincible + needing only 3 hours of sleep describes mania, not depression. These are opposite findings.
Q3Appropriate Response
A manic inpatient makes a sexually inappropriate comment toward the nurse. Which response is MOST appropriate?
  • AIgnore the comment to avoid escalating the patient
  • B"That behavior is inappropriate. Let's refocus on your care."
  • CLecture the patient extensively about appropriate boundaries
  • DLeave the room immediately without addressing the comment
RationaleB is correct. Set clear, calm, firm limits: "That behavior is inappropriate." Direct, non-argumentative, and immediate.

A — Ignoring never sets limits. C — Lengthy lectures escalate manic agitation. D — Leaving without responding provides no therapeutic feedback.
Q4Expected vs Unexpected
A client on lithium has a serum level of 1.6 mEq/L and reports nausea with a coarse hand tremor noted on assessment. Which best describes this finding?
  • AExpected therapeutic response to lithium
  • BUnexpected finding — the level is within normal range
  • CUnexpected finding indicating lithium toxicity — notify provider immediately
  • DExpected — tremor is always normal at any lithium level
RationaleC is correct. Therapeutic range = 0.4–1.4 mEq/L. A level of 1.6 is TOXIC. Coarse tremor (not fine tremor) + GI symptoms at this level = lithium toxicity. This is UNEXPECTED and requires immediate provider notification, holding the next dose, and close monitoring. Fine tremor can be a normal side effect at therapeutic levels, but coarse tremor + 1.6 = toxicity.
Q5Select All That Apply
A nurse teaches a client prescribed phenelzine (Nardil, MAOI) which foods to avoid. Which should the client AVOID? Select all that apply.
  • ✓Aged cheddar cheese
  • ✓Fresh grilled chicken breast
  • ✓Pepperoni and salami
  • ✓Red wine and beer
  • ✓Fresh apple
  • ✓Smoked salmon
RationaleA, C, D, F are correct — aged cheese, cured meats, alcohol, and smoked fish are high in tyramine. MAOIs block the breakdown of tyramine → massive NE release → hypertensive crisis.

B (fresh chicken) and E (fresh apple) are SAFE — they are not fermented, aged, cured, or smoked.
Q6Priority Action — Admission
A nurse is admitting a client following a suicide attempt today. The client is now calm and cooperative. Which action should the nurse perform FIRST?
  • AOrient the client to the unit rules and daily schedule
  • BAssign a roommate to provide social support
  • CSearch all personal belongings for sharp objects and medications
  • DAsk the client their preferred room temperature and meal choices
RationaleC is correct. The FIRST action when admitting any suicidal patient is environmental safety — search belongings for sharps, belts, medications, cords, and anything potentially lethal. This is standard of care, not optional, and does not require a separate physician order. Orientation, social assignments, and comfort are all appropriate later steps — safety comes first.
Q7Select All That Apply — Interventions
A nurse cares for a client in acute mania on an inpatient unit. Which nursing interventions are appropriate? Select all that apply.
  • ✓Provide a quiet, low-stimulation environment
  • ✓Offer high-calorie portable finger foods throughout the day
  • ✓Place the client near the nurses' station for extra stimulation
  • ✓Argue with the client to correct their grandiose thinking
  • ✓Monitor daily weight to assess nutritional status
  • ✓Set clear, consistent limits on inappropriate behavior
RationaleA, B, E, F are correct.
✅ Low stimulus reduces agitation · ✅ Finger foods maintain nutrition while mobile · ✅ Daily weights detect nutritional decline · ✅ Limits are always set for inappropriate behavior

C is WRONG — nurses' station = high stimulation = worsens mania. Use a quiet private room.
D is WRONG — arguing with manic patients escalates agitation. Never argue.
Q8Expected vs Unexpected
Post-ECT, a client is confused and asks "Where am I?" Which nursing interpretation is CORRECT?
  • AUnexpected — call a rapid response immediately
  • BExpected — post-ECT confusion is normal; reorient the client calmly
  • CUnexpected — the client should be alert and oriented immediately after ECT
  • DThis means the ECT was ineffective and must be repeated
RationaleB is correct. Post-ECT confusion and disorientation are EXPECTED. The nurse positions the patient on their side (prevents aspiration), monitors VS q15 min, and reorients the patient calmly as they wake. Memory loss and confusion are normal effects of the controlled seizure and typically resolve within minutes to an hour. A rapid response is not needed for expected post-procedure findings.
Q9Appropriate Response
At a 3-week postpartum visit, a mother says: "I feel so sad all the time. I cry for no reason and feel like I'm failing as a mother." Which response is MOST appropriate?
  • A"These baby blues are completely normal — they'll go away in a day or two."
  • B"Ask your partner to take the baby more so you can rest."
  • C"What you're describing sounds like more than baby blues. How long has this been happening, and have you had thoughts of harming yourself or the baby?"
  • D"This is very common and will get better. Just sleep when the baby sleeps."
RationaleC is correct. Baby blues resolve within 2 weeks — this patient is 3 weeks postpartum. These persistent symptoms indicate postpartum depression, which requires clinical assessment and treatment. The nurse must assess duration, severity, and critically — ask about thoughts of self-harm or harm to the baby (postpartum psychosis can develop).

A and D minimize symptoms incorrectly. B gives practical advice but does not address the clinical concern at all.
Q10Patient Teaching — SATA
A nurse teaches a client starting lithium carbonate. Which statements reflect accurate teaching? Select all that apply.
  • ✓"Do not suddenly decrease your salt intake while on lithium."
  • ✓"You may take ibuprofen freely for pain while on lithium."
  • ✓"Stay well hydrated — dehydration can cause dangerous lithium levels."
  • ✓"Blood levels are drawn 12 hours after your last dose."
  • ✓"Report nausea, vomiting, or tremor to your provider right away."
  • ✓"Avoid diuretics unless specifically prescribed by your doctor."
RationaleA, C, D, E, F are correct.
✅ Consistent Na = prevents lithium retention · ✅ Hydration prevents toxic concentration · ✅ 12-hr trough is most accurate · ✅ Early toxicity signs must be reported · ✅ Diuretics ↑ lithium retention → toxicity

B is WRONG. NSAIDs (including ibuprofen) decrease lithium clearance, raising blood levels dangerously. Use acetaminophen instead.
Q11Safety vs Autonomy
A suicidal inpatient refuses to have their belongings searched on admission. What should the nurse do?
  • ARespect the refusal and do not search belongings
  • BSearch belongings anyway — patient safety supersedes autonomy here
  • CCall the physician before making any decision
  • DAllow the client to keep belongings and monitor closely
RationaleB is correct. Core NCLEX principle: SAFETY > AUTONOMY when there is imminent risk to life. Searching belongings on psychiatric admission is standard of care — not optional and not subject to patient refusal when suicidality is involved. The right to refuse doesn't extend to creating lethal risk in a supervised inpatient setting. No separate physician order is needed for this standard admission safety check.
Q12Recognize the Emergency
A bipolar patient is pacing frantically, shouting at people who aren't there, expressing belief they are a world prophet, and appears terrified. How should the nurse classify this?
  • AHypomania — mildly elevated, needs redirection
  • BAcute mania — needs medication adjustment
  • CDelirious mania — psychiatric emergency requiring immediate intervention
  • DSchizophrenic episode — unrelated to bipolar disorder
RationaleC is correct. The triad of hallucinations + delusions + extreme agitation/panic = Stage 3 (Delirious) Mania — psychiatric emergency.

Hypomania (A) is mild and functional — no psychotic features. Acute mania (B) does not typically include hallucinations. Delirious mania = psychotic features + extreme agitation + panic together. This patient needs immediate safety intervention, emergency medications, and psychiatric team involvement.

  • 3 stages: Hypomania → Acute → Delirious (emergency)
  • Delirious = hallucinations + delusions + agitation
  • Low-stimulus environment — #1 intervention
  • Finger foods — never full meal trays
  • Set limits — never ignore inappropriate behavior
  • Never argue — stay calm, short sentences
  • Daily weights to monitor nutrition

🚨 Suicide

  • Previous attempt = #1 risk factor
  • Assess: Plan → Means → Intent → Timeline
  • Ask directly: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?"
  • Safety PLAN (not contract)
  • 1:1 observation — never leave alone
  • Search belongings — remove all sharps, strings, meds
  • Safety always > Autonomy
  • Sudden calm = RED FLAG

💊 Medications

  • Lithium therapeutic: 0.4–1.4 mEq/L

  • MAOI + tyramine → hypertensive crisis
  • MAOI + SSRI → serotonin syndrome
  • Lamotrigine → Stevens-Johnson rash (stop immediately)
