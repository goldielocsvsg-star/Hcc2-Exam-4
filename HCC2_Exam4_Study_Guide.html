Topic 1
Anxiety & Panic Disorders
Panic disorder, PTSD, and nursing interventions. Peak in 10 minutes — so should your knowledge.
📊 ~8 exam questions

🎯 The Big Picture

😰
Definition
What is Anxiety?

A subjectively distressful experience activated by the perception of threat. Has both psychological AND physiological expression. Distinct from normal anxiety when it interferes with daily function.

⚡
Panic Attack
Peak in 10 min

4+ symptoms = panic attack. Abrupt onset. Peaks within 10 minutes. Patients often go to the ED thinking it's a heart attack. Know why!

💥
PTSD
Re-experiencing Trauma

Follows exposure to extreme trauma. Characterized by flashbacks, avoidance, hyperarousal, and detachment. Higher incidence with previous history of abuse.

🧠 Mnemonic — Panic Attack Symptoms
CHEST PAIN + 3 More
C
Chest pain + palpitations, pounding heart
H
Hot flushes/chills + sweating, trembling
E
Environment feels unreal (derealization, depersonalization)
S
Smothering/SOB sensation + choking feeling
T
Terror of dying / losing control + paresthesias, nausea

Remember: 4+ of these symptoms = panic attack diagnosis

🩺 Nursing Interventions — Panic Attack

⚠️ DURING Acute Panic Attack (Priority Actions)
  • Stay with the client — never leave them alone
  • Minimize stimuli — quiet room, dim lights, calm voice
  • Do NOT try to teach — client CANNOT learn new info during attack
  • Remain calm yourself — your anxiety is contagious
✅ Long-Term Goals (After Acute Phase)
  • Recognize early symptoms and intervene BEFORE reaching panic stage
  • Develop balanced life; teach relaxation/breathing techniques
  • Postpone health teaching until acute anxiety has subsided
  • CBT, prolonged exposure, mindfulness — all evidence-based

💙 PTSD Deep Dive

👁️
PTSD Symptoms
Know ALL 4 clusters
  • Re-experiencing: Flashbacks, hallucinations, illusions, nightmares
  • Avoidance: Avoiding people/places, emotional numbness
  • Hyperarousal: Irritability, anger, trouble concentrating, insomnia
  • Detachment: Interpersonal problems, can't show feelings
🏥
PTSD Nursing Priorities
Safety First
  • Always assess suicide risk — they won't always say it directly
  • Assess comorbid substance abuse (common!)
  • Focus client on the present — use structured interview
  • Validate and acknowledge feelings (therapeutic comm.)
  • Help identify relaxation techniques
📖 Story Anchor — PTSD vs Panic: Remember the Difference

Pat (Panic Disorder) has random episodes that come out of nowhere — no clear trigger, peaks in 10 min, goes to the ED thinking heart attack.

Pam (PTSD) was assaulted 2 years ago. She has nightmares, avoids her neighborhood, can't show emotion to her kids, and snaps at everything. Her symptoms have a CAUSE — a specific traumatic event.

Alprazolam (Xanax), Lorazepam (Ativan)

🧪 Evidence-Based Treatments for PTSD

👁️
EMDR

Eye Movement Desensitization & Reprocessing — patient processes trauma while tracking side-to-side movements. Evidence-based.

🧩
CBT / CPT

Cognitive Behavioral / Processing Therapy — identify and challenge distorted trauma-related thoughts.

📢
Prolonged Exposure

Gradually confront trauma-related fears. Also used for phobias. Helps break avoidance cycle.

Topic 2
Substance Use & Addiction
Withdrawal timelines, dangerous substances, treatment meds, and nursing in SUD care.
📊 ~12 exam questions
🧠 Mnemonic — Most Dangerous Withdrawals
"A-B-O = Always Be On alert"
A
Alcohol — MOST DANGEROUS. Can cause seizures & death. NEVER cold turkey.
B
Benzos — With high doses/extended use: seizures possible.
O
Opioids — Uncomfortable, rarely fatal. (N/V, bone pain, gooseflesh)

Stimulant withdrawal = lethargy, depression, suicidal thoughts — NOT seizures

🍺 Alcohol Withdrawal — Timeline You MUST Know

Time After Last DrinkSymptomsNursing Action
6–12 hrsShakes, tremors, nausea, anxiety, diaphoresisBaseline vitals, CIWA score, notify MD
12–24 hrsHallucinations (visual, auditory, tactile)Administer benzos per protocol, seizure precautions
24–48 hrsPEAK — tonic-clonic seizuresDiazepam, safe environment, airway management
2–3 days⚠️ Delirium Tremens (DTs) — MEDICAL EMERGENCYICU-level care, antipsychotics, fluids
🚨 Delirium Tremens — MEDICAL EMERGENCY

Occurs 2–3 days after cessation. Lasts 2–3 days. Can be fatal. Symptoms: hallucinations, severe HTN, tachypnea, severe disorientation, cardiac dysrhythmias, delirium. Symptoms may progress to death.

📖 Story Anchor — CIWA Scale

Think of CIWA as the alcohol withdrawal "thermometer." It measures 10 symptoms (tremors, sweating, anxiety, headache, agitation, etc.) scored 0–7 each. A high score = more benzos needed. CIWA guides treatment — it does NOT diagnose AUD or predict relapse. Classic NCLEX trap: CIWA's purpose is to monitor severity and guide benzo administration.

💉 Opioid Overdose & Treatment

🚑
Overdose Signs
The "3 P's of Opioid OD"
  • Pinpoint pupils (miosis)
  • Plummeting respirations (<10/min)
  • Profound sedation / unconsciousness
Also: euphoria, slurred speech, bradycardia, hypotension, flushed skin
💉
Emergency Tx
Naloxone (Narcan) Protocol
  • Administer Naloxone — opioid antagonist, reverses OD
  • Give O2 if RR <10/min or hypoxia present
  • Have suction available (risk of vomiting on reversal)
  • Monitor vitals and LOC continuously
  • Do NOT leave patient until fully responsive

🔄 Opioid Long-Term Treatment

Dolophine
Opioid AgonistSubstitution Tx

Goal:
Prevents withdrawal, reduces cravings, enables functioning

Key:
Agonist-antagonist combo — naloxone deters IV misuse

👩‍⚕️ SUD in Nurses — Know This!

⚠️ Impaired Nurse — What To Watch For
  • Frequent tardiness, mood swings, unusual fatigue
  • Discrepancies in controlled substance counts
  • Volunteers to administer pain medications frequently
  • Defensive when questioned about behavior
✅ Correct Response Steps
  • 1️⃣ Report concern to management (NOT law enforcement first, NOT confront publicly)
  • 2️⃣ Conduct formal evaluation and drug screening
  • 3️⃣ If diagnosed with SUD: Peer assistance / alternative-to-discipline program — NOT license revocation

🤝 Twelve-Step Programs (AA/NA)

  • Abstinence is necessary for recovery
  • A higher power is needed to assist in recovery
  • You are not responsible for the disease, but ARE responsible for recovery
  • Others cannot be blamed; must acknowledge own feelings/problems
  • Non-religious alternatives exist (SMART Recovery, etc.)

💊 Antidepressants — Know Your Classes

SSRIs
Fluoxetine (Prozac), Sertraline, Paroxetine, Escitalopram
First-LineNCLEX ★★
MOA:
Block reuptake of serotonin → more serotonin available
Takes:
2–4 weeks for full therapeutic effect
⚠️ Risk:
Serotonin syndrome (agitation, hyperthermia, tachycardia, tremors) — especially if combined with MAOIs or tramadol
Teach:
Don't stop abruptly. Watch for suicidal ideation in first weeks (BLACK BOX in teens/young adults)
SNRIs
Venlafaxine (Effexor), Duloxetine (Cymbalta)
SNRIDepression + Anxiety + Pain
MOA:
Block reuptake of serotonin AND norepinephrine
Bonus:
Good for pain disorders (fibromyalgia, neuropathy)
Monitor:
BP (norepinephrine = ↑ BP), serotonin syndrome
TCAs
Amitriptyline, Imipramine, Nortriptyline
TricyclicOverdose Risk
⚠️ NCLEX:
LETHAL in overdose — do NOT give full supply to suicidal patients
SE:
Anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, blurred vision)
Use:
Depression, chronic pain, neuropathy
MAOIs
Phenelzine (Nardil), Tranylcypromine
Dangerous Diet RestrictionsNCLEX ★★
⚠️ BIG:
Tyramine-rich food = Hypertensive Crisis! No aged cheeses, red wine, cured meats, fermented foods
Also:
Do NOT combine with SSRIs → serotonin syndrome
Use:
Treatment-resistant depression (rarely first-line)
🚨 Serotonin Syndrome — Emergency!

Caused by too much serotonergic activity. Triad: Mental status changes + Neuromuscular abnormalities (tremors, clonus) + Autonomic instability (hyperthermia, tachycardia, diaphoresis). Most common when SSRIs combined with MAOIs, tramadol, or linezolid. Treat: stop the drug, supportive care, cyproheptadine.

⚖️
Bipolar Types
I vs II
  • Bipolar I: Full manic episodes (≥7 days or hospitalization needed). May have depression.
  • Bipolar II: Hypomanic episodes (less severe) + depressive episodes. No full mania.
  • Cyclothymia: Milder chronic mood fluctuations ≥2 years
⚠️ Nursing Priority in Mania

Safety + nutrition + sleep. Manic patients don't eat/sleep. Provide finger foods, limit stimulation, set clear limits.

🚨 NCLEX: Suicide Assessment Priorities
  • Always ask directly about suicidal ideation — asking does NOT plant the idea
  • Assess for a plan, means, and timeline — specific plan = high risk
  • Remove means — sharps, medications, belts from environment
  • 1:1 supervision (one-to-one observation) for high-risk patients
  • Clients who seem suddenly calm/improved after being severely depressed = DANGER — they may have made a decision
  • ECT (Electroconvulsive Therapy) — used for severe, treatment-resistant depression & immediate suicide risk
  • Used for: Severe treatment-resistant depression, active suicidal risk, during pregnancy (safer than meds)
  • Pre-procedure: NPO, remove jewelry, dentures, informed consent, baseline vitals
  • Post-procedure: Monitor airway, vitals, orientation; short-term memory loss is common and expected
  • Therapeutic communication: Reassure that confusion and memory loss are temporary side effects
  • Contraindicated with: increased ICP, recent MI, space-occupying brain lesions
Topic 4
Feeding & Eating Disorders
Anorexia, Bulimia, and Binge Eating Disorder — critical to distinguish, critical to treat safely.
📊 Included in Mood/Mental Health questions
🧠 Mnemonic — Tell Them Apart Fast
"A = Away from food. B = Binge then Banish. BED = Binge, no Exit"
AN
Anorexia Nervosa — Restrict, restrict, restrict. BMI <17.5. Weight way below normal.
BN
Bulimia Nervosa — Binge THEN purge/laxatives. Normal or near-normal BMI. Dental erosion clue!
BED
Binge Eating Disorder — Binge, feel guilty, NO compensatory behaviors. Obesity risk.

🚨 Refeeding Syndrome — Anorexia Danger!

Reintroducing nutrition too quickly in severely malnourished patients causes dangerous electrolyte shifts (especially hypophosphatemia). Can cause cardiac failure, seizures, respiratory failure. Reintroduce nutrients SLOWLY with close monitoring. This is why medical stabilization comes BEFORE psychotherapy.

⚠️ Bupropion is CONTRAINDICATED in Bulimia and Anorexia

Due to increased seizure risk in eating disorder patients (electrolyte imbalances lower seizure threshold). This is a classic NCLEX trap — know it!

👩‍⚕️ Nursing Approach

🏥
Acute Priority
  • Stabilize vitals first — dysrhythmias, BP, electrolytes
  • Monitor ECG for QT prolongation (hypokalemia risk)
  • Assess suicide risk (especially bulimia)
  • Start nutrition slowly (refeeding syndrome prevention)
💬
Long-Term Care
  • Psychosocial intervention, family therapy, CBT
  • Weight monitoring with empathy — not punitive
  • Teach healthy coping behaviors
  • Education on fluid/electrolyte risks
  • NON-JUDGMENTAL approach always
Topic 5
Psychosis & Schizophrenia
Lost in reality. Nursing goal: safety, trust, and reality orientation. 20 questions — know your antipsychotics.
📊 ~20 exam questions
🧠 Mnemonic — Positive vs Negative Symptoms
"Positive = Present (stuff added). Negative = Not there (stuff removed)."
➕
Positive (added to behavior): Hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech/behavior, catatonia
➖
Negative (taken away): Flat affect, alogia (can't speak), avolition (no motivation), anhedonia, social withdrawal

Typical antipsychotics treat positive symptoms well. Atypical antipsychotics treat BOTH positive AND negative symptoms.

🩺 Nursing Care of Psychotic Patient

🚨 Priority: Safety
  • Do NOT argue with delusions — don't reinforce, but don't dispute
  • Focus on feelings, not the content of delusions
  • Assess for command hallucinations — "Are you hearing voices telling you to hurt yourself/others?"
  • Safe environment, appropriate room assignment
✅ Therapeutic Communication in Psychosis
  • Short, simple, concrete statements
  • Calm, non-threatening approach
  • Don't whisper or laugh near client (paranoia)
  • Orient to reality gently — "I understand you believe that, but I don't see/hear that"
  • Build trust slowly — consistency is key

💊 Antipsychotic Medications

💊
1st Generation (Typical)
Haloperidol, Chlorpromazine
  • Treat positive symptoms only
  • High EPS risk (extrapyramidal symptoms)
  • Used for acute agitation (Haldol IM)
  • Older drugs, more side effects
💊
2nd Generation (Atypical)
Clozapine, Risperidone, Olanzapine, Quetiapine
  • Treat BOTH positive AND negative symptoms
  • Lower EPS risk than typical
  • BUT: metabolic syndrome, weight gain, diabetes risk
  • Clozapine = agranulocytosis risk → weekly WBC monitoring required

⚠️ Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS) — MAJOR NCLEX Topic

EPS TypeWhat It Looks LikeOnsetTreatment
Acute DystoniaSudden muscle spasms, torticollis, oculogyric crisis (eyes stuck up)Hours to daysBenadryl (diphenhydramine) IM/IV or Cogentin (benztropine)
AkathisiaExtreme restlessness, can't stop moving/pacingDays to weeksBeta-blockers, benzodiazepines, lower dose
PseudoparkinsonismTremor, rigidity, shuffling gait, masklike faceWeeksAnticholinergics (Cogentin)
Tardive Dyskinesia (TD)Repetitive involuntary movements — lip smacking, tongue protrusion, facial grimacingMonths to years⚠️ IRREVERSIBLE. Stop drug if possible. Switch to atypical.
🔥 Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) — Life-Threatening Emergency

Rare but deadly complication of antipsychotics. FEVER + MUSCLE RIGIDITY + Altered LOC + Autonomic instability (unstable BP, tachycardia, diaphoresis). Treatment: Stop antipsychotic IMMEDIATELY, cooling measures, dantrolene, bromocriptine. Often confused with serotonin syndrome — key difference: NMS has prominent rigidity; no clonus.

🧠 Mnemonic — NMS vs Serotonin Syndrome
"Serotonin = Shaky. NMS = Stiff."
SS
Serotonin Syndrome: Shaky (clonus/tremors), SSRIs/MAOIs, starts fast (hours)
NMS
NMS: Stiff (severe "lead-pipe" rigidity), antipsychotics, starts slower (days)

💊 Clozapine Special Monitoring

⚠️ Clozapine (Clozaril) — The Most Dangerous Antipsychotic
  • Used for treatment-resistant schizophrenia
  • Risk of agranulocytosis — life-threatening low WBC
  • Mandatory weekly WBC monitoring — must have normal WBC to get next prescription
  • Also risks: seizures (dose-related), myocarditis, extreme weight gain, hypersalivation
  • Classic NCLEX: patient on clozapine gets a sore throat and fever → hold med, check WBC immediately
