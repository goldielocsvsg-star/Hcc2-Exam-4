Slide 3 — Terms
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Mood – how a person feels
  • Affect - the observable response a person has to her or his feelings
  • Flat
  • Blunted
  • Inappropriate - example?
  • Labile
  • Restricted
  • Anhedonia - complete loss of interest in things that used to give you pleasure
📖 Textbook Expansion

Your notes make a big distinction between mood and affect. Mood is subjective and comes from the patient’s self-report. Affect is what the nurse can observe. This matters because nurses do not diagnose bipolar disorder or major depressive disorder, but nurses absolutely are expected to notice affective instability, changes in function, and danger signals that need immediate follow-up.

Mood regulation depends on coordinated function of the prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, limbic system, and key neurotransmitters such as serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine. When those systems become dysregulated, the patient may move downward into melancholy/depression or upward into hypomania/mania. As mood shifts farther from euthymia, functional status falls.

Nursing Priority / Safety

  • Reduced function matters just as much as sad mood. A patient who cannot eat, sleep, work, or perform ADLs is not “just having a bad week.”
  • Previous suicide attempt is one of the strongest red flags.
  • Chronic illness, substance use, and poor support systems can worsen depression and complicate treatment response.
  • Do not minimize postpartum symptoms or symptoms in older adults.

Exam Tips / NCLEX Traps

  • This is a trap: calling normal sadness “depression” without looking at duration and functional impairment.
  • They will test whether you know affect is what you observe, not what the patient reports.
  • If the stem includes hopelessness, anhedonia, worthlessness, impaired ADLs, or prior attempt, move your brain toward suicide assessment first.
🎯 How This Shows Up on Exams

The instructor is likely to connect the concept back to the module objectives by asking you to identify abnormal mood/psychomotor changes, assess support systems, recognize crisis, and use safety precautions. If a stem asks what the nurse should do first, the safest first move is usually to assess self-harm risk, violence risk, functional decline, and contributing factors rather than jumping straight to reassurance or teaching.

🧠 Quick Memory Tools

Mood = inside. Affect = outside. If you can see it, it is affect. If they tell you how they feel, that is mood. For consequences of mood disorders, think “LIFE”: Limited productivity, Interpersonal damage, Function drops, End-of-life risk by suicide.

Slides 6–11 + Textbook Notes

Depression: Symptoms, Developmental Patterns, and Postpartum Considerations

Slide 6–7 — Major Depression Symptoms
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • DSM Criteria - lasts 2 weeks minimum
  • has one of 1st 2 symptoms & 5 of others
  • Feelings of melancholy (Depressed mood) most of day, nearly Q D
  • Children & adolescents - irritable mood
  • ↓ interest & pleasure almost all activities – most Q D & most of day
  • Expression of hopelessness, feelings of worthlessness, ↓ self esteem, ↓ attentiveness to oneself & one’s tasks
  • ↓ energy, fatigue
  • ↓ ability to concentrate, indecisiveness
  • Excessive, recurrent thoughts of death, suicide
  • Significant weight loss/gain
  • Change sleep patterns – insomnia – early morning awakenings, hypersomnia
  • Psychomotor retardation/agitation – slowed physical movements, slow speech, ↓ interest grooming, slumped posture
📖 Textbook Expansion

The key pattern in depression is not just sadness. It is persistent mood disturbance + disturbed vegetative function + impaired function. The vegetative pieces are huge: sleep, appetite, energy, libido, and concentration. That is why exam questions often disguise depression as fatigue, chronic pain, sleep change, appetite change, or inability to keep up with work or ADLs.

Depression also has a neurobiologic piece. Low or dysregulated serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine contribute to changes in mood, concentration, sleep, motivation, reward processing, and energy. Clinically, that is why depressed patients may look slowed down, stop bathing, stop eating, or report that “nothing feels good anymore.”

Slide 10–11 — Developmental Implications + Postpartum Depression
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Childhood depression (e.g. crying, withdrawal)
  • Adolescence (e.g. irritability, insomnia, low self esteem, chronic pain with no physiological origin)
  • Senescence (assoc w/ aging) (their emotional pain may be overlooked)
  • Postpartum depression (“baby blues” versus depression?)
  • May last for a few weeks to several months
  • Associated with hormonal changes, tryptophan metabolism, or cell alterations
📖 Cross-Analysis Addition from Book Notes

The book adds that depression does not always look purely slowed down. Some patients show mixed features such as agitation, insomnia, or increased activation even while their mood remains persistently low. That is why the safer clinical pattern is to link symptoms back to persistent low mood, impaired function, and abnormal vegetative changes rather than expecting one stereotyped presentation.

Clinical Connection

  • A depressed adult may not say “I am depressed.” They may say “I can’t sleep,” “I can’t think,” “I don’t care anymore,” or “I’m so tired.”
  • An adolescent may show depression as irritability, not tearfulness.
  • An older adult may present with somatic complaints, low energy, poor concentration, or withdrawal and get overlooked because people assume it is “just aging.”
  • Postpartum depression is not the same as brief “baby blues.” Ongoing sadness, inability to function, impaired bonding, hopelessness, or thoughts of harm require urgent evaluation.

Nursing Priority / Safety

  • Any mention of death, worthlessness, hopelessness, or giving up = assess suicide risk right away.
  • Psychomotor retardation can look calm, but it may actually reflect severe depression with impaired self-care.
  • Psychomotor agitation is dangerous too because low mood + rising energy can increase suicide capability.
  • In postpartum patients, assess infant safety, maternal bonding, sleep deprivation, support system, and intrusive thoughts.
⭐ Exam Tips / NCLEX Traps
  • This is a trap: assuming depression always looks like crying. It can look like irritability, apathy, insomnia, indecisiveness, or body complaints.
  • They will try to test you on the 2-week minimum for major depressive symptoms.
  • They love the difference between normal postpartum adjustment and postpartum depression with persistent impairment.
  • When you see anhedonia + hopelessness + worthlessness, think severe depression and suicide screening.
🧠 Quick Memory Tools

Depression = SLOW + EMPTY. Slow speech, slow movement, sleep disruption, sad thoughts, and loss of enjoyment. For children/adolescents, remember: “sad may look mad.” In younger patients, irritability can be the clue.

🎯 How This Shows Up on Exams

You may get stems asking the nurse to identify which patient is at highest risk, which finding requires immediate follow-up, or which patient response suggests worsening depression. The high-yield cues are hopelessness, recurrent thoughts of death, severe sleep change, weight change, poor grooming, and inability to function. These map directly to the module objectives on behavioral assessment, coping, crisis recognition, and psychosocial education.

Assessment of Depression: What the Nurse Must Check First

Slide 8–9 — Assessment for Depression
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Self harm or harm to others - IMPORTANT
  • Determine other contributing factors
  • Psychosis
  • Drugs or alcohol
  • Medical conditions (e.g. cardiac disease, diabetes, irritable bowel, chronic liver, Alzheimer’s, AIDS, other chronic illnesses)
  • Medications -( e.g. CNS depressants, antihypertensives, oral contraceptives, antianxiety meds, psychostimulants, corticosteroids, Digoxin )
  • History of psychiatric conditions
  • History of depression and suicidal behavior
  • Support systems
  • Screening tools- Routine screening recommended for all adults
  • Over the past two weeks, have you ever felt down, depressed, or hopeless?
  • Have you ever felt little interest or pleasure in doing things?
  • Observe for other symptoms
📖 Textbook Expansion

Depression assessment is not just “Do you feel sad?” The nurse has to separate primary mood disorder from substance-related, medication-related, psychotic, or medical causes. That is why the slide specifically points you toward alcohol/drugs, chronic medical illness, and medication review. A patient on corticosteroids, for example, may have mood changes related to treatment. A patient with chronic illness may have depression that shows up as fatigue and poor adherence.

Your textbook also emphasizes that mood screening tools do not diagnose the disorder. They are helpful for detecting risk and tracking severity, but they do not replace assessment of affect, function, judgment, cognition, and violence potential.

Another key book note is to treat mental status almost like a sixth vital sign. Appearance, motor activity, speech, alertness, orientation, judgment, and reasoning should be reassessed over time, especially when the patient has worsening mood, poor coping, psychosis, or safety concerns.

Nursing Priority / Safety

  • If self-harm risk is even possible, do not move on casually to long-term teaching first.
  • A patient with low mood + psychosis needs a higher index of concern because judgment may be impaired.
  • Always ask directly about suicide. Direct questions do not put the idea into the patient’s head.
  • Support system assessment matters for both outpatient planning and relapse prevention.

Clinical Connection

  • You may see a medical-surgical patient with chronic illness who stops eating, stops engaging, and misses meds. That could be depression, not “noncompliance.”
  • An overwhelmed patient with vague somatic complaints and insomnia may screen positive when you ask the two simple depression questions.
  • A patient who says “I’m fine” but looks flat, withdrawn, and poorly groomed still needs further assessment.
⭐ Exam Tips / NCLEX Traps
  • This is a trap: choosing a comforting statement before performing a suicide assessment.
  • They love asking what additional information is needed. The best answers usually clarify plan, means, history, supports, and contributing conditions.
  • Routine adult screening is fair game. Know the two classic screening questions.

For depression assessment, think “SAD PMHS”: Suicide, Alcohol/drugs, Delusions/psychosis, Past history, Medical issues/meds, Help/supports, Screening questions.

Slides 12–16 + Bipolar Textbook Notes

Mania: Stages, Presentation, and Acute Nursing Care

Slides 12–13 — Symptoms of Bipolar Disorder + Manic States
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Symptoms of Bipolar Disorder
  • What are the symptoms of mania?
  • Stage I—Hypomania: Symptoms not severe, able to function
  • Stage II—Acute mania: Marked impairment in functioning of mood, cognition, perception, activity, & behavior; usually requires hospitalization
  • Stage III—Delirious mania: labile mood, despair to ecstasy, panic anxiety, auditory & visual hallucinations, delusions, incoherent, distractible, frenzied psychomotor activity
📖 Textbook Expansion

Mania is not just “happy” or “energized.” The core issue is a state of abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood with persistently increased energy and goal-directed activity. As mania escalates, insight drops, sleep decreases, judgment becomes impaired, and the patient may become reckless, sexually disinhibited, grandiose, or psychotic.

The textbook notes that early mania may look euphoric and exciting, but it often progresses into agitation, irritability, impulsivity, distractibility, psychosis, and eventually exhaustion. Severe mania is a psychiatric emergency because the patient may no longer accurately perceive reality and may engage in dangerous behavior.

Cross-analysis with the bipolar notes also adds relapse teaching: even during remission, the patient and family need teaching on medication adherence, sleep hygiene, early warning signs of mania, and the effect of alcohol/drugs/OTC products on stability. Families often notice decreased sleep, pressured speech, spending, or irritability before the patient does.

Slides 14–16 — Nursing Interventions in Acute Mania + Sample Question
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Do not overreact to client.
  • Set simple limits to inappropriate behavior.
  • Be kind, but firm (client may try to bend rules)
  • Monitor safety – delusions of grandeur
  • High calorie, high vitamin diet (frequent finger foods might be helpful - why?)
  • Non-competitive activities – can be very irritable
  • Teach client: to monitor self and have family assist – recognize mania; monitor sleep (may need frequent naps if cannot sleep well at night)
  • Maintain a low stimuli environment
  • Weigh client daily
  • Monitor vital signs every 1-2 hours throughout the day
  • Sample Question answer: Calmly tell the client that it is inappropriate to speak to any nurse that way.

Nursing Priority / Safety

  • In acute mania, physical needs and safety come first: food, fluids, rest, boundaries, protection from impulsive acts.
  • Low-stimulus environment is not just comfort care; it reduces escalation.
  • Daily weights matter because manic clients may burn calories constantly and neglect intake.
  • Frequent VS monitoring matters because agitation, dehydration, overactivity, and medication effects can destabilize the patient.

Exam Tips / NCLEX Traps

  • This is a trap: choosing “ignore the behavior” when a patient says something sexually inappropriate. Ignoring does not set limits.
  • They will test whether you know to be kind, neutral, and firm instead of shaming, debating, or overreacting.
  • Finger foods are used because the patient often will not sit long enough for a meal.
🧠 Quick Memory Tools

Think “MANIA = FAST”: Flight of ideas / food neglect, Agitation, Sleep decreased, Talkative / thoughts racing. For care, think “REST”: Reduce stimuli, Enforce limits, Safety first, Throw in finger foods.

🎯 How This Shows Up on Exams

Mania questions often ask what the nurse should do first when the patient is intrusive, inappropriate, not sleeping, or refusing meals. The best answers usually protect safety, preserve dignity, and meet physical needs. This directly ties to your objectives on behavioral intervention, anxiety reduction, abnormal mood recognition, crisis management, and nonthreatening care.

Antidepressants: What to Know for Nursing Safety and Hard Exam Questions

Slide 17 — Antidepressants
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) e.g. Paroxetine, Fluoxetine *serotonin syndrome risk
  • SNRIs (selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors)
  • Tricyclics (e.g. amitryptyline)
  • MAO inhibitors (e.g. phenalzine)
  • What is most dangerous potential side effect? (Hypertensive crisis caused by _______?)
  • Patient education?
  • Avoid concurrent use with SSRIs! May cause serotonin syndrome!
📖 Textbook Expansion

SSRIs are often first-line because they are generally better tolerated than older antidepressants. But the nurse has to teach patients that improvement is not immediate. Early benefit may begin in 2–3 weeks, but full effect often takes 4–8 weeks. That delay matters because some patients stop the medication too early, assume it is not working, or remain suicidal while waiting for mood improvement.

The older classes matter because they are high-yield on exams. TCAs are more dangerous in overdose. MAOIs are not first-line because of potentially fatal interactions with tyramine-containing foods and other serotonergic medications. Serotonin syndrome can occur with serotonergic excess and may begin abruptly with tachycardia, diaphoresis, shivering, dilated pupils, hyperreflexia, myoclonus, and hyperthermia.

Most Dangerous Things to Know

  • Serotonin syndrome: tachycardia, shivering, diaphoresis, dilated pupils, myoclonus, hyperreflexia, hyperthermia.
  • MAOI + tyramine = hypertensive crisis.
  • MAOI + SSRI = serotonin syndrome risk.
  • TCA overdose can be lethal.
  • Do not stop antidepressants abruptly because discontinuation symptoms can occur.

Patient Education

  • Take medication exactly as prescribed; do not stop abruptly.
  • Do not expect instant relief; improvement may take several weeks.
  • Report new/worsening suicidal thoughts, severe restlessness, fever, tremor, or unusual confusion.
  • MAOI patients need strict dietary and drug-interaction teaching.
  • St. John’s wort is not harmless just because it is herbal; it can interact with antidepressants.
A patient taking phenelzine says, “I had wine and aged cheese at dinner and now I have a pounding headache.” What is the safest interpretation? ▼
✓ Possible hypertensive crisis

This is a classic MAOI + tyramine danger pattern. The nurse should treat this as urgent, not as routine med teaching. This is one of those exam cues designed to see whether you immediately connect MAOI to tyramine restriction.

⭐ Exam Tips / NCLEX Traps
  • This is a trap: picking a vague side effect when the question is really testing the dangerous one. For MAOIs, think hypertensive crisis; for SSRIs, think serotonin syndrome.
  • They may ask which statement by the patient needs correction. Anything suggesting abrupt discontinuation or careless mixing of antidepressants should jump out.
  • They love stems where mood is still low but energy starts to return. That can be a suicide risk window when starting antidepressants.
🧠 Quick Memory Tools

MAOI = “M” for meat, mature cheese, and major BP risk. SSRI = serotonin. TCA = toxicity in overdose.

Lithium, Mood Stabilizers, and Acute Mania Med Safety

Slide 18 — Mood Stabilizing Drugs
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Lithium carbonate - controls mania, decreases incidence of suicide
  • Therapeutic blood level: 0.4 to 1.4 mEq/L
  • Toxic blood level: 1.5 mEq/L and above
  • Blood levels should be obtained in the morning, usually 12 hours after last dose
  • Antiepileptics
  • Second generation Antipsychotics
  • Atypical Antipsychotics - can help promote sleep and decrease anxiety and agitation. May also help with mood stabilization (example - quetiapine)
  • Anxiolytics (e.g. benzodiazepines)- can be useful with acute mania
  • Antidepressants - may be used during depressive phase
  • Antiseizure meds - e.g. lamotrigene
📖 Textbook Expansion

Lithium remains a classic mood stabilizer and is especially high-yield because it has a narrow therapeutic range. Your notes emphasize that kidney and thyroid function should be checked before and during therapy. It is lost with perspiration and affected by fluid/electrolyte status, so dehydration can push the patient into toxicity.

The textbook lists early lithium toxicity signs as diarrhea, vomiting, drowsiness, muscular weakness, and lack of coordination. More severe toxicity can progress to ataxia, blurred vision, tinnitus, marked neurologic change, and large amounts of dilute urine. This is not “watch and wait” territory. It requires immediate provider notification and usually holding the medication.

Cross-analysis with the bipolar notes adds that lithium is avoided in pregnancy/breastfeeding, blood levels are checked about 12 hours after the last dose, kidney and thyroid labs need ongoing follow-up, and second-generation antipsychotics require metabolic screening. Family teaching should also cover relapse signs, sleep changes, hydration, and adherence even when the patient feels well.

When to Hold / Call Provider Immediately

  • Lithium: diarrhea, vomiting, drowsiness, muscular weakness, lack of coordination, ataxia, blurred vision, tinnitus, marked neuro change.
  • Hold lithium and report if dehydration, major fluid loss, or toxicity is suspected.
  • Do not give lithium casually to pregnant or breastfeeding patients per your notes.
  • Any severe oversedation or respiratory/CNS depression with benzodiazepines needs prompt action.

Patient & Family Teaching

  • Maintain consistent fluid and electrolyte intake.
  • Do not suddenly change sodium intake without guidance.
  • Recognize early toxicity and report it right away.
  • Keep lab appointments. Lithium without lab follow-up is unsafe.
  • Family should help monitor sleep, irritability, spending, hyperactivity, and early relapse cues.

Li = likes liquids. If fluids fall, lithium can rise. For toxicity progression, remember “GI then neuro”: vomiting/diarrhea first, then coordination and neurologic problems.

Slides 19–24 + Textbook Notes

ECT, CBT, and Other Therapies for Depression and Mania

Slides 19–21 — ECT + Role of the Nurse
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Grand mal seizure (25 sec) induced by electrical current
  • Theory - ↑ availability of neurotransmitters
  • Usually QOD three X/week; 6 to 12 treatments
  • Indicated for depression & mania when medication is ineffective
  • Contraindications – Increased intracranial pressure (e.g. Brain tumor, CVA w/in 3 months)
  • Caution – severe heart disease, pulmonary disease, severe underlying hypertension, and heart failure
  • Pre-procedure: assess mood, anxiety, thought and communication patterns, baseline VS, describe procedure, short term memory loss - common side effect, NPO – 8 hours prior, informed consent, remove dentures, etc.
  • During: muscle paralyzing agent (usually succinylcholine), anesthesia, supplemental oxygen
  • Post-care: VS Q 15 min/1 hour, remain with client until fully awake, side lying, orient client to time, place, and situation
Slides 22–24 — CBT + Other Therapies
📊 Slide Content — Exactly as Presented
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy — based upon changing distorted thinking, mood & behavior
  • Goal to learn to identify & alter dysfunctional beliefs that predispose them to distort experiences
  • Highly structured & short-term, 12 to 16 weeks
  • Role of nurse with CBT: explain connection between thoughts and emotions and behavior; recognize thoughts; thought recording; imagery; teach thought stopping
  • Other therapies: Exercise, Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation, Vagus Nerve Stimulation, Deep Brain Stimulation, Light Therapy, St. John’s Wort
📖 Textbook Expansion

ECT is used when symptoms are severe, dangerous, or treatment-resistant. Nursing care is basically peri-anesthesia care plus mental status support. The nurse teaches before the procedure, protects airway and safety after the procedure, and reorients the patient as they wake. Short-term memory problems are common and expected.

CBT works by targeting maladaptive thought patterns and behaviors that feed depression. It is especially useful because it teaches the patient to notice automatic negative thoughts, challenge distortions, and replace them with more accurate thinking. That makes it highly testable in psych nursing because it connects directly to coping, self-control, and treatment adherence.

The book notes also strengthen this section by showing that outcomes are often best when psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy are combined. Interpersonal therapy is especially useful for grief issues and postpartum depression, and family-focused therapy supports communication, problem solving, and adherence.

Nursing Priority / Safety

  • ECT contraindication from the slide: increased intracranial pressure.
  • Post-ECT, protect airway, monitor VS, place side-lying, and reorient.
  • Do not forget informed consent and NPO teaching before ECT.
  • Do not present St. John’s wort as risk-free because it can interact with prescribed meds.

Clinical Connection

  • ECT may be chosen when the patient is severely depressed, suicidal, psychotically depressed, or not responding to meds quickly enough.
  • CBT shows up in practice as thought logs, identifying distortions, and reframing self-defeating thoughts.
  • Exercise and light therapy may be adjunctive, but they do not replace emergency safety management.
⭐ Exam Tips / NCLEX Traps
  • This is a trap: forgetting that ECT care includes routine anesthesia-style pre/post safety steps.
  • They love asking why the patient is placed side-lying after ECT — airway protection.
  • Thought stopping and reframing are classic CBT items.
🧠 Quick Memory Tools

ECT = “airway, awake, orient.” CBT = “catch it, check it, change it.”

  • GUIDELINES FOR TREATMENT OF THE SUICIDAL CLIENT (INPATIENT)

📖 Textbook Expansion

The textbook emphasizes that patients with affective instability should be assessed for suicide whenever functional status drops and mood disturbance is significant. The nurse should ask directly about thoughts of suicide and other violence. Direct conversation is therapeutic and can reduce isolation; it does not increase the likelihood of a suicide attempt.

A very testable concept from your notes is that low mood with increasing energy/cognition can be especially dangerous. A person who is severely depressed but now has enough energy to plan and act may actually be at greater acute risk. Another ominous clue is sudden unexplained calm or euthymia after severe distress, especially if paired with giving away belongings or farewell behaviors.

The book also adds practical safety details: high-risk patients may need limited medication quantities, family supervision, and mouth checks if medication hoarding is a concern. A written safety plan is more appropriate than a casual no-suicide promise because it identifies warning signs, coping steps, support people, and crisis resources.

Nursing Priority / Safety

  • Do not leave a high-risk suicidal patient alone.
  • Outpatient safety requires plan/means assessment, safety plan creation, support activation, and close follow-up.
  • Inpatient safety includes environmental sweep and searching belongings for sharps, cords, pills, lighters, belts, and other dangerous items.
  • Restrict access to firearms and overdose-prone medications.

Clinical Connection

  • A patient may feel relieved after deciding on suicide. Sudden calm is not always improvement.
  • Family members may promise secrecy. The nurse must teach that suicide threats or hints are never secrets to keep.
  • Support after a suicide death also matters. Survivors need active grief support and nonjudgmental listening.

✓ Possible escalating suicide risk

This is the classic trap. Sudden calm can reflect relief after deciding to die, not true recovery. Treat this as a safety concern, assess directly, and increase protection.

⭐ Exam Tips / NCLEX Traps
  • This is a trap: choosing a “no-suicide contract” over a safety plan. Your slide specifically favors a safety plan.
  • They will test whether you know that talking about suicide is appropriate and does not make it more likely.
  • Any answer that leaves the patient alone when risk is high is almost certainly wrong.
  • If the question asks about family teaching, “do not keep secrets” is huge.
🧠 Quick Memory Tools

For suicide assessment, think “PMP”: Plan, Means, Past attempts. For safety with loved ones, remember “No secrets, no weapons, no alone time.”

Module Wrap-Up + Objective Integration

Big Picture Summary, Top 10 Must-Know Exam Points, and Things That Will Get You Questions Wrong

Big Picture Summary

This whole module is really about recognition + safety + function + medication judgment. Depression lowers mood, energy, and often function. Mania raises energy and activation but destroys judgment and safety. Suicide risk can emerge anywhere on the mood spectrum, but it becomes especially dangerous when hopelessness combines with enough energy and cognition to act. The nurse’s role is not to diagnose the DSM label, but to rapidly identify unstable mood states, assess risk, protect the patient, support the family, and implement collaborative care that is realistic and evidence-based.

Top 10 Must-Know Exam Points
  • 1. Mood is subjective; affect is observable.
  • 2. Major depression symptoms must persist at least 2 weeks.
  • 3. Anhedonia, hopelessness, worthlessness, and recurrent thoughts of death are red flags.
  • 4. Children/adolescents may show depression as irritability.
  • 5. Acute mania care focuses on safety, limits, fluids/food, sleep, and low stimulation.
  • 6. SSRIs can cause serotonin syndrome; MAOIs can cause hypertensive crisis with tyramine.
  • 7. TCA overdose can be lethal.
  • 8. Lithium has a narrow therapeutic range and toxicity can start with GI symptoms.
  • 9. ECT care includes informed consent, NPO status, airway/VS monitoring, side-lying, and reorientation.
  • 10. Never leave a high-risk suicidal patient alone; use a safety plan, not a casual promise.
Things That Will Get You Questions Wrong
❌ Common Mistakes
  • Confusing mood with affect.
  • Thinking depression always presents as obvious sadness.
  • Missing irritability as a sign of depression in youth.
  • Ignoring psychomotor changes, poor grooming, or sleep/appetite changes.
  • Choosing to ignore inappropriate manic behavior instead of setting calm limits.
  • Forgetting that finger foods are used because manic patients often cannot sit still.
  • Not connecting MAOIs with tyramine and hypertensive crisis.
  • Not recognizing serotonin syndrome early.
  • Failing to hold/flag lithium when GI losses and coordination problems show up together.
  • Mistaking sudden calm in a suicidal patient for true recovery.
