High-Yield NCLEX Themes
🎯
Safety Is Always First
PRIORITY FRAMEWORK
  • 1. Is the patient safe?
  • 2. Is anyone else at risk?
  • 3. Is this a medical emergency?
  • 4. What is the most immediate need?
⚠️ Suicide risk is ALWAYS the #1 priority — even when indirect: "I don't see a way out," "I'm tired of everything."
🗣️
Therapeutic Communication Keys
UNIVERSAL RULES
  • ✓ Stay with the anxious client — presence = acceptance
  • ✓ Short, simple sentences during acute anxiety
  • ✓ Acknowledge feelings without validating delusions
  • ✓ Ask about command hallucinations — assess safety first
  • ✓ Never argue with delusions
  • ✗ Do NOT teach during severe/panic-level anxiety
💊
Drug Class Quick Reference
WHAT GOES WHERE
  • → SSRIs — first-line for anxiety & PTSD (fluoxetine, sertraline, paroxetine)
  • → Benzodiazepines — acute anxiety, short-term; GABA enhancers
  • → Buspirone — GAD; non-addictive, delayed onset, no sedation
  • → Typical antipsychotics — positive symptoms; EPS risk
  • → Atypical antipsychotics — broader; metabolic syndrome risk
🧠
The GAD Nursing Trap
MOST MISSED ON NCLEX
✅ Help client develop healthy, adaptive coping mechanisms — replacing maladaptive worrying
  • ✗ Creating anxiety-free environment — not possible or desirable
  • ✗ Simply avoiding triggers — usually not possible
  • ✗ Promising anxiety can be eliminated — false reassurance
Section 01

Foundations &
Neurotransmitters

The "why" behind every disorder and drug. Understand the chemistry before you memorize the interventions.

⚡
Serotonin
MOOD · SLEEP · ANXIETY
📉LOW serotonin = anxiety disorders, PTSD, depression
  • → SSRIs increase serotonin availability
  • → First-line for anxiety and PTSD
  • → Takes 3–4 weeks to reach therapeutic effect
🎯
Dopamine
PERCEPTION · THINKING · REWARD
📈EXCESS dopamine = psychosis, hallucinations, delusions
  • → Antipsychotics block dopamine receptors
  • → Explains positive symptoms of schizophrenia
  • → Dopamine pathway affected determines side effects
🛑
GABA
PRIMARY INHIBITOR
📉LOW GABA = overstimulation → anxiety
  • → Benzodiazepines enhance GABA activity
  • → Calming, sedating effect
  • → Risk of dependence with long-term use
Levels of Anxiety
🔑 NCLEX Key: To assess the degree of anxiety, assess the PERCEPTUAL FIELD — it narrows as anxiety increases. Memory, creativity, and delusional system are NOT measures of anxiety level.
📊
Four Levels of Anxiety
PERCEPTUAL FIELD NARROWS WITH EACH LEVEL
LevelPerceptionPhysical SignsNursing Focus
MILD Enhanced awareness, increased alertness ✅ Beneficial Normal vitals, alert, focused Teaching can happen here
MODERATE Narrowed field; problem-solving possible but limited ↑HR, ↑RR, muscle tension, ↑BP Guided coping, stay nearby
SEVERE Very narrow; cannot learn; focused on one detail Severe physical symptoms, hyperventilation Stay present, brief directions — NO teaching
PANIC Complete disorganization, loss of rational thought Terror, chest pain, feeling of dying Safety first; grounding; calm presence
💡 Mild anxiety initially amplifies physiological function and is beneficial — it enhances focus and decision-making. Prolonged anxiety leads to exhaustion and decreased function. Primary purpose of taking action to reduce anxiety = REDUCTION OF TENSION.
Nursing Assessment Framework
🩺
What to Always Assess
COMPREHENSIVE MENTAL HEALTH ASSESSMENT
  • 1.
    Suicide risk — always #1 priority. Hidden signs: "I don't see a way out," "I'm tired of everything"
  • 2.
    Support systems — family, friends, community, therapy access, resources
  • 3.
    Coping ability — handling new diagnosis, loss of independence, life stressors
  • 4.
    Defense mechanisms — adaptive vs maladaptive use
  • 5.
    Perceptual field — narrows with increasing anxiety
  • 6.
    Role adaptation — coping with life role changes
🏥
Rule Out Medical Causes First
NEVER ASSUME PURELY PSYCHOLOGICAL
⚕️ Anxiety is a subjective feeling of distress triggered by perceived threat, with both psychological AND physiological components.
  • → Cardiac conditions (arrhythmias)
  • → Endocrine disorders (hyperthyroidism)
  • → Substance use or withdrawal
  • → Medications (stimulants, decongestants)
Section 02

Anxiety Disorders

Panic Disorder, PTSD, GAD — nursing interventions, therapeutic communication, and expected outcomes.

💥
Panic Disorder
SUDDEN INTENSE EPISODES

Involves sudden, intense fear with physical symptoms that mimic life-threatening conditions. Patients often go to the ED believing they're having a heart attack.

Symptoms

  • · Tachycardia, palpitations
  • · Shortness of breath
  • · Chest pain, nausea
  • · Fear of dying or losing control
  • · Sweating, trembling

Acute Nursing (All SATA!)

  • ✓ Stay with the client
  • ✓ Give brief, simple directions
  • ✓ Use short, simple sentences
  • ✓ Reduce environmental stimuli
  • ✓ Guide controlled breathing
"You are safe. I am here with you." — correct therapeutic response during acute panic
🎖️
PTSD
POST-TRAUMATIC STRESS DISORDER

Develops after severe trauma; involves persistent re-experiencing. Survivors of disasters (earthquakes, etc.) should be assessed with the IES-R Tool.

IES-R ScoreReferral
High on one subscale (Client C)Social worker / mental health counselor
High on all subscales (Client A)Psychiatrist / clinical psychologist
Normal scores (Client B)No referral needed
Low on all (Client D)No referral needed
🧭 Long-term PTSD care: grounding techniques, encourage expression of feelings, support coping development, therapy participation. Do NOT initiate teaching during severe anxiety.
Therapeutic Communication Masterclass
💬
What Nurses Say vs What They Should Say
HIGH-FREQUENCY NCLEX SCENARIOS
ScenarioWrong ResponseCorrect Response & Why
Anxious client starts crying uncontrollably "Talking about your problem is upsetting you." / "It helps to get it out." "It's okay to cry; I'll just stay with you for now." — nonjudgmental; offers presence; reduces anxiety before talking can begin
Post-op patient anxious after teaching "Your anxiety may have kept you from understanding." / "Let me quiz you." "Sometimes clients having this surgery are anxious. Do you have any concerns?" — normalizes anxiety; opens communication
Pre-op client says "I gave my spouse all my financial records in case I don't make it" "Your surgeon is very experienced." / "People your age do well." "Are you concerned that you may die during surgery?" — reflective; addresses real fear; encourages communication
Withdrawn client refuses to leave bed Leave them alone indefinitely / Force them out of bed Stay with client until calm — provides support without power struggle; eventual limits may be needed but presence is immediate action
Anxious client pacing, looking side to side Suggest relaxation exercises / Watch from a distance Talk with the client to assess the meaning of behavior — determines if client is at risk; pacing = rising anxiety, early intervention essential
Section 03

OCD & Phobias

Ritualistic behaviors, compulsions, school phobia, and the critical nursing principle: rituals temporarily relieve anxiety.

🔁
OCD — Key Principles
OBSESSIVE-COMPULSIVE DISORDER
🔑The compulsive ritual temporarily relieves anxiety. The ritual IS the coping mechanism — it's useful, not useless.
  • ⚡ Interrupting a ritual → increases anxiety (most tested fact)
  • ⚡ Setting limits prematurely → increases anxiety; client must first develop other defenses
  • ⚡ Rituals manifest rigidity and inflexibility of the disorder
  • ⚡ OCD clients do NOT hallucinate
  • ⚡ Depression is common comorbidity but rituals do NOT relieve depression
  • ⚡ Anxiety in OCD is NOT related to time of day (sundowning is dementia)
🏁Correct discharge criterion: "Able to intervene when increasing levels of anxiety occur" — knowing when & how to interrupt the anxiety-ritual cycle
❌Wrong discharge criteria: "Verbalizes positive aspects of self" / "Follows therapeutic milieu rules" / "Recognizes hallucinations at extreme anxiety" (OCD ≠ hallucinations)
🐕
Phobias
SPECIFIC & AGORAPHOBIA
  • ✓ Discussing the feared object triggers the fear response
  • ✓ Clients have rigid impulse control — NOT poor impulse control
  • ✓ No distortion of reality — unlike psychosis
  • ✓ Treatment: desensitization programs (systematic exposure)
  • ✓ School phobia = BOTH legal AND emotional aspects (school attendance is legally required)
Short vs Long-Term Goals

Short-Term ✅

Describe thoughts and feelings in terrifying situations — realistic first step before solutions

Long-Term Only

Ride elevator without anxiety · Mood elevation · Going out unaccompanied

🏥Agoraphobia inpatient: essential SHORT-TERM goal = feeling safe in the unit. Going out unaccompanied & group comfort = long-term only.
Body Dysmorphic Disorder
🪞
Body Dysmorphic Disorder — Nursing Approach
PRIORITY INTERVENTION
✅Priority: Explore issues that influence the client's self-perception — address body image disturbance, provide nonthreatening and realistic feedback
  • ✗ Controlling manipulative behavior — clients with BDD are NOT manipulative
  • ✗ Relaxation techniques — not the priority; must address self-perception first
  • ✗ Desensitization — appropriate for phobias, not BDD
💡Key differentiator: Desensitization is for phobias. Body image work is for BDD. Know your treatment modalities!
Section 04

Defense Mechanisms

Nine essential defense mechanisms — every clinical scenario, every EAQ pattern, every NCLEX trap explained.

Memory Device
D · R · P · U · D · I · C · S · R
D
Denial — Ignoring/refusing to accept painful reality. "This can't be happening."
R
Regression — Reverting to earlier/childlike behavior for security. Fetal position = regression.
P
Projection — Attributing own feelings/impulses to another. Blaming others for your mistakes.
U
Undoing — Act that partially negates a previous one. OCD hand washing = undoing.
D
Displacement — Redirecting feelings to a safer target. Parents arguing after child's cancer dx.
I
Introjection — Treating external things as internal. Incorporating others' criticism into self-image.
C
Compensation — Making up for a perceived deficiency by emphasizing another asset.
S
Suppression — CONSCIOUS exclusion from awareness (vs. repression = unconscious).
R
Reaction Formation — Conscious behavior that is the opposite of an unconscious feeling.
Clinical Scenarios — Test Yourself
Answer: UNDOING
Undoing is an act that partially negates a previous one — the client uses this primitive defense mechanism to reduce anxiety. Clients who wash hands compulsively may be having thoughts they consider "dirty." The ritual temporarily undoes the perceived transgression. NOT displacement, projection, or suppression.
Answer: DISPLACEMENT
The parents are directing feelings about their child's diagnosis (the real threatening source) onto each other (a "safer" target). Denial = refusing to accept reality. Projection = attributing your feelings TO another person. Compensation = making up for a perceived deficiency.
Answer: REGRESSION
The client is reverting to an earlier developmental stage — curling up in the fetal position for both protection and security. In displacement, feelings are directed at others who are less threatening. Dissociation = detachment of emotional affect from an incident. Reaction formation = conscious behavior opposite of unconscious feeling.
Answer: PROJECTION
The client is attributing their own unacceptable impulses and behaviors to another person. This is the hallmark of projection — "it's not me, it's them." Common in paranoid presentations.
Answer: INTROJECTION
Introjection = treating something outside the self as if it's actually inside the self. The person takes in others' criticism and makes it part of their self-concept. This is different from projection (attributing your feelings to someone else) — introjection goes inward.
Answer: Defense against anxiety from real or imagined threats
Delusions are unconsciously used to defend against anxiety — they are precipitated by anxiety, NOT anger. Magical thinking (believing thoughts control events) is normal in young children but pathological in adults. Illusions = false interpretation of actual external stimuli (different from delusions).
Answer: DISPLACEMENT
Displacement = emotions directed at a different target (safer person/object) when the primary source creates too much anxiety or danger.

Projection = YOUR feelings attributed TO someone else (same or different target).

Memory trick: Displacement moves the emotion to a new target. Projection assigns your emotion to another person.
Section 05

Psychosis &
Schizophrenia

Loss of contact with reality. Positive vs negative symptoms, hallucination management, and the single most important question to ask.

🚨 Most important question for hallucinating client: "What are the voices telling you to do?" — Always assess for COMMAND HALLUCINATIONS to determine immediate safety risk.
➕
Positive Symptoms
EXCESS — ADDED BEHAVIORS
📌These are treated more effectively by typical (first-gen) antipsychotics
  • + Hallucinations (auditory most common)
  • + Delusions (fixed false beliefs)
  • + Paranoia
  • + Disorganized speech
  • + Disorganized behavior
➖
Negative Symptoms
DEFICIT — LOST BEHAVIORS
📌Harder to treat. More strongly impact quality of life. Atypical antipsychotics address these better.
  • – Flat affect (no emotional expression)
  • – Alogia (poverty of speech)
  • – Avolition (lack of motivation)
  • – Social withdrawal
  • – Anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure)
Therapeutic Communication
💬
Hallucinations & Delusions — What to Say
EVERY NCLEX PATTERN
SituationCorrect ApproachAvoid This
Client reports hearing voices "I understand you hear voices, but I do not hear them." — acknowledge without validating "Those voices aren't real, ignore them." — dismissive, confrontational, increases distrust
Client expresses frightening delusion "That must be very frightening." — empathize; do NOT argue with delusional content "That's not true." / "That can't be happening." — arguing escalates anxiety
Command hallucinations present Priority: "What are the voices telling you to do?" — assess for harm risk immediately Ignoring or only redirecting without safety assessment
New admission: suspicious & paranoid Accept the client's behavior — recognize as illness manifestation; meet immediate needs first Setting limits immediately, reviewing history, or meeting family first
Section 06

Pharmacology

Anxiolytics, antidepressants, and antipsychotics. Nursing priorities, expected/unexpected outcomes, medication safety, and dosage calculations.

Anxiolytics (Benzodiazepines)
🧘
Benzodiazepines — Nursing Priorities
GABA ENHANCERS · SHORT-TERM USE ONLY
DrugUsePriority AssessmentKey Teaching
Alprazolam (Xanax) Panic disorder, anxiety CHECK BP — orthostatic hypotension is EARLY side effect; also vital signs, cheeking, baseline comparison Change positions slowly; avoid alcohol; no abrupt stop
Lorazepam (Ativan) Acute panic attacks Monitor respiratory status; sedation; fall risk Calculation: 1.5 mg ÷ 0.5 mg × 1 tab = 3 tablets
Midazolam Pre-procedure sedation Older adults: smaller increments + SLOWER injection; administer over ≥2 min; wait 2+ min to evaluate NOT for rapid IV push; most serious SE = CNS depression
⚠️ New anxiolytic (within 24 hrs): NO aerobic exercise — orthostatic hypotension risk. DO: monitor vitals, check for cheeking, compare to baseline. Aerobic exercise = needs further teaching.
Antidepressants
🌈
Antidepressant Reference Guide
SSRIs · SNRIs · TCAs · Atypicals
Drug / ClassUsed ForMust-Know Nursing PointSide Effects
Fluoxetine, Sertraline, Paroxetine — SSRI Anxiety, PTSD, depression Takes 3–4 weeks to work; never stop abruptly; monitor for suicidal ideation Anxiety, insomnia (fluoxetine), sexual dysfunction
Venlafaxine ER (Effexor) — SNRI Anxiety and depression Do NOT crush/cut; taper up; never stop suddenly; 3–4 weeks onset; report suicidal thoughts Nausea, increased BP
Bupropion (Wellbutrin) — NDRI Depression RAISES BLOOD PRESSURE — reconsider if hypertension present! ↑BP, seizure risk, insomnia
Trazodone Depression, sleep Sedative effects — often used at bedtime Sedation, priapism
Mirtazapine Depression Drowsiness and abnormal dreams are expected side effects Drowsiness, weight gain
Imipramine — TCA Childhood enuresis (bedwetting) Used as adjuvant in pediatric enuresis; NOT OCD (that's clomipramine) Anticholinergic effects
Clomipramine — TCA OCD TCA specifically for OCD; different from imipramine (enuresis) Anticholinergic effects
Buspirone GAD Non-addictive; delayed onset (weeks); no sedation; no dependence Dizziness, headache
Antipsychotics
⚡
First-Gen (Typical)
HALOPERIDOL · CHLORPROMAZINE
🎯Treats positive symptoms. Higher EPS risk than atypical antipsychotics.

⚠️ EPS Side Effects:

  • → Acute dystonia — muscle spasms → treat with Benztropine
  • → Pseudoparkinsonism — tremor, rigidity, shuffling gait
  • → Akathisia — restlessness, can't sit still
  • → Tardive Dyskinesia — IRREVERSIBLE; involuntary rhythmic movements
☠️NMS (Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome) — LIFE-THREATENING: high fever + severe rigidity + altered consciousness + autonomic instability → STOP drug IMMEDIATELY → Notify provider → ICU → Dantrolene
🌟
Second-Gen (Atypical)
RISPERIDONE · OLANZAPINE · CLOZAPINE
📌Treats both positive AND negative symptoms. Less EPS but metabolic risks.

Side Effects to Teach:

  • → Metabolic syndrome — weight gain, diabetes → teach diet & exercise
  • → Orthostatic hypotension → teach slow position changes
  • → Anticholinergic — dry mouth, constipation
🩸CLOZAPINE → AGRANULOCYTOSIS
Dangerous drop in WBC. Mandatory CBC monitoring. Report fever IMMEDIATELY. Blood tests CANNOT be skipped.
Medication Calculations
🧮
Desire ÷ Have × Quantity
RATIO AND PROPORTION METHOD
①Lorazepam 1.5 mg ordered / 0.5 mg tabs available
1.5 ÷ 0.5 × 1 = 3 tablets
②Paroxetine 30 mg / 10 mg per 5 mL suspension
(30 ÷ 10) × 5 = 15 mL
③Ziprasidone 15 mg IM / 20 mg/mL available
15 ÷ 20 = 0.75 mL

Flash Cards

Tap any card to reveal the answer. Test yourself on high-yield EAQ concepts before the NCLEX practice set.

👆Click any card to flip — cover the grid and quiz yourself by topic!
What parameter assesses degree of anxiety?
Perceptual Field — narrows as anxiety ↑. Not memory, creativity, or delusional system.
Primary purpose of taking action to reduce anxiety?
Reduction of tension — not denial, not avoiding discomfort, not decision-making.
Interrupting an OCD ritual causes what?
Increased anxiety — the ritual controls anxiety. Interrupting = more anxiety, not hostility or withdrawal.
Benefit of MILD anxiety?
Increased alertness — attention becomes focused. Mild anxiety is beneficial and improves decision-making.
Alprazolam — first priority assessment?
Check blood pressure — orthostatic hypotension is a common EARLY side effect. Not pupils, urine output, or abdomen (those = prolonged use).
Which antidepressant raises blood pressure?
Bupropion (Wellbutrin) — flag this if client has hypertension! Fluoxetine = insomnia/anxiety, Trazodone = sedation.
NMS — first nursing action?
Stop the medication IMMEDIATELY → notify provider → ICU → dantrolene → supportive care.
Clozapine's most dangerous side effect?
Agranulocytosis — dangerous WBC drop. Mandatory CBC monitoring. Never skip blood tests. Report fever immediately.
Fetal position in corner = what defense mechanism?
Regression — reverting to earlier developmental behavior for protection and security.
Parents argue after child's cancer diagnosis = ?
Displacement — redirecting painful feelings about the diagnosis onto each other (safer target).
Childhood enuresis → which drug?
Imipramine (TCA) — childhood bedwetting adjuvant. OCD = clomipramine. Anxiety = alprazolam. Bipolar = lithium.
Most important question for hallucinating client?
"What are the voices telling you to do?" — COMMAND HALLUCINATIONS. Determines immediate safety risk. ALWAYS assess this.
Midazolam — how to adjust in older adults?
Smaller increments + slower injection rate — peak effect delayed in elderly. Administer over ≥2 min. Wait 2+ min to evaluate.
OCD correct discharge criterion?
"Able to intervene when increasing levels of anxiety occur" — NOT "verbalizes positives about self," NOT "follows milieu rules."
Body dysmorphic disorder — priority nursing action?
Explore issues influencing self-perception — address body image disturbance with nonthreatening realistic feedback. Desensitization is for phobias, not BDD.
4-month-old infant crying while NPO for surgery?
Offer a pacifier — sucking provides comfort via oral gratification. NOT rattle (stimulation), mobile (visual), or blanket (tactile only).
Agoraphobia inpatient: essential short-term goal?
Feeling safe in the unit — calm, quiet, nonthreatening environment. Going out unaccompanied and group comfort are long-term goals.
Delusions per psychodynamic theory serve what purpose?
Defense against anxiety from real or imagined threats — precipitated by anxiety, NOT anger. Unconscious protective mechanism.
