01

Depression: Mood Disorder Overview

Foundation

Depression is a widespread mood disorder that causes significant disability worldwide. It's characterized by overwhelming sadness, emptiness, and loss of functional status — sometimes to the point of incapacitation and loss of will to live.

Practice Question — Jennifer, MDD Admission ⚡ Priority Assessment

You are talking with Jennifer, a patient admitted with major depression. Which statement by the patient is the most concerning and indicates a need for immediate further assessment?

  • a."I have suicidal thoughts at times, but I don't have any plan and don't think I would ever actually hurt myself."
  • b."I don't have a good support system, but I am planning on joining a recovery group."
  • c."I think things will be much better soon."
  • d."I have trouble getting out of bed most days – I'm just so tired."
Show rationale

Why C: A sudden shift from persistent depression to unexplained calm, peace, or optimism is a significant red flag for impending suicide. Once a person decides to end their life, they often feel a sense of relief — they've made the decision, and the internal struggle is over. This may be accompanied by gift-giving, organizing affairs, writing letters, or acts of farewell.

Why not the others: Option A describes suicidal ideation without plan or intent (lower acuity, still requires monitoring). Option B shows engagement with recovery and forward-looking coping — a protective factor. Option D is a vegetative sign of depression (anergia) that's expected and not the most urgent finding.

Key teaching: An individual with newly prescribed antidepressants is especially vulnerable during the first weeks as mood lifts — energy returns before mood fully lifts, giving them the capacity to act on suicidal thoughts.

Who can diagnose depression? What can the nurse observe and report?

Who diagnoses: Depression is a medical/psychiatric diagnostic term. Diagnosis is outside the scope of practice for the generalist nurse. Only qualified advanced practice professionals can diagnose mood spectrum disorders — this includes advanced practice nurses, social workers, psychologists, physicians, and other mental health professionals with specialized training.

What the nurse observes and reports:

  • Affect — the objective, observable response to feelings
  • Mental status: appearance, motor activity, mood, speech, alertness/orientation
  • Signs of affective instability — crying, rage, euphoria, blunting, flatness
  • Functional status (ADLs, problem-solving ability)
  • Risk for suicide or violence
  • Vegetative signs (sleep, appetite, energy, sex drive)
◆
Clinical Pearl
Mental status = the sixth vital sign. Just as a nurse monitors pulse, respirations, BP, temperature, and pain, mental status requires continuous monitoring and documentation.
What is affect?

Affect is the outward, observable representation of a person's internal emotional state. It's an objective finding that nurses assess. Mood is subjective (what the person feels); affect is objective (what the nurse sees — facial expression, voice tone, posture, eye contact).

Affect is described as either congruent with mood (sad face with depressed mood) or incongruent (smiling despite reporting depression).

Types of affect commonly seen in major depressive disorder:

Constricted

  • Reduction in range and intensity of normal emotional expression
  • Less variation than typical

Blunted (Shallow)

  • More severe than constricted
  • Significant decrease in emotional reactivity

Flat

  • No or nearly no emotional expression or reactivity at all
  • Most severe form
▲
Primary Concern — Depressed Client
SAFETY — Risk for Suicide. The most dangerous aspect of major depressive disorder is preoccupation with death and suicidal ideation. Patients with major depressive disorder must always be evaluated for suicidal ideation. Risk is further elevated when depression is accompanied by hopelessness, substance use, recent loss or separation, a history of past suicide attempts, and acute suicidal ideation. Safety always comes first, then everything else.

Assessment: Signs & Symptoms

What You'll Find
What signs and symptoms will you likely find when interviewing a client who has depression?

Depression affects the whole person — cognition, behavior, physiology, and emotion. Use this organized framework for assessment:

Area What You Find
Appearance Poor grooming and hygiene, unkempt clothing, slumped/hunched posture, head down with shoulders forward, minimal or no eye contact
Behavior Anergia (abnormal lack of energy) leading to psychomotor retardation — slowed movements, decreased facial expressions, fixed gaze, sometimes complete inactivity. Alternatively, psychomotor agitation — pacing, nail biting, finger tapping
Mood Depressed, sad, empty, hopeless. Anhedonia — inability to feel pleasure in activities that normally bring joy. Mood can last weeks to months
Affect Constricted, blunted, or flat. May be congruent (sad face) or incongruent (smiling while hopeless)
Speech Slow, soft, monotone, lacking spontaneity. May require repeating questions. In severe cases — mute
Thought Processes Slowed thinking ("poverty of thought"), delayed or absent responses, difficulty with decisions
Thought Content Worthlessness, guilt, hopelessness, helplessness. Exaggerated perceived faults. In severe depression — delusions (mood-congruent, e.g., "I deserve to die") or hallucinations (critical voices)
Cognition Decreased ability to think or concentrate, indecisiveness, deficits in attention, short-term and working memory, processing speed. Cognitive deficits may linger even after treatment
Vegetative Signs Hallmark feature. Appetite: typically decreased (up to 5% weight loss in a month) or occasionally increased. Sleep: insomnia with early morning waking (3–4 AM) common; sometimes hypersomnia. Elimination: constipation common (especially with psychomotor retardation). Sex drive: decreased libido in both men and women; impotence in men
Suicidality Passive thoughts of death → fantasies → active suicidal ideation → plan → means. Always assess directly
✦
Quick Recall — SIG E CAPS†1
A classic mnemonic for DSM-5 depression criteria: Sleep changes · Interest loss (anhedonia) · Guilt/worthlessness · Energy loss · Concentration difficulty · Appetite/weight change · Psychomotor changes · Suicidality. Need 5 of 9 for ≥ 2 weeks, with at least one being depressed mood or loss of interest.
How would you phrase your questions to assess for depression?

Use open-ended questions, make observations, avoid "why" questions (they feel accusatory), and allow plenty of time for responses because slowed thinking makes processing take longer.

Opening and broad assessment:

  • "Over the past 2 weeks, have you ever felt down, depressed, or hopeless?"
  • "Have you felt little interest or pleasure in doing things?"
  • "How do you feel?" (while observing facial cues, voice tone, posture)
  • "You said you are depressed. Tell me what that is like for you."
  • "When you feel depressed, what sort of thoughts do you have?"

Progressing to suicide assessment:

  • "Have you had thoughts about ending your life?"
  • "Do you have a plan?"
  • "Do you have the means to carry out your plan?"
  • "Is there anything that would prevent you from carrying out your plan?"
●
Common Screening Tools
PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire) — quick 9-item tool widely used in primary care · Beck Depression Inventory · Hamilton Depression Scale · Geriatric Depression Scale — 30-item, yes/no format, valid in older adults with cognitive deficits.

Adolescents & Depression

Age-Specific
▲
Why This Matters
Suicide is among the top three causes of death for adolescents in the United States (CDC). In fact, suicide is the second-leading cause of death in 10–24 year olds. In 2017, approximately 13% of U.S. adolescents had at least one major depressive episode — a rate higher than adults, making early recognition critical.
How might signs/symptoms of depression vary for adolescents? What should the nurse ask adolescents and their caregivers?

The core symptoms — sadness and loss of pleasure — are the same across all ages. What differs is how they are displayed.

Young Children

  • Crying, sadness
  • Sudden refusal to go to school
  • Somatic complaints
  • Behavioral regression

School-Age

  • Withdrawal from peers and activities
  • Decline in school performance
  • Irritability

Adolescents — Key Red Flags

  • Irritability rather than overt sadness (DSM-5 allows irritable mood to replace depressed mood in kids/teens)
  • Substance use — alcohol, drugs, vaping
  • Sexual promiscuity, risk-taking
  • Preoccupation with death, suicide, writing/drawing about death
  • Giving away prized possessions
  • Sudden withdrawal from friends, hobbies, family
  • Changes in sleep and appetite
  • Declining grades

Questions to ask adolescents (and their caregivers):

  • "Tell me about the last two weeks — how have you been feeling?"
  • "Have there been changes in what you enjoy doing, your sleep, your appetite?"
  • "Have you been feeling irritable or angry more than usual?"
  • "Do you ever have thoughts of hurting yourself or ending your life?"
  • To caregivers: "Have you noticed changes in their sleep, appetite, friends, grades, or mood?"

Other conditions to screen for when assessing depression in adolescents:

  • Anxiety disorders — symptoms of anxiety occur in ~70% of MDD cases
  • Substance use — frequently co-occurs and self-medicates
  • Eating disorders
  • ADHD
  • Disruptive mood dysregulation disorder — persistent irritability and severe temper outbursts, onset before age 10
  • Bipolar disorder — especially if manic/hypomanic symptoms emerge with antidepressant treatment
  • Adverse childhood experiences, trauma, abuse, neglect

Nursing Care: Acute Depression

Priority Interventions
What are the nursing priorities for the acutely depressed client?
  1. Assess risk for suicide and self-harm continuously — not just once on admission. Overt hostility is highly correlated with suicide.
  2. Provide a safe environment. Remove all harmful objects: "sharps" (razors, scissors, nail files), strangulation risks (belts, cords, shoelaces), and any medications that could be used to overdose.
  3. Implement observation level as ordered — Q15-minute checks or 1:1 continuous observation for higher acuity.
  4. Help with ADLs because anergia and poor concentration make even basic self-care overwhelming. Give step-by-step reminders ("Wash the right side of your face, now the left").
  5. Address vegetative signs — frequent small high-calorie snacks, fluids, fiber to prevent constipation, support sleep hygiene.
  6. Promote therapeutic alliance — spend regularly scheduled time, even in silence.
▲
Direct Observation & the Bathroom
The patient on direct (1:1) observation is never left alone — including in the bathroom. The nurse/sitter must maintain visual contact at all times. This may mean keeping the bathroom door cracked or standing just inside. Preserving as much privacy and dignity as possible while maintaining line of sight is the goal. The bathroom is a common location for suicide attempts because of privacy, hanging opportunities from fixtures, and access to water — direct observation cannot be suspended even briefly.

Therapeutic Communication

With the Depressed Client
What communication techniques work best with a severely withdrawn or depressed patient?
Technique Rationale
Make observations
"There are new pictures on the wall." "You are wearing your new shoes."		 When a patient is not ready to talk, direct questions raise anxiety. Observations point to commonalities and gently draw the patient into reality
Use simple, concrete words Slowed thinking and difficulty concentrating impair comprehension

Antidepressant Medications

Pharmacology
What are the major classes of antidepressants, and how do they compare?
Class Examples Key Points
SSRIs
Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors		 Fluoxetine (Prozac), Sertraline (Zoloft), Paroxetine (Paxil), Citalopram (Celexa), Escitalopram (Lexapro) First-line Block reuptake of serotonin. Lower side-effect profile than TCAs. Risk of lethal overdose is minimized. Side effects: agitation, insomnia, headache, nausea, sexual dysfunction, hyponatremia. Discontinuation syndrome if stopped abruptly — taper slowly
SNRIs
Serotonin–Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors		 Venlafaxine (Effexor), Duloxetine (Cymbalta), Desvenlafaxine (Pristiq) Block reuptake of both serotonin and norepinephrine. Popular next-step after SSRI trial. Duloxetine also treats neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia, GAD. May increase BP (venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine, levomilnacipran)
TCAs
Tricyclic Antidepressants		 Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline (Pamelor), Imipramine (Tofranil), Desipramine, Doxepin Lethal in overdose Second-line. Inhibit serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake. Anticholinergic side effects — dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, orthostatic hypotension, cardiac toxicity, sedation. Use caution in older adults, cardiac disease, suicide risk
MAOIs
Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors		 Phenelzine (Nardil), Tranylcypromine (Parnate), Isocarboxazid (Marplan), Selegiline patch (Emsam) Strict diet restrictions Inhibit enzyme that breaks down serotonin and norepinephrine. Avoid tyramine-rich foods (aged cheese, cured meats, fermented foods, wine, soy sauce) — tyramine + MAOI = hypertensive crisis. Give patient a wallet card. 14-day washout between SSRIs and MAOIs
NDRI
Norepinephrine–Dopamine Reuptake Inhibitor		 Bupropion (Wellbutrin, Zyban) Also used for smoking cessation. Lowers seizure threshold — avoid in seizure disorders, eating disorders. Does not cause sexual dysfunction (advantage over SSRIs)
NaSSA Mirtazapine (Remeron) Enhances norepinephrine and serotonin release. Causes sedation and weight gain — useful when patient has insomnia and poor appetite
NMDA Antagonist Esketamine (Spravato) — nasal spray FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression. Used off-label for acute suicidality. Patient must be monitored 2 hours post-administration. Schedule III — risk of abuse
Why do clients need to be assessed for suicide after starting antidepressant medications?

This is one of the most critical teaching points in psychiatric nursing.

▲
The Energy-Before-Mood Paradox
Antidepressants take 1 to 3 weeks (or longer) to produce full therapeutic effect on mood. However, energy and motivation often return first. This means a patient who previously felt too depleted to act on suicidal thoughts may now have the energy and motivation to carry out a plan — while their mood and hopelessness are still at baseline. The FDA black box warning on antidepressants in children, adolescents, and young adults reflects this increased risk of suicidal thinking and behavior in the early weeks.

Nursing implications:

  • Assess for suicidal ideation at every encounter, especially in the first weeks of treatment
  • Teach patient and family to watch for increased agitation, worsening mood, or new/worsening suicidal thoughts
  • Ensure close follow-up in the first weeks — don't send home with 90-day supply
  • Provide crisis resources before the patient leaves
What should the client be taught about antidepressants?
  • Therapeutic effect may take several weeks (1–3+ weeks) — do not stop because it "isn't working" early
  • Do not discontinue suddenly — abrupt stopping causes discontinuation syndrome (dizziness, nausea, flu-like symptoms, irritability, insomnia). Always taper
  • Avoid alcohol while taking antidepressants — potentiates CNS depression and worsens depression
  • Notify provider of any new or worsening suicidal thoughts, rash, rapid heartbeat, sore throat
  • Avoid hazardous activities (driving, operating machinery) until side effects are known
  • Take with meals if GI upset occurs
  • Do not take OTC drugs (including St. John's wort) without checking with provider
  • For MAOIs: strict tyramine-free diet, avoid Asian restaurants, carry emergency card, go to ED immediately for severe headache
  • Sexual dysfunction is a common side effect — report to provider rather than stopping

Serotonin Syndrome

Emergency
What is a potentially serious side effect of SSRIs that the nurse must recognize? What is the treatment?

Serotonin syndrome is a rare but life-threatening event caused by overactivation of central serotonin receptors. It results from too high a dose or drug interactions — most commonly when an SSRI is combined with another serotonin-enhancing agent (MAOI, triptans, St. John's wort, tramadol, linezolid).

Signs & Symptoms

  • Hyperactivity, restlessness, irritability
  • Tachycardia → cardiovascular shock
  • Fever → hyperpyrexia (dangerously high)
  • Elevated blood pressure
  • Altered mental state (delirium)
  • Mood changes, hostility, irrationality
  • Seizures → status epilepticus
  • Myoclonus (muscle spasms), incoordination, tonic rigidity
  • Abdominal pain, diarrhea, bloating
  • Sweating, tremor
  • Apnea → death

Treatment / Interventions

  • Remove the offending agent(s) immediately
  • Initiate symptomatic supportive treatment
  • Serotonin-receptor blockade — cyproheptadine, methysergide, propranolol
  • Cooling blankets / chlorpromazine for hyperthermia
  • Dantrolene or diazepam for muscle rigidity
  • Anticonvulsants for seizures
  • Artificial ventilation if respiratory compromise
  • Induction of paralysis in severe cases
  • Supportive care in ICU
◆
Prevention
A patient must discontinue all SSRIs for 2 to 5 weeks before starting an MAOI (fluoxetine requires 5 weeks due to its long half-life). When switching between antidepressant classes, always check the washout period.

Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)

Biological Treatment
What is ECT?

ECT is a somatic (physical) treatment for psychiatric disorders in which brief seizures are deliberately induced by an electrical current. Despite its dramatic reputation from the past, modern ECT — with general anesthesia and paralytic agents — is actually a controlled, safe procedure.

Key Parameters

  • Seizure duration: 30–60+ seconds
  • Electrical current duration: as brief as 1 second
  • Frequency: 2–3 times per week (often QOD — every other day†3)
  • Course: 6 to 12 total treatments
  • Age approved: FDA-approved for patients 13 years and older

Theory / Indications

  • Theory: Increases availability of neurotransmitters
  • Primary use: Treatment-resistant depression and mania when medications are ineffective
  • Also used for: Acute suicidality (works faster than medications), bipolar disorder with severe depression, schizoaffective disorder, catatonia
  • Effectiveness: ~80% of patients, particularly effective in older adults
How do we prepare the client for the ECT procedure?
  • Obtain informed consent (patient if voluntary; next of kin or court order if involuntary)
  • Provide patient and family teaching about what to expect — this is crucial
  • Pre-procedure workup: chest x-ray, ECG, urinalysis, complete blood count (CBC), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), electrolyte panel
  • NPO after midnight (general anesthesia will be given)
  • Discontinue benzodiazepines — they interfere with the seizure process and reduce ECT efficacy
  • Remove dentures, jewelry, hairpins; void bladder before procedure
  • Administer general anesthetic to induce sleep and muscle-paralyzing agent to prevent muscle injury and fractures
  • Ensure IV access, oxygen, suction, and emergency equipment available
What is monitored during the ECT procedure?
  • EEG — monitors brain waves and seizure activity
  • ECG — monitors cardiac response (the heart can be stressed at seizure onset and for up to 10 min after)
  • Pulse oximetry — oxygen saturation
  • Blood pressure monitoring
  • Airway patency — anesthesia team manages airway during paralysis
◆
Post-Procedure Care
Patients wake about 15 minutes after the procedure. Expect confusion and disorientation for several hours. Most experience retrograde amnesia — loss of memory for events leading up to and including the treatment. Nursing care is like any postoperative patient: monitor airway, monitor mental status, frequently orient the patient to person, place, time, and situation. Have family available. Side effects also include headaches, nausea, muscle aches, and occasional longer-term memory loss.

Postpartum Depression

Perinatal
How do we recognize postpartum depression? What does it look like?

Postpartum depression (PPD) is a serious mood disorder occurring after childbirth. Unlike the transient "baby blues" (which resolves within 2 weeks), PPD is persistent, significantly impairs functioning, and puts both mother and infant at risk.

What It Looks Like

  • Persistent sadness, tearfulness, emptiness
  • Severe mood swings, irritability
  • Loss of interest in the baby or excessive worry about the baby
  • Difficulty bonding with the infant
  • Feelings of worthlessness, shame, guilt, or inadequacy as a parent
  • Fatigue, insomnia, or hypersomnia
  • Appetite changes
  • Thoughts of harming self or the baby
  • Anxiety, panic attacks

Risk Factors & Red Flags

  • History of depression or anxiety
  • Family history of mood disorders
  • Hormonal changes post-delivery
  • Sleep deprivation
  • Lack of social support
  • Relationship stress, financial stress
  • Severe postpartum psychosis within 2 weeks of birth — associated with 4× increased risk of developing bipolar disorder

How we screen:

  • Use the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)†2 or the PHQ-9 at prenatal and postnatal visits
  • Ask simple screening questions: "Over the past 2 weeks have you felt down, depressed, or hopeless?" and "Have you had little interest or pleasure in doing things?"
  • Screen at the 6-week postpartum visit and at well-child visits in the first year
  • Assess thoughts of self-harm or harming the baby at every visit
●
Treatment Pearl — Brexanolone
Brexanolone (Zulresso) is the first and only FDA-approved medication specifically for postpartum depression. It's a neuroactive steroid given as a 60-hour continuous IV infusion (2.5 days). Requires continuous pulse oximetry throughout treatment due to risk of hypoxia and sudden loss of consciousness. Schedule IV controlled substance. Because of sedation risk during infusion, patients need supervision especially during interactions with their child. Interpersonal psychotherapy and SSRIs are also highly effective for PPD.

Bipolar Disorder & Mania

Mood Elevation

Bipolar disorder is characterized by extreme mood swings — both lowering and exaggerated elevation of mood. Bipolar I requires at least one manic episode; bipolar II involves hypomanic episodes + major depressive episodes. Hypomania differs from mania by being less severe and lacking psychotic features.

What are the most important dangers to the patient experiencing mania? What do we always need to assess for?

Because manic patients have unlimited energy, poor judgment, and impaired insight, physical and psychological safety dominate every clinical decision.

▲
Most Dangerous Threats in Mania
Physical exhaustion and cardiac collapse from nonstop activity · Dehydration and malnutrition (too distracted to eat/drink) · Injury from reckless behavior · Suicide (manic patients have higher suicide risk than depressive patients due to increased energy) · Violence to self or others · Harm from poor judgment — spending sprees, sexual indiscretions, dangerous driving, substance use

Always assess for:

  • Risk for injury and self-harm
  • Suicide risk (even in mania!)
  • Violence potential toward others
  • Vital signs — especially HR and BP (overactivity strains the heart)
  • Hydration status and intake/output
  • Nutritional intake
  • Sleep patterns (even one sleepless night can worsen mania)
  • Hygiene deficits
  • Medication adherence and lithium level
What does the patient look like when they are manic?
DomainPresentation
Mood Elevated, expansive, euphoric ("on top of the world") OR irritable, hostile. Unstable — can shift rapidly to rage
Behavior Constant physical activity, pacing, hyperactivity. Provocative or seductive dress. Risk-taking — unrestrained spending sprees, sexual indiscretions, foolish business investments
Speech Pressured speech — rapid, loud, difficult to interrupt. Flight of ideas — rapid shifts from one topic to another. Loose associations. Clang associations (rhyming). Puns
Thought Content Grandiosity — inflated self-esteem, may believe they have special powers, fame, wealth, connections. In severe mania: grandiose delusions, sometimes paranoia
Cognition Racing thoughts, difficulty concentrating, highly distractible, poor judgment, impaired insight
Sleep Decreased need for sleep — may go days without sleep while feeling rested
Appearance Flamboyant dress, excessive makeup, inappropriate attire. Poor hygiene due to distractibility
Psychotic Features (severe mania only) Hallucinations, grandiose or paranoid delusions. Note: psychosis does NOT occur in hypomania
What are potential patient problems and their nursing solutions?
ProblemNursing Solution
Risk for injury / self-harm Priority! Low-stimulation environment. PRN medication, seclusion, or restraint in acute mania. Remove valuables (store in hospital safe). Redirect aggression
Imbalanced nutrition Offer frequent, high-calorie, high-protein finger foods and drinks (sandwiches, fruit, milkshakes) that can be eaten on the run. Monitor I&O. Weigh daily. Provide frequent reminders: "Tom, finish your milkshake"
Fluid volume deficit / dehydration Frequent high-calorie fluids. Monitor intake, output, vital signs, specific gravity
Sleep deprivation Structured quiet periods. Direct to low-stimulation areas. Avoid caffeine. Warm baths, soothing music at night. PRN sleep medication
Self-care deficit (hygiene, dress) Step-by-step reminders ("Here is your razor. Shave the left side... now the right side"). Encourage appropriate clothing choices to preserve dignity
Impaired verbal communication Short, concise statements. Firm, calm approach. Consistent expectations. Do not argue with delusions
Constipation High-fiber foods, fluids, exercise. Evaluate for laxative. Dehydration and decreased peristalsis can lead to fecal impaction
Risk for sexual indiscretion / exploitation Two-nurse approach when appropriate. Never leave a male staff member alone with a seductive female patient (and vice versa). Redirect without shaming
Financial / legal harm from manipulation Store valuables (money, credit cards, jewelry) in hospital safe until rational judgment returns
What therapeutic communication techniques work with the manic patient?
  • Firm, calm, neutral approach — "John, come with me. Eat this sandwich."
  • Use short, concise explanations and statements — short attention span limits comprehension
  • Be consistent in approach and expectations — minimizes staff manipulation
  • Identify expectations in simple, concrete terms with consequences — "Do not yell at Peter. If you cannot control yourself, we will help you."
  • Hear and act on legitimate complaints — reduces underlying feelings of helplessness
  • Redirect energy into appropriate channels — distractibility is your most useful tool
  • Avoid arguing with delusions or grandiose thinking
  • Maintain low-stimulation environment — away from bright lights, loud noises, crowds
  • Set limits firmly and without anger
Practice Question — Priority in Mania ⚡ Priority Setting

The priority problem for a hyperactive manic client during the acute phase is:

  • a.Risk for injury or self-harm
  • b.Ineffective role performance
  • c.Risk for sleep disturbance
  • d.Impaired verbal communication
Show rationale

Why A: In acute mania, the hyperactive patient cannot meet basic physiological needs or set limits on behavior. Dehydration, exhaustion, cardiac collapse, reckless behavior, violence, and suicide all fall under "risk for injury/self-harm." Safety is always the priority.

Maslow's hierarchy — physiological needs and safety come before all other psychosocial concerns. Ineffective role performance (B), sleep disturbance (C), and impaired communication (D) are real problems but are secondary to keeping the patient alive and uninjured during the acute phase. All three will also be addressed through the safety interventions (low-stimulation environment promotes sleep; firm limits help communication).

What teaching must the patient on lithium receive?
▲
Lithium Therapeutic Range — Memorize!
Therapeutic: 0.8–1.4 mEq/L · Toxicity begins: 1.5 mEq/L and above. The narrow window means dosing adjustments are critical. Toxic range may even overlap the therapeutic range.

Early Signs of Toxicity

  • Diarrhea
  • Vomiting
  • Drowsiness
  • Muscular weakness
  • Lack of coordination

Severe Toxicity

  • Ataxia (irregular muscle movements)
  • Giddiness
  • Tinnitus
  • Blurred vision
  • Large output of dilute urine
  • Seizures, coma, death

Patient Teaching Essentials (Box 13.2):

  • Lithium is a mood stabilizer, not addictive — continue even when feeling well
  • Maintain consistent fluid intake — 1500–3000 mL/day (about six 12-oz glasses)
  • Maintain consistent sodium intake. Low sodium → higher lithium levels → toxicity. High sodium → lower lithium levels → loss of effect
  • Stop lithium and call provider if excessive diarrhea, vomiting, or sweating (dehydration → toxicity)
  • Check with prescriber before any OTC medications — even NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) can raise lithium levels
  • Take with meals to reduce GI irritation
  • Expect ~5 lb water weight gain in the first week; discuss ongoing weight changes
  • Periodic monitoring: thyroid function (hypo- or hyperthyroidism risk), parathyroid, renal function, lithium blood level
  • Avoid diuretics without provider knowledge — they affect lithium levels
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy and breastfeeding
  • Tapered gradually if discontinued, to minimize relapse risk
◆
Physiologic Integrity in Mania — Big Three
Dehydration · Malnutrition · Lack of sleep — these three threats drive the majority of acute mania interventions. Every action the nurse takes (offering finger foods, providing high-calorie fluids, redirecting to quiet areas, decaffeinated drinks, warm baths at bedtime) targets one of these three concerns. Nutritional deficiencies and dehydration plus constant physical activity can cause exhaustion and cardiac collapse.

Suicide

Safety Priority

Suicide is the intentional act of ending one's life. If a client has had a recent suicide attempt, the biggest threat to the client — the thing most likely to cause their death — is another completed suicide attempt. Past attempts are the strongest predictor of future attempts.

Practice Question — Ava & Asking About Suicide ⚡ Therapeutic Communication

You are working with Ava, another student nurse on the psychiatric unit. She tells you she doesn't want to ask her patient about suicidal ideation because "It might put ideas in her head about suicide." The correct response would be:

  • a."You're right. She may not have thought of suicide before, and we don't want to introduce that."
  • b."You are right; however, because of professional liability, we have to ask that question."
  • c."Actually, it's a myth that asking about suicide puts ideas into someone's head."
  • d."If I were you, I'd ask the doctor to talk to the patient about that subject."
Show rationale

Why C: It has been consistently demonstrated that talking about suicidal feelings does NOT increase the likelihood of acting on them at any age. In fact, most people contemplating suicide feel relief when someone finally asks — they feel less isolated and more able to problem-solve. Asking directly about suicide is a professional responsibility, comparable to asking about chest pain in a cardiac patient.

Why not the others: Option A reinforces the myth — dangerous misinformation. Option B concedes the myth is true and frames the question only as legal CYA. Option D passes the buck and avoids education — assessment is a nursing responsibility, not just medical.

What is the most important question we can ask a patient we think is suicidal?

The single most important assessment question is a direct, unambiguous question about suicide:

◆
The Question
"Are you thinking about suicide?" or "Are you thinking about ending your life?"

Follow immediately with: "Do you have a plan?"

Other associated things to assess:

  • "Have you ever felt that life was not worth living?"
  • "Have you been thinking about death recently?"
  • "Have you ever attempted suicide before?" (previous attempts = highest predictor)
  • "Do you have a plan for ending your life?"
  • "If so, what is your plan?" — assess specificity
  • "Do you have access to [the method mentioned]?" — assess means
  • "Is there anything that would prevent you from carrying out your plan?" — assess protective factors
  • "When did these thoughts start, and how often do they occur?"

Other issues that often accompany suicidal ideation — assess these too:

  • Hopelessness and helplessness
  • Substance use (alcohol or other substances) — reduces inhibitions
  • Recent loss (relationship, job, health, housing, loved one, pet)
  • Psychiatric diagnoses — MDD, substance use disorder, psychosis, anxiety, personality disorders
  • Physical/chronic illness, chronic pain
  • Recent discharge from psychiatric care
  • Social isolation
  • Access to lethal means (firearms, medications)
  • History of childhood abuse/trauma (ACEs)
  • Family history of suicide
Who is at increased risk for suicide?

Demographic & Population

  • Men (die by suicide ~3.5× more than women, though women attempt more)
  • White males account for ~70% of all suicide deaths
  • Adults ages 45–54 (highest rate); second-highest is 85+
  • Men 85+ have highest rate of any age group
  • Veterans (1.5× higher than general population)
  • American Indian/Alaska Native youth (highest among 15–44 yr)
  • LGBTQ+ youth†5
  • Adolescents and young adults (2nd leading cause of death in 10–34 yr)

Clinical Risk Factors

  • Previous suicide attempt(s) — strongest predictor
  • Family history of suicide
  • Mental illness — MDD, bipolar, schizophrenia, anxiety, personality disorders
  • Substance use disorders — alcohol present in 41% of deaths
  • Hopelessness
  • Impulsive or aggressive tendencies
  • Adverse childhood experiences, trauma
  • Chronic physical illness, chronic pain, loss of mobility, disfigurement
  • Recent loss (relationship, job, financial, housing)
  • Easy access to lethal means
  • Social isolation
  • Barriers to mental health treatment
  • Local epidemics / cluster suicide
How do we assess for a plan and lethality?

Evaluating the suicide plan has three main elements:

  1. Is there a specific plan with details? (time, place, means) — the more specific, the higher the risk
  2. How lethal is the proposed method?
  3. Is there access to the planned method?

People with definite plans including time, place, and means are at highest risk.

"Hard" Methods — Higher Risk

Death is rapid; reversal difficult

  • Firearm (most common method — over 50% of deaths)
  • Jumping from a high place
  • Hanging
  • Carbon monoxide poisoning
  • Staging a car crash

"Soft" Methods — Lower Lethality

Slower onset; more time for rescue

  • Cutting wrists
  • Inhaling natural gas
  • Ingesting pills
▲
Psychosis = High Risk Regardless
When a patient is experiencing a psychotic episode, they are at high risk regardless of the specificity of details because impulse control and judgment are grossly impaired. A person with psychosis is especially vulnerable when also depressed or experiencing command hallucinations.
What does a safe environment look like in the hospital? Whose responsibility is it to keep the patient safe?

Safety is everyone's responsibility — but primarily the nurse's. The entire interdisciplinary team (nurses, prescribers, mental health technicians, social workers, dietitians, family) participates, but the nurse coordinates and maintains the safety plan.

Essential elements of suicide precautions:

  • Remove all "sharps" — razors, scissors, nail files, glass, metal utensils, tweezers, matches
  • Remove strangulation risks — belts, shoelaces, drawstrings, cords, neckties, long jewelry
  • Remove medications that could be used to overdose (patient's home meds go to pharmacy)
  • Check all items brought in by visitors; no plastic bags†4
  • Use plastic utensils and count them when meal tray is collected; ensure no glass or metal silverware
  • Do not assign to a private room; ensure the room door remains open at all times
  • Jump-proof and hang-proof bathrooms — breakaway shower rods, recessed shower nozzles, tamper-proof window screens
  • Keep electrical cords to minimal length; install unbreakable glass; keep windows locked
  • Lock all utility rooms, kitchens, stairwells, and offices
  • Perform mouth checks — observe the patient swallow each dose of medication to prevent hoarding
  • When patient is sleeping, hands should always remain in view, not under covers
  • Assign observation level per provider order and acuity
  • Document mental status and observations continuously (including affect descriptors — "hostile, fearful, calm" — not just location/activity)
◆
Observation Levels & Frequency
Q15-minute checks (every 15 minutes) — for moderate risk, visually verify patient's location and status and document each check.
1:1 / Continuous observation (direct observation) — staff member remains with the patient at all times — within arm's reach or constant line of sight, including in the bathroom. No breaks, no exceptions without relief. This is the highest level of monitoring and is used for patients at high imminent risk.
What are the nursing care priorities for the suicidal patient?
  • Help establish a safety plan collaboratively with the patient
  • Promote trust and a therapeutic alliance — consistent, nonjudgmental presence
  • Assess verbal and nonverbal clues continuously
  • Look for warning signs — talking about death, expressing no reason to live, withdrawal, increased substance use, giving away possessions, writing/organizing affairs, sudden calm after persistent depression
  • Provide a safe environment (see above)
  • Implement suicide precautions per policy and order
  • Teach coping skills — problem-solving, conflict resolution, stress management
  • Provide support and resources — 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, Crisis Text Line, community support groups
  • Always use follow-up questions — do not accept vague answers
  • Become comfortable asking questions about suicide directly
What therapeutic communication is essential?
  • Therapeutic milieu environment — safe, structured, predictable inpatient setting
  • Build therapeutic alliance — essential for treatment adherence
  • Listen for covert messages ("I won't be a problem much longer," "Things will never work out," "How can I donate my body to science?") and directly address what you're hearing
  • Listen for overt statements ("I can't take it anymore," "Life isn't worth living," "I wish I were dead," "Everyone would be better off if I died")
  • Acknowledge the underlying feeling when the patient presents as angry, hostile, or overwhelmed
  • Help the patient express feelings safely
  • Do not make promises of confidentiality you cannot keep — the safety team must know
  • Involve family and support systems in safety planning when appropriate
✦
Remember the Red Flag
Sudden shift from persistent sadness to unexplained calm, peace, or brightening of mood (especially after being newly prescribed an antidepressant) — the patient may have made the decision to end their life and now feels relief. Also watch for giving away possessions, writing letters, organizing finances, and acts of farewell.
What medications are used in the treatment of suicidal patients?

Medications don't treat suicidality directly; they target the underlying psychiatric condition that is driving the suicidal ideation.

  • Antidepressants (SSRIs most common) — for underlying depression or anxiety. Monitor closely in first weeks because energy returns before mood lifts
  • Benzodiazepines — short-term for severe anxiety or agitation; use cautiously (disinhibition can increase impulsivity)
  • Mood stabilizers — lithium in particular has been shown to reduce suicide risk in bipolar disorder
  • Antipsychotics — for psychotic features, command hallucinations, or severe agitation
  • Esketamine (Spravato) — used off-label for acute suicidality; produces rapid antidepressant effect
  • ECT — considered when suicidality is acute and medications are too slow; fastest effective option
●
Hopeful Reminder
From the Golden Gate Bridge study: of 515 individuals restrained from jumping between 1937 and 1978, approximately 90% did not later die by violent means. The desire to die is almost always temporary. Intervention works. Prevented suicide often means a long, satisfying life. Therapy, medication, family, friends, and groups all offer hope.
