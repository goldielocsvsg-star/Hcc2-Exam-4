01

Rule Out Medical Causes First

Foundation
Before we decide that someone in the ED or on the floor is experiencing anxiety from psychological causes, what serious conditions do we need to rule out, and what are key treatments to alleviate the associated anxiety?

Anxiety symptoms — racing heart, shortness of breath, chest tightness, sweating, impending doom — overlap dramatically with life-threatening medical emergencies. A determination of anxiety should never be made until other high-risk medical explanations have been ruled out.

Acute Life-Threatening

  • Myocardial infarction (MI) — chest pain, SOB, diaphoresis, impending doom
  • Pulmonary embolism — SOB, tachycardia, chest pain
  • Stroke — sudden neuro changes
  • Hypoglycemia — tremor, diaphoresis, confusion, tachycardia
  • Hypoxia — restlessness + low SpO2
  • Drug or alcohol withdrawal — DTs can be fatal

Chronic Medical Conditions (Box 31.1)

  • Cancer
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
  • Asthma
  • Heart disease
  • Diabetes
  • Thyroid disease (hyper- or hypothyroidism)
  • Pheochromocytoma
  • Chronic infections
  • Vestibular dysfunction
  • Irritable bowel syndrome

Key diagnostic workup: Vital signs, auscultation of heart and lungs, palpation of thyroid, cranial nerve assessment, blood and urine studies — CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP), arterial blood gas (ABG), thyroid function tests, urinalysis. An ECG is especially important to rule out cardiac causes.

Key treatments to alleviate associated anxiety when medical cause is identified:

  • Treat the underlying medical condition — oxygen for hypoxia, glucose for hypoglycemia, beta-blockers/nitrates for cardiac ischemia, thyroid medication, etc.
  • Short-acting benzodiazepine (lorazepam, alprazolam) for severe acute anxiety while workup proceeds
  • Calm, reassuring presence — explain what's being done
  • Minimize environmental stimuli — move to quieter area if possible
  • Slow, deep breathing coached by the nurse
  • Remain with the patient — "I'm here, you are safe"
▲
Clinical Rule
Anxiety is a diagnosis of exclusion. Chest pain + SOB + diaphoresis + impending doom looks exactly like an MI and exactly like a panic attack. Always work up the cardiac cause first. An ECG, troponins, and a good history take minutes and save lives.
02

Panic Attacks

Acute Anxiety

A panic attack is an abrupt surge of intense fear or discomfort with a sense of impending doom and rapid onset. It is the most extreme level of Peplau's anxiety continuum (mild → moderate → severe → panic).

What are the signs and symptoms of panic?

Panic-level anxiety manifests as massive sympathetic nervous system activation combined with a loss of reality contact:

Physical / Autonomic

  • Palpitations, pounding heart, increased HR
  • Sweating (diaphoresis)
  • Shaking, trembling, chills or overheating
  • Nausea, vomiting, GI distress
  • Sensation of choking or smothering
  • Shortness of breath, hyperventilation
  • Chest pain or tightness
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness
  • Paresthesia (numbness, tingling)

Psychological / Cognitive

  • Overwhelming fear
  • Sense of impending doom
  • Derealization — the world feels unreal
  • Depersonalization — feeling detached from self
  • Fear of dying, losing control, or "going crazy"
  • Greatly reduced and distorted perceptual field
  • Unable to process what is happening
  • Hallucinations or delusions possible at panic level
✦
Peplau's Four Levels of Anxiety
Mild — heightened perceptual field, problem-solving enhanced, mild tension-relieving behaviors (nail biting, foot tapping). Constructive.
Moderate — narrowed perceptual field, selective inattention, tension, pounding heart, increased vitals. Learning still possible.
Severe — greatly reduced perceptual field, focus on one detail or scattered, learning not possible, dazed, somatic symptoms (headache, nausea, dizziness), hyperventilation, sense of impending doom.
Panic — unable to process environment, loss of touch with reality, dysregulated behavior (pacing, running, shouting, withdrawal), possible hallucinations, exhaustion.
Practice Question — Panic Priority Intervention ⚡ Priority Setting

For a patient experiencing panic, which nursing intervention should be implemented first?

  • a.Teach relaxation techniques
  • b.Administer a short-acting anxiolytic medication
  • c.Prepare to implement the use of restraints
  • d.Provide calm, brief, directive communication
Show rationale

Why D: At the panic level, the patient's perceptual field is so reduced they cannot process complex information — learning is impossible. The first intervention is always establishing a calm, reassuring presence with clear, simple, directive communication. A calm nurse in a low-stimulation environment directly lowers the patient's anxiety through co-regulation.

Why not the others: Teaching relaxation techniques (A) is impossible when the patient cannot learn — save this for after acute anxiety reduces. Medication (B) may become necessary, but nonpharmacologic methods come first. Restraints (C) are a last resort after all other interventions fail and only if there's risk of harm.

Nursing priority order for panic: Stay with patient → speak calmly with a low-pitched voice → minimize stimuli → use short, simple statements → coach slow breathing → if still severe, assess need for PRN medication.

03

Nursing Care: Panic

Acute + Long Term
What acute and long-term nursing care does a patient with panic disorder need?
Intervention Rationale
Maintain a calm manner Anxiety is communicated interpersonally. The nurse's quiet calm can serve to calm the patient; nurse anxiety can escalate patient anxiety
Always remain with the patient A person left alone with overwhelming anxiety feels abandoned. Your caring face may be their only contact with reality when confusion becomes overwhelming
Minimize environmental stimuli Move to a quieter setting — helps prevent further escalation
Use clear, simple statements and repetition A person experiencing panic has difficulty concentrating and processing information
Use a low-pitched voice; speak slowly A high-pitched voice conveys anxiety. Low pitch decreases anxiety
Reassure safety — "You are safe. I will not leave you." Communicates presence and commitment; reduces fear of abandonment
Coach slow, deep breathing Activates the relaxation response — opposite of stress response. Reduces HR and muscle tension
Reinforce reality if distortions occur Focusing on and validating what is actually in the environment can decrease anxiety
Attend to physical needs High anxiety may obscure awareness of warmth, fluids, elimination, or pain. High-calorie fluids if the patient is pacing — prevents dehydration and exhaustion
Set firm physical limits if needed "You may not hit anyone here. If you can't control yourself, we will help you." A person out of control is often terrorized; limits offer protection
Offer physical outlets Walking with the nurse, punching bag, ping-pong — physical activity channels and dissipates tension
Assess need for PRN medication After other interventions have been tried — only when exhaustion or harm must be prevented

Long-term nursing care:

  • Teach the patient to recognize symptoms of rising anxiety before full panic develops
  • Teach and practice preventive therapies — deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, mindfulness, imagery
  • Refer for cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) — highly effective for panic disorder
  • Refer for behavioral therapies — systematic desensitization (graduated exposure), modeling, thought stopping, flooding
  • Coordinate with psychiatric provider for maintenance medications (SSRIs as first-line)
  • Identify community support groups
  • Discuss lifestyle changes — limit caffeine, alcohol, get adequate sleep

Anxiety Medications

Pharmacology
What medications treat anxiety, and what are the key differences between them?
Class / Use Examples Key Points
SSRIs
Long-term / Maintenance		 Fluoxetine (Prozac), Sertraline (Zoloft), Paroxetine (Paxil), Escitalopram (Lexapro) First-line First line of defense for most anxiety disorders. Takes several weeks for full effect. Fluoxetine and sertraline are more activating (may worsen anxiety initially); paroxetine tends to be more calming
SNRIs
Long-term		 Venlafaxine (Effexor), Duloxetine (Cymbalta) Also first-line. Duloxetine is especially effective for generalized anxiety disorder and FDA-approved for children 7–17 with GAD
Benzodiazepines
Acute / Short-term		 Alprazolam (Xanax), Lorazepam (Ativan), Diazepam (Valium), Clonazepam (Klonopin), Oxazepam (Serax), Chlordiazepoxide (Librium) Dependence risk Quick onset. Used during acute panic when nonpharmacologic methods fail. Short-term only due to dependence. Monitor for sedation, ataxia, decreased cognitive function. Paradoxical reactions can occur
Buspirone (BuSpar)
Atypical anxiolytic		 Buspirone No dependence Partial serotonin-1A agonist. Takes 2–4 weeks to reach full effect. Safe for long-term use. Side effects: dizziness, nausea, headache, nervousness. Avoid in hepatic/renal impairment
Beta-blockers Propranolol (Inderal), atenolol Block the physical effects of anxiety (racing heart, tremor, blushing). Used for social anxiety disorder and performance anxiety. Also used to treat akathisia from antipsychotics
Antihistamines Hydroxyzine (Vistaril), diphenhydramine (Benadryl) Safe, non-addictive. Good choice for patients with substance use history. Sedating
Anticonvulsants Gabapentin (Neurontin), pregabalin (Lyrica) Some benefit in GAD and social anxiety disorder
Antipsychotics Various Used adjunctively for severe anxiety symptoms
What are precautions with benzodiazepines?
  • Dependence risk — short-term use only (usually a few weeks max). Withdrawal can cause insomnia, irritability, nervousness, dry mouth, tremors, convulsions, and confusion
  • Avoid in substance use disorder — high abuse potential
  • Avoid in older adults — increased risk of delirium, falls, and fractures
  • Pregnancy considerations — benzo use shortly before delivery can cause floppy infant syndrome (dystonia, muscle weakness); prenatal exposure slightly increases oral cleft lip/palate risk. FDA warns against breastfeeding
  • Avoid alcohol — synergistic CNS depression, potentially fatal
  • Paradoxical reactions — anxiety, agitation, talkativeness, loss of impulse control (opposite of intended effect)
  • Monitor for sedation, ataxia, decreased cognitive function
  • Caution with driving and machinery
  • Drug interactions — antacids delay absorption; cimetidine increases sedation; CNS depressants (alcohol, barbiturates) increase sedation dangerously
  • Taper gradually — never stop abruptly after regular use
What is the difference between "first line" and "first generation"?

"First Line"

Describes the preferred first choice for treating a condition based on evidence of safety, efficacy, tolerability, and side-effect profile. It's a treatment hierarchy term.

Example: SSRIs are first-line treatment for anxiety disorders and depression because they work well with fewer serious side effects than older options.

"First Generation"

Refers to the order in which drug classes were developed historically. It's a chronological term.

Example: First-generation antipsychotics (Haldol, Thorazine) were developed first, in the 1950s. Second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics came later with improved side effects. Tricyclics are "first generation" antidepressants; SSRIs came later.

◆
The Key Insight
These terms often disagree. A first-generation drug (developed earlier) is usually not first-line anymore because newer alternatives have better side-effect profiles. TCAs are first-generation antidepressants but are second-line because SSRIs are safer. Haldol is first-generation but no longer always first-line for schizophrenia.
Practice Question — PRN for Severe Anxiety ⚡ Pharmacology Priority

A patient experiences a sudden episode of severe anxiety. Of these medications in the patient's medical record, which is most appropriate to give as a PRN anxiolytic?

  • a.Buspirone
  • b.Lorazepam
  • c.Amitriptyline
  • d.Fluoxetine
Show rationale

Why B — Lorazepam (Ativan): Benzodiazepines have a quick onset of action, making them the appropriate PRN medication for acute, sudden, severe anxiety. Lorazepam works within minutes.

Why not the others:

  • A. Buspirone — takes 2 to 4 weeks to reach full effect. Not useful acutely. It's for long-term daily use only.
  • C. Amitriptyline — a tricyclic antidepressant. Takes weeks for antidepressant effect. Not indicated PRN for anxiety.
  • D. Fluoxetine — SSRI. Takes weeks for effect. Actually activating and can increase anxiety initially. Never given PRN.
Practice Question — Lorazepam Teaching (SATA) ⚡ Patient Teaching

A nurse plans health teaching for a patient diagnosed with panic disorder who has been given a new prescription for lorazepam (Ativan). What information should be included? Select all that apply.

  • a.Caution with driving and use of machinery
  • b.Foods allowed on a tyramine free diet
  • c.The importance of caffeine restriction
  • d.Avoidance of alcoholic beverages
  • e.Take the medication on an empty stomach
  • f.This medication should be taken at the same time every day
  • g.This medication may take one to four weeks to take effect
Show rationale

Correct: A, C, D

  • A. Driving/machinery caution — benzos cause sedation, ataxia, decreased cognitive function. Do not operate cars or machinery until effects are known ✓
  • C. Caffeine restriction — caffeine counteracts the anxiolytic effect and increases anxiety symptoms ✓
  • D. Avoid alcohol — additive CNS depression, potentially fatal. Absolutely avoid ✓

Incorrect:

  • B. Tyramine-free diet — only applies to MAOIs, not benzodiazepines
  • E. Empty stomach — wrong. Benzos should be taken with or shortly after meals to reduce GI discomfort
  • F. Same time daily — incorrect. Lorazepam is PRN for acute anxiety, not a scheduled drug like buspirone or SSRIs
  • G. 1–4 weeks to take effect — incorrect. That describes buspirone and SSRIs. Benzos work in minutes

PTSD

Trauma-Related

PTSD develops after exposure to a traumatic event involving actual or threatened death or serious injury. It causes persistent re-experiencing, avoidance, increased arousal, and mood alterations. About 60% of men and 50% of women experience at least one trauma in their lifetime; approximately 8% of the population develops PTSD at some point.

What are the signs and symptoms of PTSD?

PTSD is characterized by four major symptom clusters:

Cluster Presentation
Re-experiencing Flashbacks — dissociative experiences where the event is relived with vivid sensory input. Intrusive recurrent recollections. Nightmares. Physical reactions when reminded of the event (stomach terror, heart palpitations, muscle tensing). Illusions — misperceptions of real stimuli
Avoidance Avoiding talking about the event, avoiding activities, people, or places that arouse memories. Accompanied by feelings of detachment, emptiness, and numbing. Social withdrawal
Increased arousal Irritability, difficulty sleeping (sleep disturbances), difficulty concentrating, hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response
Alterations in mood Chronic depression, negative appraisals, lack of interest in previously pleasurable activities, guilt (survivor guilt, feeling responsible for the event), hating oneself for feeling "needy"
Behavioral Destructive behaviors, aggression, substance use (self-medication), suicidal thoughts or plans
Interpersonal Trust issues, difficulty in social/occupational/romantic relationships, feeling permanently damaged

PTSD-inducing events include: military combat, prisoner-of-war experience, crime (assault, rape, mugging, bombing), natural disasters (floods, tornadoes, earthquakes), human-caused disasters (car, plane, train accidents), and an often-overlooked cause — being diagnosed with a life-threatening illness or treated in an ICU.

Onset: Symptoms can begin within a month or be delayed months to years.

What nursing care does the PTSD patient need?

Acute phase — address all domains:

  • Emotional: Provide a hopeful, accepting attitude. Build trust through consistency. Implement suicide precautions if indicated (many PTSD patients are suicidal)
  • Psychological: Encourage sharing of feelings at the patient's pace. Validate that reactions are a normal response to an abnormal event — not personal weakness or failure
  • Physiological: Address sleep disturbances (nightmares), nutrition, pain, any somatic symptoms
  • Safety: Maintain safety from self-harm; monitor substance use

Long-term:

  • Help establish meaningful goals and develop a narrative of the event
  • Refer for cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) — first-line psychological treatment
  • Refer for EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) — evidence-based, helps process traumatic memories
  • Refer for prolonged exposure therapy (PE) and cognitive processing therapy (CPT)
  • Collaborate with psychiatric providers
  • Connect with support groups (especially veteran groups)
  • Teach relaxation: slow deep breathing, imagery, mindfulness
  • Improve socialization — reduces isolation

Therapeutic communication:

  • Encourage the client to share feelings at their own pace
  • Validate feelings — reassure that their reactions are normal responses to abnormal events
  • Help the patient focus on the present during flashbacks or dissociative episodes — grounding techniques
  • Reassure safety — "You are safe now. This is [date]. You are in [place]"
  • Listen without pushing for details before the patient is ready

Medications for PTSD:

  • SSRIs — FDA-approved: sertraline (Zoloft) and paroxetine (Paxil). First-line
  • Other antidepressants off-label — fluoxetine, venlafaxine (SNRI)
  • Prazosin†1 — often used for nightmares
  • Second-line: nefazodone, imipramine, phenelzine
  • Not recommended: atypical antipsychotics, citalopram, amitriptyline, topiramate, lamotrigine

Defense Mechanisms

Ego Protection
Clients with anxiety disorders often use "defense mechanisms" — why are they used, and what are some common ones?

Why defense mechanisms are used: Defense mechanisms are automatic coping styles that protect people from anxiety and enable them to maintain their self-image by blocking feelings, conflicts, and memories that are too painful to handle consciously. They operate unconsciously and are not always apparent to the person using them.

◆
Adaptive vs Maladaptive
Most defense mechanisms can be used in both healthy and unhealthy ways. Whether the use is adaptive (helps lower anxiety and achieve goals in acceptable ways) or maladaptive (used to excess, interferes with function) depends on their frequency, intensity, and duration of use. Sublimation is always constructive; conversion and splitting are almost always pathological.
Mechanism Definition Example
Altruism Unconscious motivation to feel caring and concern for others Adaptive: Anonymous donation to a colleague's fundraiser · Maladaptive: Compulsively rescuing cats to the point of hoarding
Compensation Counterbalancing perceived deficiencies by emphasizing strengths Adaptive: Short man excels in business through assertiveness · Maladaptive: Drinking when self-esteem is low
Conversion Unconscious transformation of anxiety into a physical symptom with no organic cause Almost always pathological. Example: going blind after seeing a spouse enter a hotel with another person
Denial Escaping unpleasant thoughts, feelings, or needs by ignoring their existence Adaptive: Initial reaction "No, I don't believe you" to news of a death · Maladaptive: Keeping a deceased spouse's clothes and speaking of them in present tense years later
Displacement Transferring emotions from one person/object/situation to a nonthreatening one Adaptive: Child yells at teddy bear after being bullied · Maladaptive: Being unable to acknowledge fear of father, child becomes fearful of animals
Dissociation Disruption in consciousness, memory, identity, or perception — compartmentalizing uncomfortable aspects of self Adaptive: Art student tunes out noise to focus on work · Maladaptive: Abuse survivor perpetually disconnected from reality
Identification Attributing to oneself the characteristics of another person or group Adaptive: Child pretends to be her teacher · Maladaptive: Boy dresses like a neighborhood drug dealer
Intellectualization Analyzing events based on cold facts without feeling or emotion Adaptive: After losing farm to tornado, analyzes options and leads child to safety · Maladaptive: Focuses on day-care logistics instead of grieving spouse's death with children
Projection Unconsciously rejecting unacceptable features and attributing them to others Always an immature/pathological defense. Example: A person who has repressed same-sex attraction refuses to socialize, fearing others will come on to her
Rationalization Justifying illogical ideas or actions with acceptable explanations Adaptive: "I didn't get the raise because the boss doesn't like me" · Maladaptive: Father justifies harsh treatment of a child by saying "He is lazy and doesn't listen"
Reaction formation Unacceptable feelings kept out of awareness by expressing the opposite Adaptive: Recovering alcoholic constantly speaks of the evils of drinking · Maladaptive: Mother with unconscious hostility toward daughter becomes overprotective/hovering
Regression Reverting to an earlier, more primitive pattern of behavior Adaptive: 4-year-old with new baby brother temporarily thumb-sucks · Maladaptive: Man loses promotion, starts complaining, missing appointments, late to meetings
Repression Unconscious exclusion of unpleasant experiences from awareness Adaptive: Man forgets spouse's birthday after a fight · Maladaptive: Woman unable to enjoy sex due to repressed childhood trauma
Splitting Inability to integrate positive and negative qualities of self or others into a cohesive image Almost always pathological. Example: Person initially idealizes acquaintances but becomes disillusioned when they have flaws ("all good" → "all bad")
Sublimation Unconscious transformation of negative impulses into productive ones Always constructive. Example: Woman angry at boss channels feelings into deep-cleaning her house
Suppression Conscious decision to delay addressing a disturbing situation Businessman told by wife she wants a divorce the morning of an important speech; he sets it aside until after the speech when he can give it full attention
Undoing Making up for a regrettable act or communication Adaptive: After flirting with a coworker, a wife brings her husband concert tickets · Maladaptive: Man with rigid morals compulsively washes his hands around attractive women
✦
Suppression vs Repression — Commonly Confused
Suppression is conscious — "I will deal with this later." Repression is unconscious — the mind blocks it entirely without awareness. Classic NCLEX distinction.

Schizophrenia & Psychosis

Brain Disorder

Schizophrenia is a brain disorder with psychotic thinking and an inability to distinguish what is real from what is not. It affects roughly 1% of the population. The core symptom clusters are positive, negative, and cognitive.

What are the biggest concerns for the client's health and safety?

Safety Concerns

  • Suicide — approximately 10% die by suicide at 10 years after diagnosis, 15% by 30 years. #1 cause of early death in this population
  • Violence toward others — especially with paranoid delusions or command hallucinations
  • Command hallucinations directing self-harm or harm to others
  • Impaired judgment from psychosis — poor decisions about safety, finances, relationships
  • Substance use disorders — common comorbidity; complicates picture
  • Homelessness, incarceration, victimization

Health Concerns

  • Medication side effects — EPS, tardive dyskinesia, metabolic syndrome, agranulocytosis
  • Metabolic syndrome — weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia, hypertension
  • Cardiovascular disease and premature death
  • Reduced ability to recognize or respond to signs of illness — delayed medical care
  • Anosognosia — lack of insight into their own illness; interferes with treatment adherence
  • Poor self-care — hygiene, nutrition, sleep
  • Smoking — 70% of patients with schizophrenia smoke (vs. 22% general population), dramatically increasing cancer/CV risk
  • Impaired swallowing from antipsychotics → drooling and choking risk
What are the positive, negative, and cognitive symptoms of schizophrenia?
Category Findings
Positive Symptoms
"Added" manifestations — things that shouldn't be there		 Hallucinations — sensory experiences with no external stimulus (auditory most common; also visual, olfactory, gustatory, tactile)
Delusions — fixed false beliefs not changed by evidence (paranoid, grandiose, somatic, referential, thought insertion/withdrawal/deletion)
Bizarre behavior — disorganized or abnormal motor behavior
Alteration in speech — derailment, loose associations, tangentiality, word salad, neologisms, echolalia
Paranoia, magical thinking
Negative Symptoms
Absence of things that should be there		 Affect — flat or blunted
Alogia — poverty of speech; minimal verbal output
Anergia — lack of energy
Anhedonia — inability to experience pleasure
Avolition — lack of motivation, inability to initiate or sustain goal-directed activity
Asociality — social withdrawal
Cognitive Symptoms Disordered thinking
Inability to make decisions
Impaired memory, concentration, attention
Executive dysfunction
Respond less well to antipsychotics than positive symptoms do — and can be more debilitating long-term
✦
The Five A's of Negative Symptoms
Affect (flat) · Alogia · Anergia · Anhedonia · Avolition (plus Asociality as a sixth)†2

Hallucinations & Command Safety

Priority Safety
Practice Question — Hallucination Danger ⚡ Safety Priority

Which hallucination necessitates the nurse to implement safety measures to protect others? The patient says:

  • a."I hear angels playing harps."
  • b."The voices say everyone is trying to kill me."
  • c."My dead father tells me I am a bad person."
  • d."The voices talk only at night when I'm trying to sleep."
Show rationale

Why B: This statement indicates paranoia — the belief that others intend to kill the patient. Paranoia often leads to fearfulness, and the patient may attempt to harm others to protect themselves. From the textbook: "Paranoia: An irrational fear, ranging from mild to profound. Fear may result in dangerous defensive actions, such as harming another person before that person can harm the patient."

Why not the others: The other statements involve hallucinations but do not indicate a specific risk of harm to others. They may still require assessment and intervention for the patient's wellbeing (especially C — auditory hallucination critical of self could increase self-harm risk), but they don't specifically require protecting others.

Practice Question — Command Hallucination Priority ⚡ Assessment Priority

A patient diagnosed with schizophrenia and auditory hallucinations anxiously tells the nurse, "The voice is telling me to do things." Select the nurse's priority assessment question.

  • a."How long has the voice been telling you to do things?"
  • b."Are you afraid of the voice?"
  • c."Do you recognize the voice speaking to you?"
  • d."What is the voice telling you to do?"
Show rationale

Why D: Learning what a command hallucination is telling the patient to do is the priority because commands often place the patient or others at risk for harm. Command hallucinations are a psychiatric emergency when they direct violence. The content of the command drives every subsequent intervention — safety precautions, medication, staffing.

Why not the others: Duration (A), fear (B), and recognition of voice (C) are useful assessments but of lesser importance than identifying the command itself.

What do we need to know and do if the client is having command hallucinations?

Command hallucinations are hallucinations that direct the patient to take an action. They may be dangerous and must be evaluated carefully. They are often frightening and may signal a psychiatric emergency.

Key assessments — what we need to know:

  • What is the voice telling you to do? — this is the priority question
  • Are the commands directed at harming self or harming others?
  • Does the patient recognize the hallucination as "not real"?
  • Can the patient resist the commands?
  • Has the patient ever acted on commands before?
  • How is the patient responding right now?
  • What does the patient do to cope when the commands come?

Interventions — what we need to do:

  • Explore the content of hallucinations — identify command hallucinations directly
  • Implement safety precautions — suicide and/or violence precautions as indicated by the content
  • Help the patient realize that commands can safely be ignored — teach that they are a symptom of the illness, not real instructions
  • Give the patient options for coping — distraction techniques, telling the voice "no," notifying staff when commands occur
  • Administer antipsychotic medications as ordered — reduce positive symptoms including hallucinations
  • Monitor closely — increase observation level (Q15-minute or 1:1)
  • Do not argue with the reality of the hallucination — but do not validate it either
  • Acknowledge the patient's experience — "I understand you're hearing voices. I don't hear them, but I believe you hear them and that they are frightening"
  • Document thoroughly — content, patient response, interventions
  • Communicate with the treatment team immediately if commands are violent
▲
Command Hallucination Red Flag
A command hallucination telling the patient to harm self or others is a psychiatric emergency. Treat it with the same urgency as a patient reporting a suicide plan. Increase observation to 1:1, notify the provider, implement safety precautions immediately.

Therapeutic Communication with Psychosis

Delusions & Paranoia
Practice Question — Responding to Delusions ⚡ Therapeutic Communication

A person has had difficulty benefiting from treatment because of arguing with medical staff and accusing them of conspiracy. Today the person shouts, "They're all plotting to destroy me. Isn't that true?" Select the nurse's most therapeutic response.

  • a."Everyone here is trying to help you. No one wants to harm you."
  • b."Feeling that people want to destroy you must be very frightening."
  • c."That is not true. People here are trying to help you if you will let them."
  • d."Staff members are health care professionals who are qualified to help you."
Show rationale

Why B: This response focuses on the feelings the patient is expressing rather than the delusional content. This strategy prevents arguing about the reality of delusional beliefs. Arguments increase patient anxiety and the tenacity with which the patient holds to the delusion.

Why not the others: Options A, C, and D all engage with the irrational thought content and invite argument. Telling a patient experiencing delusional paranoia "that's not true" or "everyone is trying to help you" fundamentally misses that the patient's reality, at this moment, is that people are plotting against them. You cannot reason someone out of a delusion — you can only address the emotion behind it.

Golden rule for delusions: Acknowledge the feeling, not the content.

Practice Question — Paranoid Delusion Response ⚡ Therapeutic Communication

A client says, "Facebook has a new tracking capacity. If I use the Internet, Homeland Security will detain me as a terrorist." Select the nurse's appropriate initial action:

  • a.Reassure the client, "Facebook is not a dangerous website, and Homeland Security is not after you."
  • b.Tell the client, "You are in a safe place where you will be helped."
  • c.Administer an as needed dose of an antipsychotic medication.
  • d.Gently say, "You don't need to worry about any of that."
Show rationale

Why B: The client is experiencing paranoia and delusional thinking, which leads to fear. Explaining that the client is in a safe place helps relieve the fear. It focuses on what's real and addresses the underlying emotion (fear) without arguing about the delusion.

Why not the others:

  • A — disagreeing with the delusion will only strengthen it and create mistrust
  • C — medication won't relieve the immediate concern. Therapeutic communication comes first; PRN medication is not the initial action
  • D — dismissive, minimizing; gives advice rather than addresses feelings
What are the guiding principles of therapeutic communication with psychosis?
  • Address feelings, not content — "That must be frightening" rather than arguing with the delusion
  • Do not argue with delusions or hallucinations — it reinforces them and damages trust
  • Do not agree with or validate delusions either — don't play along
  • Use simple, concrete language — avoid abstract terms, idioms, or complex sentences
  • Reassure safety — "You are in a safe place. We are here to help you"
  • Provide a calm, predictable milieu — low stimulation, consistent routines
  • Be honest and consistent — builds trust, especially with paranoid patients
  • Reality orient gently — "I don't see anyone in the room, but I know you do"
  • Avoid whispering or laughing within the patient's sight — paranoid patients may interpret as plotting
  • Use patient-friendly language when asking about symptoms — instead of "Are you hallucinating?" ask "Do you ever see or hear things that other people can't?"
  • Maintain appropriate personal space — paranoid patients may feel threatened by closeness

First-Generation Antipsychotics

Typical / Neuroleptics
What are first-generation antipsychotics and what are their risks?

First-generation antipsychotics (also called typical antipsychotics or neuroleptics) are dopamine (D2) receptor antagonists. They were the first class developed (1950s) and work primarily by reducing positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) but have little effect on negative symptoms.

Common examples: Haloperidol (Haldol), Chlorpromazine (Thorazine), Fluphenazine, Thioridazine (Mellaril), Perphenazine, Loxapine.

Major side-effect categories:

  • Extrapyramidal side effects (EPS) — from D2 blockade in motor areas (see next section)
  • Anticholinergic effects — dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, tachycardia
  • Sedation — especially with low-potency agents
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Lowered seizure threshold
  • Hyperprolactinemia → sexual dysfunction, galactorrhea, amenorrhea, gynecomastia
  • Weight gain
  • Photosensitivity, cataracts (especially chlorpromazine, thioridazine)
  • Prolonged QT interval → risk of fatal arrhythmias
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) — rare but life-threatening
◆
Timing Note
Antipsychotic drugs take 2 to 6 weeks to become fully effective. Patient-specific dose adjustment is required to balance effectiveness and side effects.
11

Extrapyramidal Side Effects (EPS)

Movement Disorders
What are the four types of EPS and how are they treated?

EPS results from D2 receptor blockade in motor areas (nigrostriatal pathway). First-generation antipsychotics have much higher EPS risk than second-generation. The four categories below follow the typical timeline of onset.

EPS Type Onset Timeline Presentation & Treatment
Acute Dystonia Hours to days Acute What it looks like: Sudden, sustained muscle contractions usually of head and neck. Painful and frightening.
• Torticollis — twisted neck, head pulled to one side†3
• Oculogyric crisis — eyes roll back
• Retrocollis — backward arching of head/neck/spine
• Laryngeal dystonia — can threaten airway (rare but emergent)
Treatment: Benztropine (Cogentin) or diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 25–50 mg IM/IV. Relief usually in 5–15 min. Monitor airway. Stay with patient. Consider oral prophylaxis afterward.
Akathisia Days to weeks†4 What it looks like: Motor restlessness — pacing, inability to stand still, rocking while seated, shifting from foot to foot. Not anxiety — it's a physical drive to move. Patient often describes it as unbearable; can contribute to suicidality in severe cases.
Treatment: Benztropine, or propranolol (Inderal), lorazepam (Ativan), or diazepam (Valium). Consult provider about dose reduction or medication change. Takes care to distinguish from anxiety.
Pseudoparkinsonism Weeks to months Chronic onset What it is: A drug-induced, temporary group of symptoms that mimic Parkinson disease. Acute onset relative to true Parkinson's but develops over weeks. Resolves when drug is stopped or reduced.
Symptoms: Tremor, reduced accessory movements (arm swing), gait impairment (shuffling), mask facies, bradykinesia, drooling, pill-rolling finger movements, dysphagia.
Treatment: Antiparkinsonian agents — benztropine (Cogentin) or trihexyphenidyl (Artane). Consult about dose reduction. Wipe excess saliva. Teach fall prevention.
Tardive Dyskinesia (TD) Months to years Chronic — usually persistent even after medication is stopped What it is: Persistent involuntary rhythmic movements. Develops in ~20–25% of patients on antipsychotics (especially first-generation). More common in women. Often permanent.
Symptoms:
• Face: protruding/writhing tongue, lip smacking, blowing, facial distortion
• Limbs: chorea (rapid, irregular), athetoid (slow, serpentine)
• Trunk: neck/shoulder movements, hip jerks, twisting pelvic thrusts
Screening: AIMS (Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale) every 3 months is a key nursing role
Treatment: Valbenazine (Ingrezza) or deutetrabenazine (Austedo). Switching to a second-generation antipsychotic. Discontinuing the drug rarely relieves symptoms.
◆
How Benztropine and Diphenhydramine Work
Neuroleptics block dopamine receptors, creating an imbalance between dopamine and acetylcholine in the basal ganglia (where they must be balanced for normal movement). Benztropine and diphenhydramine are anticholinergics — they block acetylcholine, which restores the dopamine/ACh balance. Benztropine is more commonly used because diphenhydramine causes more sedation.

NMS vs Serotonin Syndrome

Life-Threatening
What is neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS)?

NMS is a life-threatening medical emergency caused by excessive dopamine receptor blockade from antipsychotic medications. Fatal in about 6% of cases. Usually occurs early in therapy but can occur years into treatment.

NMS Signs & Symptoms

  • Severe muscle rigidity ("lead pipe")
  • Hyperthermia — very high fever (often > 103°F)
  • Autonomic dysfunction — labile BP (hypertension), tachycardia, tachypnea, diaphoresis, incontinence
  • Altered mental status — decreased LOC, delirium, stupor, coma
  • Elevated creatine kinase (CK)
  • Leukocytosis

NMS Treatment

  • Stop the antipsychotic immediately
  • Contact prescriber stat; transfer to ICU
  • Frequent vital signs
  • Cooling measures — cooling blankets, ice packs, cool water baths
  • IV fluids for hydration and electrolyte correction
  • Dantrolene (Dantrium) — decreases muscle rigidity and reduces fever from muscle contractions
  • Bromocriptine (Parlodel) — dopamine agonist; relieves rigidity
  • Treat dysrhythmias
  • Small heparin doses to reduce PE risk
How do you tell the difference between neuroleptic malignant syndrome and serotonin syndrome?

These two syndromes share fever, altered mental status, and autonomic instability — but the cause, timeline, and motor findings differ. The distinction is clinically critical because the offending drug is different.

Feature Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome Serotonin Syndrome
Cause Dopamine blockade from antipsychotics (Haldol, risperidone, olanzapine, etc.) Excess serotonin from SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, triptans, tramadol, St. John's wort — especially in combination
Onset Slow — over days to weeks (though can occur years into therapy) Rapid — within hours of starting or increasing the drug
Muscle tone "Lead-pipe" rigidity — severe, diffuse, constant Myoclonus (muscle jerks), hyperreflexia, tremor, clonus — especially in lower extremities
Reflexes Normal or decreased (bradyreflexia) Hyperreflexia and clonus
Pupils Normal Dilated (mydriasis)
Bowel sounds Normal or decreased Hyperactive — diarrhea often present
Fever Very high, prolonged May be high but often less extreme
Resolution Days to weeks after stopping drug Usually resolves within 24 hours of stopping drug
Treatment Stop antipsychotic · Dantrolene · Bromocriptine · Cooling · ICU Stop serotonergic agent · Cyproheptadine (serotonin antagonist) · Benzodiazepines for agitation · Cooling · Supportive care
✦
Quick Distinguishing Feature
NMS = rigid and slow (lead-pipe rigidity, days to develop, dopamine-blocker cause).
Serotonin Syndrome = jerky and fast (myoclonus/clonus/hyperreflexia, hours to develop, serotonergic cause, dilated pupils, GI hyperactivity).
How do you differentiate between acute dystonia and NMS?
Feature Acute Dystonia NMS
Muscle involvement Localized — head, neck, tongue, eyes (torticollis, oculogyric crisis). One muscle group or a few Generalized — diffuse "lead-pipe" rigidity of the whole body
Fever None Very high fever (often > 103°F)
Mental status Alert, oriented, frightened but cognitively intact Altered LOC — confusion, delirium, stupor
Vital signs Usually stable (maybe mildly elevated from anxiety) Autonomic instability — labile BP, tachycardia, tachypnea, diaphoresis
Severity Frightening and uncomfortable but not life-threatening (unless laryngeal involvement) Medical emergency — 6% mortality
How the patient feels Anxious, scared, painful muscle spasms in specific areas. Aware of what's happening Confused, delirious, rigid all over. May not be able to communicate
Treatment Benztropine or diphenhydramine IM/IV — relief in 5–15 min Stop antipsychotic, ICU, dantrolene, bromocriptine, cooling, IVF

Medications used to treat acute dystonia:

  • Benztropine (Cogentin) — anticholinergic, restores dopamine/acetylcholine balance
  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 25–50 mg IM/IV — also anticholinergic, can be given IM for faster response
  • Trihexyphenidyl (Artane) — another antiparkinsonian agent

Second & Third-Generation Antipsychotics

Atypical
What are second-generation antipsychotics and how do they differ from first-generation?

Second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics are both serotonin (5-HT2A) and dopamine (D2) antagonists. They're often chosen as first-line because they're equally effective for positive symptoms and may help negative symptoms. They have a markedly lower risk of EPS than first-generation.

Common examples: Clozapine (Clozaril), Risperidone (Risperdal), Olanzapine (Zyprexa), Quetiapine (Seroquel), Ziprasidone (Geodon), Aripiprazole (Abilify), Lurasidone (Latuda), Paliperidone (Invega).

◆
The Tradeoff
Second-generation antipsychotics have much lower EPS risk BUT much higher metabolic risk. First-generation = movement problems. Second-generation = metabolic problems. Pick your poison.
What are the major side effects of second-generation antipsychotics and how do we teach patients about them?
Side Effect What It Is Patient Teaching
Anticholinergic effects "Can't see, can't pee, can't spit, can't poop" — blurred vision, dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, tachycardia, dry eyes, cognitive impairment Drink fluids and chew sugar-free gum for dry mouth · Use stool softeners and fiber for constipation · Report urinary retention · Use artificial tears for dry eyes · Rise slowly · Watch for toxicity — hot dry skin, hyperthermia, delirium, agitation = emergency
Orthostatic hypotension Drop in BP upon standing up — systolic pressure drops, diastolic drops, pulse rises. Leads to dizziness, falls, syncope. Usually subsides in 1–2 weeks Change positions slowly — move from lying to sitting, pause, then stand · Hold onto railings/furniture while arising · If dizzy, sit back down · Ensure adequate hydration · Avoid hot showers and alcohol (vasodilation worsens it) · Hold dose and call provider if systolic BP < 80 standing
Metabolic syndrome Cluster: weight gain + dyslipidemia + insulin resistance + hypertension. Increases risk of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, early mortality. Especially high with olanzapine, clozapine, quetiapine Monitor weight, BP, fasting glucose, A1C, lipid panel at baseline and regularly · Healthy eating — low-calorie snacks, portion control · Regular physical activity — walking, cycling · Avoid sugary drinks · Report new thirst, frequent urination, vision changes (diabetes signs) · Metformin may be prescribed off-label to reduce diabetes risk
Agranulocytosis / Severe Neutropenia Dangerously low white blood cell count — specifically absolute neutrophil count (ANC) < 500/μL. Risk of life-threatening infection (septicemia). Most common with clozapine but possible with others Clozapine REMS program — mandatory ANC monitoring: weekly × 6 months, then every 2 weeks × 6 months, then monthly · Report immediately: fever, chills, sore throat, flu-like symptoms, any signs of infection · Don't share bathrooms if possible, avoid crowds and sick people during first months · Good hand hygiene
Tardive dyskinesia (lower risk but still possible) Involuntary movements — mainly face, tongue, limbs. Lower risk than with first-generation but still possible, especially with long-term use Report any new involuntary movements · AIMS screening every 3 months
Sedation Common, especially early in treatment. More with clozapine, olanzapine, quetiapine Take at bedtime if possible · No driving until effects known · Alcohol and CNS depressants worsen this
QT prolongation Delay in ventricular repolarization → fatal arrhythmia risk. Especially with quetiapine, risperidone, ziprasidone ECG at baseline and during treatment · Report palpitations, fainting, dizziness
Practice Question — Risperidone Teaching Priority ⚡ Teaching Priority

A patient diagnosed with schizophrenia begins a new prescription for risperidone. The patient is 5'6" and currently weighs 204 lbs. Which topic is most important for the nurse to include in the teaching plan related to this medication?

  • a.How to recognize tardive dyskinesia
  • b.Weight management strategies
  • c.Ways to manage constipation
  • d.Sleep hygiene measures
Show rationale

Why B: Risperidone is a second-generation antipsychotic. The most common AND most serious potential side effect in this class is metabolic syndrome — weight gain leading to diabetes, dyslipidemia, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease. This patient's BMI is already ~33 (obese) at 5'6" and 204 lbs, so she's starting from a high-risk baseline. Weight management becomes the most important teaching priority because it directly addresses the most dangerous side effect of her medication combined with her existing risk factors.

Why not the others:

  • A — Tardive dyskinesia: TD risk is much lower with second-generation antipsychotics than first-generation. Still screen with AIMS, but not the priority teaching here.
  • C — Constipation: A real anticholinergic side effect, but far less dangerous than metabolic syndrome.
  • D — Sleep hygiene: Not the most important given this patient's cardiovascular risk profile.

Schizophrenia Relapse

Prevention
What do you know about schizophrenia and relapse? What are some causes?

Schizophrenia follows a pattern of recurrent exacerbations (relapses) separated by periods of reduced or dormant symptoms. Each relapse may increase residual dysfunction and disability — prevention is essential.

Common causes of relapse:

  • Medication nonadherence — the leading cause. May be due to side effects, anosognosia (no insight into illness), cost, complexity, stigma, or the belief they're "cured"
  • Substance use — alcohol, cannabis, stimulants can trigger or worsen psychosis
  • Stress — major life events, relationship conflict, loss, trauma
  • Sleep disturbance — especially insomnia
  • Social withdrawal / isolation
  • Comorbid depression — a warning sign
  • High expressed emotion in the family environment — criticism, overinvolvement
  • Poor engagement with outpatient services
  • Medication changes — new drug, dose reduction, switching

Warning signs of impending relapse (family should know these):

  • Reduced sleep or changes in sleep pattern
  • Social withdrawal — skipping therapy, avoiding friends/family
  • Worsening concentration
  • Return of positive symptoms — suspiciousness, hearing voices, unusual beliefs
  • Increased anxiety or depression
  • Poor hygiene, self-care decline
  • Missed medication doses
  • Increased substance use
  • Resumption of odd behaviors or speech patterns
What is relevant client and family education for schizophrenia?

Core teaching for patient and family:

  • Schizophrenia is a chronic brain disorder — not a character flaw, laziness, or bad parenting
  • Medication adherence is essential — stopping meds is the leading cause of relapse. Consider long-acting injectable (LAI) formulations given every 2–4 weeks or even 12 weeks
  • Medications take 2–6 weeks to work fully — don't stop early
  • Recognize warning signs of relapse — sleep changes, withdrawal, return of suspiciousness, missed doses
  • What to do if relapse suspected — contact the treatment team immediately, do not wait. Call the provider, outpatient clinic, or crisis line. If safety is at risk, go to ED
  • Avoid alcohol and illicit substances — can trigger psychosis and interact with meds
  • Regular sleep schedule — sleep disruption precedes relapse
  • Manage stress — ongoing therapy, coping skills, relaxation
  • Negative symptoms may look like laziness or stubbornness but are part of the illness — apathy and poor hygiene are symptoms, not attitude
  • Recognize and respond to side effects — don't just stop meds, call the provider to switch
  • Stay engaged with outpatient care — case management, CBT, family therapy
  • Join support groups — NAMI (National Alliance on Mental Illness) offers Family-to-Family program
  • Metabolic monitoring — regular weight, BP, glucose, lipids
  • Smoking cessation — affects drug levels and overall health
  • Hope and recovery are real — with treatment, many live meaningful lives
●
Resources for Families
NAMI (National Alliance on Mental Illness — nami.org) is the largest consumer and family organization for those living with mental illness. Their Family-to-Family program is specifically designed to educate family members about schizophrenia and how to support their loved one while caring for themselves. Social isolation and stigma hurt families too — connection helps.
◆
A Family Member's Realization (from textbook)
"I used to think my daughter was just being lazy… I didn't know it was her illness. I thought she could make the symptoms stop if she tried hard enough." Teaching families that negative symptoms are part of the disease — not a behavior choice — transforms how they support their loved one.
